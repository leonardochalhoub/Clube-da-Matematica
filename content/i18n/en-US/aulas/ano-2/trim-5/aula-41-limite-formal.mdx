<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Rigorous definition">

<Definicao titulo="ε-δ Limit (Cauchy 1821, formalized by Weierstrass 1872)">
$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ means: for every $\eps > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ (depending on $\eps$) such that if $0 < |x - a| < \delta$, then $|f(x) - L| < \eps$.

Note: $a$ does not need to be in the domain of $f$; what matters is the behavior near $a$.
</Definicao>

### How to prove a limit via ε-δ

1. Write $|f(x) - L|$ in terms of $|x - a|$.
2. For arbitrary $\eps > 0$, find $\delta = \delta(\eps)$ that makes $|f(x) - L| < \eps$ whenever $0 < |x - a| < \delta$.

### Model example: $\lim_{x \to 2} (3x + 1) = 7$

$|3x + 1 - 7| = |3x - 6| = 3|x - 2|$. Given $\eps > 0$, choose $\delta = \eps/3$. Then $|x - 2| < \delta \Rightarrow 3|x - 2| < \eps$. ∎

### One-sided limits

- $\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L$: $0 < x - a < \delta$ (right side only).
- $\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = L$: $0 < a - x < \delta$ (left side only).
- $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \iff \lim_{x \to a^+} = \lim_{x \to a^-} = L$.

### Infinite limits

$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = +\infty$: $\forall M > 0, \exists \delta > 0 : 0 < |x - a| < \delta \Rightarrow f(x) > M$.

$\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L$: $\forall \eps > 0, \exists N : x > N \Rightarrow |f(x) - L| < \eps$.

### Table of the 4 quantifications

| Type | Quantification |
|---|---|
| $\lim_{x \to a} = L$ | $\forall \eps, \exists \delta$ |
| $\lim_{x \to a} = \infty$ | $\forall M, \exists \delta$ |
| $\lim_{x \to \infty} = L$ | $\forall \eps, \exists N$ |
| $\lim_{x \to \infty} = \infty$ | $\forall M, \exists N$ |

### Basic properties

- Limit of a sum = sum of limits.
- Limit of a product = product of limits.
- Limit of a quotient = quotient, if denominator $\neq 0$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

Imagine a ladder approaching a little star up high.

We want the rungs to get **very close** to the star. You say: "I want to be close enough to touch with my finger". I answer: "OK, just take a few more steps".

You say: "now I want to be super, super close". I answer: "then just take **even more** steps".

For **any** small distance you invent, I know how to tell **how many steps** you need to take. That's a limit.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

Imagine you're driving with a GPS, and the destination is point $L$ on the map. The car never quite touches the destination, but gets close.

**Limit** is: for any **bubble** you draw around the destination (no matter how small), the car **enters** the bubble and **stays** inside. It doesn't matter if the bubble has a 1-meter or 1-millimeter radius — just wait long enough.

The Greek letter ε (epsilon) is the **size of the bubble** around the destination. The letter δ (delta) is the **size of the bubble** around the arrival time (on the $x$-axis).

The rule: **you choose ε first** (any small value), and **I guarantee δ** (a distance on the $x$-axis) that makes it work.

If I always manage to give a δ answer, the limit **exists**. If some ε breaks the game, the limit **does not exist**.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

In Lesson 19 you saw the limit **intuitively**: $a_n$ "approaches" $L$ as $n$ grows.

Here we formalize. Cauchy's definition (1821) uses two Greek letters: ε (epsilon) and δ (delta).

The idea: you want to show that $f(x)$ stays **very close** to $L$ when $x$ is close to $a$. How close? **As close as you want**. For every ε ("as close as I want"), there exists δ ("$x$ must be at this distance from $a$") such that if $|x - a| < δ$, then $|f(x) - L| < ε$.

It's a **dialogue**:
- "I want $f(x)$ at distance $0.001$ from $L$" → "OK, $x$ just needs to be at $0.0003$ from $a$"
- "now I want $0.000001$" → "$0.0000003$"
- For any challenge, I can give a δ answer.

### Numerical example: $\lim_{x \to 1} x^2 = 1$

Table of what happens near $x = 1$:

| $x$ | $x^2$ | $|x^2 - 1|$ |
|---|---|---|
| $0.9$ | $0.81$ | $0.19$ |
| $0.99$ | $0.9801$ | $0.0199$ |
| $0.999$ | $0.998001$ | $0.001999$ |
| $1.001$ | $1.002001$ | $0.002001$ |
| $1.01$ | $1.0201$ | $0.0201$ |

To ensure $|x^2 - 1| < \eps$, just $|x - 1| < \eps/3$ (using $|x + 1| \leq 3$ for $x$ near 1). So $\delta = \min(1, \eps/3)$ works.

### Why do we need this?

Without ε-δ we can't answer: does $\lim$ exist? what's its value? how to prove? The rigorous definition **decides** all these questions.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

The $\eps$-$\delta$ definition is the **basis of rigorous calculus**. Before it (18th century), Newton and Leibniz used "infinitesimals" — quantities smaller than any positive but non-zero. It worked in practice but was **logically questionable** (Berkeley in 1734 attacked: "ghosts of departed quantities").

Cauchy formalized in 1821 ([**Cours d'analyse**](https://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/bpt6k90196z)). Weierstrass refined in 1872. **All of modern analysis starts there.**

### Model proof: $\lim_{x \to 3} x^2 = 9$

We want $|x^2 - 9| < \eps$, that is, $|x - 3| \cdot |x + 3| < \eps$. Restricting $|x - 3| < 1$, we have $|x + 3| < 7$. So it suffices that $|x - 3| < \eps/7$ and $|x - 3| < 1$. Take $\delta = \min(1, \eps/7)$. ∎

### Squeeze theorem

If $g(x) \leq f(x) \leq h(x)$ near $a$ and $\lim g = \lim h = L$, then $\lim f = L$.

**Classic application**: $\lim_{x \to 0} x \sin(1/x) = 0$ (squeezed between $\pm |x|$).

### Notable limits (revisited)

- $\lim_{x \to 0} \sin x / x = 1$ (geometry + squeeze).
- $\lim_{x \to 0} (1 - \cos x)/x = 0$.
- $\lim_{x \to 0} (e^x - 1)/x = 1$.
- $\lim_{x \to \infty} (1 + 1/x)^x = e$.

### Indeterminate forms

$0/0$, $\infty/\infty$, $\infty - \infty$, $0 \cdot \infty$, $1^\infty$, $0^0$, $\infty^0$ — require algebraic manipulation or L'Hôpital (Term 7).

### Applications in physics and engineering

- **Instantaneous velocity**: $v(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \Delta s/\Delta t$.
- **Steady-state response**: $V(t) \to V_\infty$ in an RC circuit.
- **Stability**: stable linear system $\Leftrightarrow$ $\lim_{t \to \infty} y(t) = 0$ for zero input.
- **Discretization error**: $\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} - f'(x) = 0$ validates a numerical method.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

The ε-δ definition is an instance of **metric topology**: limit in a metric space $(X, d)$.

### Metric generalization

If $f: (X, d_X) \to (Y, d_Y)$, then $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ means: $\forall \eps > 0, \exists \delta > 0 : 0 < d_X(x, a) < \delta \Rightarrow d_Y(f(x), L) < \eps$. The definition does not depend on $\mathbb{R}$.

### Topological (without metric)

In general topological spaces, limit via **filters** or **neighborhoods**: $f(x) \to L$ as $x \to a$ if for every neighborhood $V$ of $L$, there exists a neighborhood $U$ of $a$ such that $f(U \setminus \{a\}) \subseteq V$.

### Sequential characterization (Heine)

In metric spaces: $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \iff$ for every sequence $x_n \to a$ with $x_n \neq a$, $f(x_n) \to L$. **Crucial** in analysis: trades function limits for sequence limits (easier to manipulate).

### Uniform vs. pointwise convergence

$f_n \to f$ pointwise: $\forall x, \forall \eps, \exists N(\eps, x)$. $f_n \to f$ uniform: $\forall \eps, \exists N(\eps), \forall x$. **The order of quantifiers changes everything** — uniform preserves continuity, pointwise does not.

### Applications in functional analysis

- **Banach spaces**: continuity of linear operators $T: X \to Y$ characterized by $\sup_{\|x\| = 1} \|Tx\|< \infty$.
- **Spectral theory**: $\lim_{n \to \infty} \|T^n x\|^{1/n}$ defines spectral radius.
- **Distributions (Schwartz)**: limit via continuous linear functionals on $C_c^\infty$.

### Non-standard: Robinson's infinitesimal analysis

The ε-δ definition can be replaced by rigorous **infinitesimals** via **hyperreals** $^*\mathbb{R}$ (Robinson 1960). $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \iff f(a + \delta) \approx L$ for every infinitesimal $\delta \neq 0$. Equivalent, but notation closer to Leibniz.

### Numerical

In computational practice, ε-δ becomes **error estimation**: libraries (NumPy, MATLAB) use tolerances `atol`, `rtol` that are finite versions of the definition. Iterative convergence is only declared when the error is below a predefined ε.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practical application">

**Recipe: how to compute or prove a limit.**

### To compute numerically

1. **Substitute directly**. If $f(a)$ exists and $f$ is continuous, $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$.
2. **If you get an indeterminate form ($0/0$, $\infty/\infty$)**, manipulate:
   - **Factor and cancel**: $(x^2 - 4)/(x - 2) = x + 2$ if $x \neq 2$.
   - **Multiply by the conjugate**: $(\sqrt{x+1} - 1)/x \cdot (\sqrt{x+1} + 1)/(\sqrt{x+1} + 1)$.
   - **Use notable limits**: $\sin x \approx x$, $1 - \cos x \approx x^2/2$.
   - **Change variable**: $u = 1/x$, $u = x - a$, etc.
3. **L'Hôpital** (Term 7) is a last resort.

### Wolfram Alpha / SymPy

```python
import sympy as sp
x = sp.symbols('x')
print(sp.limit(sp.sin(x)/x, x, 0))         # 1
print(sp.limit((1 + 1/x)**x, x, sp.oo))    # E
print(sp.limit(1/x, x, 0, '+'))            # +oo
print(sp.limit(1/x, x, 0, '-'))            # -oo
```

Use it to **check** your manual calculations.

### When the limit DOES NOT exist

- **One-sided limits differ**: $\lim_{x \to 0} 1/x$ does not exist ($+\infty$ on the right, $-\infty$ on the left).
- **Oscillation**: $\lim_{x \to 0} \sin(1/x)$ does not exist (oscillates between $-1$ and $1$).
- **Non-removable discontinuity**: $\lfloor x \rfloor$ at integers.

You rarely need it in practice — engineering uses the properties. But knowing how to prove it **demonstrates understanding**. Do exercises 41.21–41.30 with pencil and paper.
