<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="FCP and trees">

### Statement

If an experiment consists of $k$ successive and independent stages, with $n_1$ outcomes in the first, $n_2$ in the second, ..., $n_k$ in the $k$-th, then the total number of possible outcomes is:
$$N = n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdots n_k$$

### Additive principle (alternative)

If a task can be done by method A in $m$ ways OR by method B in $n$ ways (mutually exclusive), the total is $m + n$.

| Connector | Operation |
|---|---|
| "AND" (sequence) | multiplication |
| "OR" (alternative) | addition |

### Archetypal example

4-character password: each can be A-Z (26 options). Total: $26^4 = 456\,976$.

### Tree of possibilities

Each stage "branches" — the tree has $n_1$ roots, each with $n_2$ children, and so on. Leaves = total of outcomes.

### Constraints — "no repetition"

If shoes can't repeat, the first has 5 options, the second 4, the third 3 — combinations without repetition. Generalizes to permutation/arrangement (Lesson 37).

### Functions between sets

- **Total functions** $f: A \to B$ with $|A| = m, |B| = n$: $n^m$.
- **Injective functions** ($f: A \to B$ with $m \leq n$): $n!/(n-m)!$ (arrangement).
- **Bijective functions** ($m = n$): $n!$ (permutation).

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

Imagine you have **3 hats** and **2 backpacks**. How many combinations can you wear to school?

For each hat, you can pick any backpack. So: hat 1 with backpack 1, hat 1 with backpack 2, hat 2 with backpack 1... in total **3 × 2 = 6 ways**.

Multiplication answers this kind of question!

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

FCP is the easiest rule of combinatorics.

**Master rule**: if you need to make several choices in sequence, **multiply** the options at each stage.

**Daily examples**:
- 3 shirts × 4 pants = **12 outfits**.
- Menu: 5 dishes, 3 sides, 4 desserts = **60 meals**.
- 4-digit password: each digit 0-9 = $10^4 = 10\,000$ passwords (that's why it's weak!).
- Toss 3 coins: $2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8$ outcomes.

**Caution: "no repetition"**: if options decrease at each choice, adjust:
- If 3 different prizes go to 5 people (no repeats): $5 \times 4 \times 3 = 60$.
- Whoever won 1st can't win 2nd.

**Tree-drawing trick**: draw a tree with branches for each option. The leaves are the outcomes.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

FCP is the simplest — and most powerful — idea in combinatorics.

**Example**: you have 3 shirts and 4 pants. How many outfit combinations? **3 × 4 = 12**. Each shirt can go with any pants.

**Another**: menu with 5 main dishes, 3 sides, 4 desserts. Possible meals: 5 × 3 × 4 = **60**.

**Password**: 4 numeric digits (0-9). Each digit has 10 options. Total: $10^4 = 10\,000$. That's why 4-digit passwords are weak.

**Watch "no repetition"**: if 3 different prizes go to 5 people, with no one taking 2: first prize 5 options, second 4 (one already taken), third 3. Total: $5 \cdot 4 \cdot 3 = 60$.

**AND vs OR** — the classic confusion:
- "Shirt **and** pants" → multiply.
- "Pencil **or** pen" (mutually exclusive) → add.

**Cases with constraints**: "How many 4-digit numbers with first $\neq 0$?" $9 \cdot 10 \cdot 10 \cdot 10 = 9\,000$.

**Inclusion-exclusion principle** (for non-exclusive events): $|A \cup B| = |A| + |B| - |A \cap B|$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

FCP is the basis of combinatorics, discrete probability, and information theory.

### Full permutation

$n$ objects arranged in $n$ positions: $n!$ (Lesson 37).

### Permutation with repetition

Letters in the word ARARA: 5 letters with 3 A's and 2 R's. Total: $5!/(3! \cdot 2!) = 10$.

### FCP in probability

$P(A) = $ favorable / total. The total is often FCP. Roll 2 dice: $6 \cdot 6 = 36$ equally likely outcomes.

### Cross-domain

- **Cryptography**: size of the key space. Brute force breaks in $\sim 2^{n}/2$ attempts. AES-128 → $2^{128}$.
- **Networking**: IPv4 ($2^{32}$) vs IPv6 ($2^{128}$) addresses.
- **Genetics**: $4^n$ DNA sequences with $n$ base pairs.
- **Hashing**: collision probability (birthday paradox).
- **Information theory**: $\log_2 N$ bits needed to identify one of $N$ options (Shannon entropy).

### Structural combinatorics

- **Functions** $A \to B$: $|B|^{|A|}$ — hence "$2^{S}$" = subsets of $S$ via characteristic function.
- **Multinomial**: $(x_1 + \cdots + x_k)^n = \sum \binom{n}{n_1, \ldots, n_k} x_1^{n_1} \cdots x_k^{n_k}$.
- **Inclusion-exclusion** (Möbius): $|A_1 \cup \cdots \cup A_n| = \sum (-1)^{k+1} \sum_{|S|=k} |A_S|$.

### Pigeonhole principle

$n+1$ objects in $n$ boxes ⟹ some box has $\geq 2$. Generalizes: $kn+1$ objects in $n$ boxes ⟹ some box has $\geq k+1$.

Application: in any group of 13 people, 2 were born in the same month.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

FCP is an elementary instance of **combinatorial species** (Joyal) — a categorical formalism that generalizes enumeration.

### Generating functions

- **EGF** ($\sum a_n x^n / n!$) for labeled structures.
- **OGF** ($\sum a_n x^n$) for unlabeled.
- Operations: union → addition, product → product, sequence → $1/(1-A)$.

### Crypto/security

- **Brute force**: $2^k$ attempts for a $k$-bit key.
- **Birthday attack**: hash collision in $2^{k/2}$ — hence SHA-256 effectively offers 128 bits.
- **Password entropy**: $\log_2(N^L)$ where $N$ is the alphabet, $L$ the length. Human passwords have far less entropy than they appear.

### Quant — combinatorial pricing

- **Binomial models**: tree with $2^n$ paths. Recursion prunes to $O(n^2)$.
- **Trinomial**: $3^n$ paths.
- **Monte Carlo**: samples from a space of astronomical size.

### Research

- **Polynomial method**: uses generating functions for extremal combinatorics problems.
- **Probabilistic method**: existence via $E[X] > 0$.
- **Algorithmic enumeration**: generate $\binom{n}{k}$ subsets without repetition, in a specific order.

### Big numbers

- $52!$ ≈ $8 \times 10^{67}$ — deck shuffles. More than the existing gold atoms.
- $2^{256}$ — AES-256 key space, ~ atoms in the observable universe.
- $26^{20}$ — 20-letter passwords (≈ $2 \times 10^{28}$).

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practical application">

**FCP workflow.**

1. **Identify stages**: each decision is a stage.
2. **Count options per stage**: respect constraints ("no repetition", "first $\neq 0$", etc).
3. **AND or OR?** Sequential stages → multiply. Mutually exclusive → add.
4. **Verify with small cases**: list explicitly for $n = 2$ or $3$ and check.

### Python recipe

```python
import itertools

# Cartesian product
list(itertools.product([1,2,3], ['a','b']))   # 6 pairs

# Permutations
list(itertools.permutations([1,2,3]))           # 6

# Permutations of size k
list(itertools.permutations([1,2,3,4], 2))      # 12 (arrangement)

# Combinations
list(itertools.combinations([1,2,3,4], 2))      # 6

# With replacement
list(itertools.combinations_with_replacement([1,2,3], 2))

# For direct counting:
import math
math.factorial(5)
math.perm(10, 3)         # arrangement A(10,3) = 720
math.comb(10, 3)         # combination C(10,3) = 120
```

### SymPy

```python
import sympy as sp
sp.factorial(10)
sp.binomial(60, 6)       # mega-sena: 50,063,860
```

### Pitfalls

- **Distinguish "ordered" vs "unordered"**. Password = ordered (1234 ≠ 4321), committee = unordered.
- **"At least one"** is usually easier via the complement: total − cases without.
- **Disjoint cases**: if you split the problem, make sure not to double-count.
- **Constraints**: handle the most restricted position first.

### Essential task

Solve every exercise. FCP is the foundation — every combinatorics problem comes from it.
