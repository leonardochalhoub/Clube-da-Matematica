<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Computation and properties">

### 2x2

$$\det \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} = ad - bc$$

### 3x3 — Sarrus's rule

$$\det \begin{pmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i \end{pmatrix} = aei + bfg + cdh - ceg - bdi - afh$$

(Repeat the first 2 columns to the right, 3 descending products − 3 ascending products.)

### n×n — Laplace expansion (cofactors)

$$\det A = \sum_{j=1}^n (-1)^{i+j} a_{ij} M_{ij}$$

where $M_{ij}$ is the **minor** (det of the submatrix removing row $i$ and column $j$). Recursive: reduces $n \times n$ to a sum of $(n-1) \times (n-1)$.

### Definition via permutations (Leibniz)

$$\det A = \sum_{\sigma \in S_n} \text{sgn}(\sigma) \prod_{i=1}^n a_{i,\sigma(i)}$$

Sum over all $n!$ permutations.

### Properties

| # | Property |
|---|---|
| 1 | $\det(I) = 1$ |
| 2 | $\det(A^T) = \det(A)$ |
| 3 | $\det(AB) = \det(A) \cdot \det(B)$ (Cauchy-Binet) |
| 4 | $\det(\alpha A) = \alpha^n \det(A)$ for $A_{n\times n}$ |
| 5 | $\det(A^{-1}) = 1/\det(A)$ |
| 6 | Swapping 2 rows/columns flips the sign |
| 7 | Row of zeros ⟹ $\det = 0$ |
| 8 | Equal rows ⟹ $\det = 0$ |
| 9 | Proportional rows ⟹ $\det = 0$ |
| 10 | Adding a multiple of one row to another **does not change** $\det$ |
| 11 | Multiplying a row by $\alpha$ multiplies $\det$ by $\alpha$ |
| 12 | Triangular: $\det = $ product of the diagonal |

### Geometric interpretation

- $|\det A|$ = volume of the parallelepiped generated by the columns of $A$.
- $\det A > 0$: orientation preserved. $\det A < 0$: orientation reversed.
- $\det A = 0$: linearly dependent columns ("flattened" parallelepiped).

### Invertibility criterion

$A$ invertible $\iff \det A \neq 0$.

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

Imagine your matrix is a toy **cardboard box**. The **determinant** is just the **size of the box** — how much fits inside.

If the box is **flat** like paper (squashed), the size is zero — nothing fits.

Some boxes flip "inside out" when you multiply — then the size is negative. It's just a way of saying that the box flipped.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

The determinant is a number that measures the **size** of the matrix, more or less:
- For a $2 \times 2$ matrix, it's the **area** of the parallelogram formed by the columns.
- For $3 \times 3$, it's the **volume** of the parallelepiped.

**Direct calculation for 2×2**: $\det\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} = ad - bc$. Multiply main diagonal, subtract off-diagonal.

**For 3×3 use Sarrus**: copy the first 2 columns to the right, sum the 3 descending products, subtract the 3 ascending ones.

**Cool things the determinant tells you**:
- $\det = 0$ → the matrix is "flattened", no inverse.
- $\det \neq 0$ → the matrix has an inverse.
- Negative $\det$ → the matrix "flips" space.

**Application**: Cramer's rule gives the system solution using determinants — pretty, but slow for big systems.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

The determinant is a **number** computed from a square matrix.

For 2x2:
$$\det \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} = ad - bc$$

For 3x3 use **Sarrus**:
$$\det\begin{pmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i \end{pmatrix} = aei + bfg + cdh - ceg - bdi - afh$$

For 4×4 or larger, use **Laplace** (cofactor expansion) or row reduction (more efficient).

**Why does it matter?**

1. **Invertibility**: $\det A \neq 0$ ⟺ $A$ is invertible.
2. **Cramer**: solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ uses ratios of determinants.
3. **Area and volume**: for 2x2, $|\det A|$ is the **area** of the parallelogram formed by the columns. For 3x3, the **volume**.

**Geometrically**: if the columns of $A$ are "vectors pointing in the same direction", the parallelogram is thin and $\det A$ is small. If they're perpendicular, the parallelogram is "fat" and $\det A$ is large.

**Essential properties to memorize**:
- $\det(AB) = \det A \cdot \det B$ — multiplicative!
- Swapping two rows flips the sign.
- Row of zeros or two equal rows ⟹ $\det = 0$.
- Triangular: product of the diagonal.

**Computation tip**: for large matrices, row-reduce to triangular — property 10 keeps the determinant. Then multiply the diagonal.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

The determinant is the **unique** multilinear, alternating functional that equals 1 on the identity. This axiomatic characterization (Weierstrass) defines $\det$ uniquely.

### Axioms (Weierstrass)

1. **Multilinear** in the rows (and columns).
2. **Alternating**: swapping two rows flips the sign.
3. **Normalized**: $\det I = 1$.

Everything else follows.

### Proof: $\det(AB) = \det A \det B$

Fix $B$. Define $f(A) = \det(AB)$. Then $f$ is multilinear and alternating in the rows of $A$. So $f(A) = c \det A$ for some $c$. Take $A = I$: $f(I) = \det B = c$. So $\det(AB) = \det A \det B$.

### Cofactors and adjugate

$\text{adj}(A) = (C_{ij})^T$ where $C_{ij} = (-1)^{i+j} M_{ij}$. Fundamental identity:
$$A \cdot \text{adj}(A) = \det(A) \cdot I$$

So $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A} \text{adj}(A)$ — useful theoretically, terrible numerically.

### Cross-domain

- **CG**: Jacobian $J = \det\frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)}$ corrects areas under coordinate change. Surface/volume integrals.
- **Mechanics**: Jacobian of the body→reference transformation measures deformation.
- **Quantum**: Slater determinant for fermions (antisymmetry).
- **Linear ODEs**: Wronskian $W(t) = \det\begin{pmatrix} y_1 & y_2 \\ y_1' & y_2' \end{pmatrix}$ — independence of solutions.
- **Dynamical systems**: $\det A$ = product of eigenvalues. Sign of $\det$ relates to stability.

### Oriented volume

In $\mathbb R^n$, $|\det A|$ = $n$-dimensional volume generated by the columns. Via SVD, $|\det A| = \prod \sigma_i$ (singular values).

### Numerical computation

- **Laplace** $O(n!)$ — only for small $n$.
- **Via LU**: $\det A = \pm \prod u_{ii}$ ($O(n^3)$). **Standard.**
- Beware of underflow for large $n$: use $\log|\det A|$.

### Connection to eigenvalues

$$\det A = \prod_i \lambda_i, \quad \text{tr}(A) = \sum_i \lambda_i$$

They characterize the matrix more than they appear to. In $2\times 2$: $\lambda_{1,2} = \frac{\text{tr} \pm \sqrt{\text{tr}^2 - 4\det}}{2}$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

The determinant is the **unique** matrix invariant that naturally generalizes to the **volume form** in differential geometry — via $\det$ one defines the canonical volume form $dx_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge dx_n$ on $\mathbb R^n$.

### Tensorial view

$\det A = \frac{1}{n!}\epsilon^{i_1\cdots i_n} \epsilon_{j_1 \cdots j_n} A^{j_1}_{i_1} \cdots A^{j_n}_{i_n}$ — contraction with Levi-Civita. Defines $\det$ on any space over any field (even positive characteristic, with caveats).

### Wolfram tour

```mathematica
A = {{1, 2, 3}, {4, 5, 6}, {7, 8, 10}};
Det[A]
Minors[A, 2]                    (* all 2x2 minors *)
Cofactor[A, {1, 1}]
Eigenvalues[A]
```

### Jacobi's identity

$$\frac{d}{dt}\det A(t) = \det A(t) \cdot \text{tr}\left(A(t)^{-1} \dot A(t)\right)$$

Crucial in continuum mechanics (rate of volume change), stochastic processes, Hamiltonian flows.

### Generalized Cauchy-Binet

For $A_{m\times n}, B_{n\times m}$ with $m \leq n$:
$$\det(AB) = \sum_{S \in \binom{[n]}{m}} \det(A_{[m],S}) \det(B_{S,[m]})$$

Appears in statistics (Gram matrix) and combinatorial physics.

### Quant/finance — risks and copulas

- $\det(\Sigma)$: aggregated total-variance measure of a portfolio.
- Change of measure via Radon-Nikodym uses Jacobians.
- Multidimensional copulas: mixed partial derivative = $\det$ of related Hessian.

### Modern computation

- **Determinant via Schur complement**: for blocks $\begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ C & D \end{pmatrix}$, $\det = \det(A) \det(D - CA^{-1}B)$.
- **Log-determinant** to avoid overflow: $\log\det = \sum \log|u_{ii}|$ via LU. Standard in ML (Gaussian priors).

### Modern research and applications

- **Determinantal point processes (DPPs)**: $P(S) \propto \det(K_S)$. Models diversity in recommender systems, quantum.
- **Computational complexity**: Valiant's $\#P$ class — permanent is hard, determinant is easy. Fundamental distinction.
- **Pfaffian**: $\text{Pf}(A)^2 = \det(A)$ for skew-symmetric — combinatorics of perfect matchings.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practical application">

**Workflow for computing determinants.**

1. **2×2**: $ad - bc$ directly. 4 multiplications + 1 subtraction.
2. **3×3**: Sarrus. Faster than Laplace.
3. **n×n** (n ≥ 4): row reduce → triangular → diagonal product.
4. **Numerically**: use LU; never implement Laplace in production.

### NumPy recipe

```python
import numpy as np

A = np.array([[1, 2, 3], [4, 5, 6], [7, 8, 10]], dtype=float)

# Determinant
print(np.linalg.det(A))        # via LU internally

# Log-determinant (stable)
sign, logdet = np.linalg.slogdet(A)
print(sign * np.exp(logdet))   # equivalent to det

# For ill-conditioned matrices, prefer slogdet
```

### Symbolic SymPy

```python
import sympy as sp
A = sp.Matrix([[1, 2], [3, 4]])
print(A.det())                 # exact closed form
```

### Explicit Cramer for 2×2

```python
def cramer_2x2(A, b):
    a, B, c, d = A[0, 0], A[0, 1], A[1, 0], A[1, 1]
    det = a * d - B * c
    if abs(det) < 1e-12:
        raise ValueError("Singular system")
    x = (b[0] * d - B * b[1]) / det
    y = (a * b[1] - b[0] * c) / det
    return np.array([x, y])
```

### Common pitfalls

- **`np.linalg.det` returns float64 even for integer matrices** — may have rounding errors. Use SymPy for exact.
- **Large determinant** can overflow — use `slogdet`.
- **Cramer doesn't scale**: $O(n^4)$ if naive, $O(n^3)$ optimized, vs Gauss $O(n^3)$ directly.
- **Sarrus** **DOES NOT work** for $4\times 4$ or larger — only $3\times 3$.

### Essential task

Solve every exercise. The determinant is a structural tool, not a computational one — understanding the properties is worth more than computing big numbers.
