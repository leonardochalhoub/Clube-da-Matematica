<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Operations">

### Sum and difference

For matrices of **the same dimension**:
$$(A + B)_{ij} = a_{ij} + b_{ij}$$

### Scalar multiplication

$$(\alpha A)_{ij} = \alpha \cdot a_{ij}$$

### Matrix product

Defined **only when** the number of columns of $A$ = number of rows of $B$:
$$A_{m \times n} \cdot B_{n \times p} = (AB)_{m \times p}$$

$$\boxed{(AB)_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^n a_{ik} b_{kj}}$$

### Properties

- **Associative**: $(AB)C = A(BC)$.
- **Distributive**: $A(B + C) = AB + AC$.
- **NOT commutative**: in general $AB \neq BA$.
- **Identity**: $AI = IA = A$.
- **Zero**: $AO = OA = O$.

### Why matrix product is "strange"

Because it corresponds to the **composition** of linear transformations: applying $B$ first and then $A$ is the same as applying $AB$. Order matters because composition matters.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

**Sum**: term by term. $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 8 \\ 10 & 12 \end{pmatrix}$.

**Scalar**: multiply every element. $3 \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 6 \\ 9 & 12 \end{pmatrix}$.

**Product** is more complicated: row × column. To multiply $A \cdot B$, the number of columns of $A$ must equal the number of rows of $B$.

Example: $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \cdot 5 + 2 \cdot 6 \\ 3 \cdot 5 + 4 \cdot 6 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 17 \\ 39 \end{pmatrix}$.

**Be careful**: $AB$ is rarely equal to $BA$. **Order matters.**

</Porta>
