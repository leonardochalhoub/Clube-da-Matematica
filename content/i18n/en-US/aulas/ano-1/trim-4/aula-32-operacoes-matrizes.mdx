<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Operations">

### Sum and difference

For matrices of **the same dimension**:
$$(A + B)_{ij} = a_{ij} + b_{ij}$$

### Scalar multiplication

$$(\alpha A)_{ij} = \alpha \cdot a_{ij}$$

### Matrix product

Defined **only when** the number of columns of $A$ = number of rows of $B$:
$$A_{m \times n} \cdot B_{n \times p} = (AB)_{m \times p}$$

$$\boxed{(AB)_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^n a_{ik} b_{kj}}$$

### Properties

| Operation | Property |
|---|---|
| Sum | commutative, associative, identity $O$ |
| Scalar | distributive: $\alpha(A+B) = \alpha A + \alpha B$ |
| Product | associative: $(AB)C = A(BC)$ |
| Product | distributive: $A(B+C) = AB + AC$ |
| Product | **NOT commutative** in general |
| Identity | $AI = IA = A$ |
| Zero | $AO = OA = O$ |

### Why the matrix product is "weird"

Because it corresponds to **composition** of linear transformations: applying first $B$ and then $A$ is the same as applying $AB$. Order matters because composition matters.

### Powers

$A^n = A \cdot A \cdots A$ ($n$ times), $A^0 = I$. Defined only for square matrices.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

Imagine two egg cartons of **the same size**. To **add**, just add egg by egg, slot by slot.

To **multiply everyone by 3**, multiply each egg by 3 — they get three times bigger.

But there's a **magic trick** called "multiplying boxes". It's quite different: a row of the first meets a column of the second, they mix, and that gives a new number. It's **harder**, but it's what computers do millions of times per second.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

**Sum and scalar are easy**:
- Sum: term by term (need the same size).
- Scalar: multiply everything by the number.

**Matrix product** is the trick. Imagine:
- You have **prices per product** (matrix $A$).
- You have **quantities per store** (matrix $B$).
- You want **revenue per store**: product $AB$!

Each entry of $AB$ comes from "row times column": take row $i$ of $A$, take column $j$ of $B$, multiply pair by pair, and sum it all. That's $(AB)_{ij}$.

**Crucial rule**: to multiply $A \cdot B$, the number of columns of $A$ must equal the number of rows of $B$. Otherwise it doesn't work.

**Strange thing**: $AB$ is rarely equal to $BA$! Order matters.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

**Sum**: term by term. $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 8 \\ 10 & 12 \end{pmatrix}$.

**Scalar**: multiply every entry. $3 \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 6 \\ 9 & 12 \end{pmatrix}$.

**Product** is more complicated: row × column. To multiply $A \cdot B$, the number of columns of $A$ must equal the number of rows of $B$.

Step-by-step example: $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix}$.

- Row 1 × column 1: $1 \cdot 5 + 2 \cdot 6 = 17$.
- Row 2 × column 1: $3 \cdot 5 + 4 \cdot 6 = 39$.

Result: $\begin{pmatrix} 17 \\ 39 \end{pmatrix}$.

**Beware**: $AB$ rarely equals $BA$. **Order matters.** Try $A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ — you'll see that $AB \neq BA$ and even $AB$ and $BA$ have different traces.

**Identity** is like the "1": $AI = IA = A$.

**Why all this?** Because **composition** of linear transformations — rotations, reflections, projections, scalings — is exactly the matrix product. You're rotating + scaling + projecting all at once.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

Matrix operations are primitives of **computational linear algebra**. In BLAS:
- **Level 1** (vector-vector): sum, scalar, inner product.
- **Level 2** (matrix-vector): $A\mathbf x$ — $O(mn)$.
- **Level 3** (matrix-matrix): $AB$ — $O(mnp)$.

### Proof: product = composition

Let $T_A: \mathbb R^n \to \mathbb R^m$ and $T_B: \mathbb R^p \to \mathbb R^n$ be linear maps with matrices $A, B$. Then $T_A \circ T_B: \mathbb R^p \to \mathbb R^m$ has matrix $AB$. This explains:

- **Non-commutativity**: a rotation $R_\theta$ followed by a reflection $S$ differs from $S$ followed by $R_\theta$.
- **Associativity**: composition of functions is associative.

### Computational cost

- Sum: $O(mn)$ flops.
- Product $A_{m\times n} B_{n\times p}$: $O(mnp)$ flops.
- **Strassen**: $O(n^{2.807})$ — only for large $n$, with overhead.
- **Coppersmith-Winograd and variants**: theoretical $O(n^{2.37})$, rarely implemented.

### Numerical stability

Relative error of $AB$: $\|AB - \widehat{AB}\|/\|AB\| \lesssim n \epsilon_\text{mach}$ — grows with $n$. In float32 vs float64, it matters a lot in deep networks (mixed precision).

### Cross-domain fundamental identities

- **Engineering**: $\dot{\mathbf x} = A\mathbf x + B\mathbf u$ — sum of matrix terms is the base equation of control.
- **ML**: forward pass: $\mathbf z = W\mathbf x + \mathbf b$, $\mathbf a = \sigma(\mathbf z)$. Each layer is GEMM + non-linearity.
- **Quantum physics**: commutator $[A, B] = AB - BA$ measures non-commutativity — Heisenberg uncertainty relation.
- **Statistics**: $\text{Cov}(X) = E[XX^T] - \mu\mu^T$ — sum and matrix product.

### Idempotent, nilpotent, involution

- **Idempotent** ($A^2 = A$): projections.
- **Nilpotent** ($A^k = 0$): strictly triangular matrices, Jordan blocks.
- **Involution** ($A^2 = I$): reflections.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

Matrix operations are instances of the **associative algebra** structure on $M_n$, and the product is an instance of **morphism composition** in $\text{Hom}(V, W)$ in the category $\textbf{Vect}$.

### Algebraic structure

$(M_n(\mathbb R), +, \cdot)$ is an **algebra**:
- $(M_n, +)$ is an abelian group.
- $(M_n, \cdot)$ is a monoid with identity $I_n$, **non-commutative** for $n \geq 2$.
- $\cdot$ distributes over $+$.

There are **zero divisors**: $A, B \neq 0$ with $AB = 0$. So $M_n$ is not an integral domain. Example: $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = O$.

### Wolfram tour

```mathematica

MatrixPower[A, 5]
```

### GEMM on modern hardware

- **Tensor cores** (NVIDIA Volta+) execute mixed-precision GEMM (FP16 input, FP32 accumulator) in a single instruction. All modern generative AI depends on this.
- **Google's TPU**: systolic array — multiplies $128 \times 128$ blocks per cycle.
- **Roofline model**: many GEMMs are compute-bound; some memory-bound — critical distinction in optimization.

### Quant/finance

PDE pricing: discretization yields $\mathbf u^{n+1} = (I - \tau A)^{-1}(I + \tau B) \mathbf u^n$ (Crank-Nicolson). Each step is two matrix-vector products + one linear solve.

### Categorical generalizations

- **$\text{Hom}(V, W)$** with sum (pointwise) and composition is an **operator algebra**.
- In **infinite dim**: bounded operators on Hilbert form a $C^*$-algebra.
- **Dual numbers** $a + b\epsilon$ ($\epsilon^2 = 0$) with matrix $\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ 0 & a \end{pmatrix}$ — used in forward-mode autodiff.

### Current research

- **Sub-cubic matrix multiplication**: $\omega < 2.37$ (Strassen-improved, Strassen-like) — open whether $\omega = 2$.
- **Sparse linear algebra**: GraphBLAS standardizes operations over semirings (shortest paths = product over $(\min, +)$).

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practical application">

**Recipe: how to combine matrix operations fluently.**

1. **Check dimensions before multiplying**. Dimension errors are bug #1 in numerical code.
2. **Use optimized libraries** (NumPy/SciPy/MATLAB), never implement triple-loop kernels in production.
3. **Vectorize** — broadcasting + ufuncs in NumPy.

### NumPy recipe

```python
import numpy as np

A = np.array([[1, 2], [3, 4]])
B = np.array([[5, 6], [7, 8]])

# Sum and scalar
print(A + B)
print(3 * A)

# Matrix product — use @ (Python 3.5+)
print(A @ B)
print(np.matmul(A, B))    # equivalent

# CAUTION: A * B is HADAMARD (element-wise), NOT matrix product!
print(A * B)              # different from A @ B

# Power
print(np.linalg.matrix_power(A, 5))

# Identity and zero
I = np.eye(2)
O = np.zeros((2, 2))
print(A @ I)              # == A
```

### Symbolic SymPy

```python
import sympy as sp
A = sp.Matrix([[1, 2], [3, 4]])
B = sp.Matrix([[5, 6], [7, 8]])
print(A * B)              # matrix product in SymPy
print((A * B).T == B.T * A.T)
```

### Non-commutativity check

```python
def commute(A, B):
    return np.allclose(A @ B, B @ A)

R = np.array([[0, -1], [1, 0]])     # 90° rotation
S = np.array([[1, 0], [0, -1]])     # reflection across x-axis
print(commute(R, S))                # False — order matters
```

### Common pitfalls

- **`*` is Hadamard in NumPy, not matrix product**. Always use `@`.
- **Shape `(n,)` vs `(n,1)`**: 1D vector vs column. `A @ v` works with both but the result may surprise you.
- **`np.dot` for 2D is matrix; for 1D is inner product**. Prefer `@`.
- **`A**2` in NumPy is element-wise!** Use `np.linalg.matrix_power(A, 2)` or `A @ A`.
