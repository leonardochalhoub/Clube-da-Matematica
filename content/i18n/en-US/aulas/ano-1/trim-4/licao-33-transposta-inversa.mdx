<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Transpose and inverse">

### Transpose

$(A^T)_{ij} = a_{ji}$. Rows are swapped for columns. Properties:

| Property | Formula |
|---|---|
| Involution | $(A^T)^T = A$ |
| Sum | $(A + B)^T = A^T + B^T$ |
| Scalar | $(\alpha A)^T = \alpha A^T$ |
| Product | $(AB)^T = B^T A^T$ (reverses order) |
| Inverse-transpose | $(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T$ |

**Symmetric** matrix: $A^T = A$. **Orthogonal** matrix: $A^T A = I$ ⟺ $A^{-1} = A^T$.

### Identity

$I_n$: $n \times n$ square matrix with 1's on the diagonal and 0 off. For every $A_{n \times n}$:
$$AI = IA = A$$

### Inverse

$A_{n \times n}$ is **invertible** (non-singular) if there exists $A^{-1}$ such that $AA^{-1} = A^{-1}A = I$. **Equivalence theorem** — for square $A$, the following are equivalent:

1. $A$ is invertible.
2. $\det A \neq 0$.
3. $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ has only $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$.
4. The columns of $A$ are linearly independent.
5. The rows of $A$ are linearly independent.
6. $A$ has full rank: $\text{rank}(A) = n$.
7. $A$ is a product of elementary matrices.

### 2x2 inverse

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}, \quad A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad - bc} \begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$$

(Valid if $ad - bc \neq 0$.)

### Inverse properties

- $(A^{-1})^{-1} = A$
- $(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1} A^{-1}$ (**reverses order!**)
- $(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T$
- $(\alpha A)^{-1} = (1/\alpha) A^{-1}$
- $\det(A^{-1}) = 1/\det(A)$

### Computation via Gauss-Jordan

Form $[A | I]$ → row-reduce until $[I | A^{-1}]$. Cost $O(n^3)$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

The **transpose** is like flipping the matrix on its side: what was standing up lies down, what was lying down stands up. Rows become columns.

The **inverse** is like **undoing**. If matrix $A$ is "putting on a coat", the inverse $A^{-1}$ is "taking off the coat". Putting it on then taking it off goes back to the start: $A \cdot A^{-1} = I$ (identity = nothing changed).

But careful: not every matrix has an inverse! Some matrices **flatten** things and you can't unflatten them.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

**Transpose**: swaps rows and columns. If $A$ has 2 rows and 3 columns, $A^T$ will have 3 rows and 2 columns.

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix}^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 2 & 5 \\ 3 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$$

The **inverse** is the "undo": $A \cdot A^{-1} = I$ (identity). Like dividing, but for matrices.

Think of it this way:
- You multiplied something by $A$ and want to reverse it. How? Multiply by the inverse $A^{-1}$.
- In an equation $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$, the "solution" is $\mathbf x = A^{-1} \mathbf b$ — just like dividing by $A$.

**But careful**: not every matrix has an inverse! Those with "zero determinant" (we'll see this in the next lesson) do not have an inverse because they lose information.

**Trick for 2×2**: $\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$. Swap the diagonal entries, flip the sign of the others, divide by $ad - bc$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

**Transpose** is simple: swap rows for columns.

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix}, \quad A^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 2 & 5 \\ 3 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$$

The most important and counterintuitive rule: $(AB)^T = B^T A^T$ — **reverses the order**. Same thing for inverse: $(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$. Memorize this.

**Identity** is the matrix with 1's on the diagonal and zeros off. Behaves like the "1" in multiplication: $AI = A$.

**Inverse** $A^{-1}$ is the matrix that "undoes" $A$. $AA^{-1} = I$. But careful: not every matrix has an inverse! Only **square ones with non-zero determinant**.

For 2x2: $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad - bc}\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$.

Example: $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$. $\det = 1\cdot 4 - 2\cdot 3 = -2$. $A^{-1} = -\frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -3 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1 \\ 3/2 & -1/2 \end{pmatrix}$.

**Where it appears?** Solving the system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is equivalent to $\mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$. In practice, row reduction is used (more efficient than computing the inverse).

**Quick criterion to have an inverse**: $\det A \neq 0$. If $\det A = 0$, the matrix is **singular** — it compresses space, loses dimension, can't be undone.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

The inverse is rarely computed **explicitly** in numerical practice — row reduction is more stable and cheaper. But the theory of the inverse is central to understanding when systems have solutions.

### Proof: $(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$

Show that $(AB)(B^{-1}A^{-1}) = I$:
$$(AB)(B^{-1}A^{-1}) = A(BB^{-1})A^{-1} = AIA^{-1} = AA^{-1} = I.$$

By uniqueness of the inverse (in an associative algebra with identity), this proves it.

### Computation of $A^{-1}$ — methods

1. **Adjugate**: $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A} \text{adj}(A)$ — $O(n!)$, didactic but useless for $n > 3$.
2. **Gauss-Jordan on $[A | I]$**: $O(n^3)$, standard.
3. **LU**: decompose $A = LU$, then solve $LUx_j = e_j$ for each column. Same $O(n^3)$ but reuses the factorization.

### Numerical stability

**Conditioning**: $\kappa(A) = \|A\| \|A^{-1}\|$. If $\kappa$ is large, small errors in $\mathbf b$ are amplified in $\mathbf x = A^{-1}\mathbf b$. Criterion: $\kappa = 10^k$ ⟹ loss of $\approx k$ significant digits.

### Cross-domain

- **Cryptography**: matrix ciphers (Hill cipher) use $\mathbf c = A\mathbf m \mod 26$. Decryption: $\mathbf m = A^{-1}\mathbf c$.
- **Computer graphics**: applying a transformation to the camera = applying the inverse to objects. View-Projection model.
- **Economics (Leontief)**: technology matrix $A$. For final demand $\mathbf d$, total production $\mathbf x = (I-A)^{-1}\mathbf d$.
- **Statistics**: $\hat\beta = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T \mathbf y$ — least-squares normal equation.
- **Filters (Kalman)**: gain $K = P H^T (H P H^T + R)^{-1}$.

### Block inverse

$$\begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ C & D \end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} + A^{-1}B(D - CA^{-1}B)^{-1}CA^{-1} & \cdots \\ \cdots & \cdots \end{pmatrix}$$

(Schur complement). Useful in optimization and Bayesian statistics.

### Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse

When $A$ is not square or is singular: $A^+ = (A^TA)^{-1}A^T$ (overdetermined) or $A^+ = A^T(AA^T)^{-1}$ (underdetermined). Generalizes the inverse via SVD.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

Invertibility characterizes the **general linear group** $GL_n(\mathbb R) = \{A \in M_n : \det A \neq 0\}$. It is the central object of **Lie theory** — every continuous matrix group is a subgroup of $GL_n$.

### Decompositions and the inverse

- **LU**: $A = LU$ ⟹ $A^{-1} = U^{-1} L^{-1}$. Triangulars are easy to invert.
- **QR**: $A = QR$ ⟹ $A^{-1} = R^{-1}Q^T$.
- **SVD**: $A = U\Sigma V^T$ ⟹ $A^{-1} = V\Sigma^{-1}U^T$ ($A$ square non-singular).
- **Cholesky** ($A$ SPD): $A = LL^T$ ⟹ $A^{-1} = L^{-T}L^{-1}$.

### Sherman-Morrison

Inverse update when $A$ changes by rank-1:
$$(A + \mathbf u \mathbf v^T)^{-1} = A^{-1} - \frac{A^{-1}\mathbf u \mathbf v^T A^{-1}}{1 + \mathbf v^T A^{-1}\mathbf u}$$

Critical in recursive filters, RLS adaptive filtering, online ML.

### Pseudoinverse via SVD

For any $A \in M_{m\times n}$ (even rectangular or singular): $A = U\Sigma V^T$, then $A^+ = V\Sigma^+ U^T$ where $\Sigma^+$ inverts the non-zero singular values.

Least-squares solution: $\mathbf x = A^+\mathbf b$.

### Quant/finance — Markowitz

$$\mathbf w^* = \frac{\Sigma^{-1}(\boldsymbol \mu - r_f \mathbf 1)}{\mathbf 1^T \Sigma^{-1}(\boldsymbol \mu - r_f \mathbf 1)}$$

Minimum-variance portfolio uses $\Sigma^{-1}$. Estimating $\Sigma^{-1}$ is ill-conditioned — hence robust estimators (graphical lasso, shrinkage).

### Unbounded operators

In infinite dim, $A^{-1}$ may be unbounded even with $A$ bounded. Spectrum vs eigenvalues diverge. Functional analysis takes over.

### Current research

- **Approximate matrix inversion** via random projections (Halko-Martinsson-Tropp).
- **Low-rank updates**: for $A^{-1}$ in online ML, incremental updates with reduced complexity.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practical application">

**Recipe: never invert a matrix if you can avoid it.**

The golden rule of computational linear algebra: **solve the system, don't invert**. `np.linalg.solve(A, b)` is faster and more stable than `np.linalg.inv(A) @ b`.

### NumPy recipe

```python
import numpy as np

A = np.array([[1, 2], [3, 4]], dtype=float)

# Transpose (free, just a view)
print(A.T)

# Inverse (EXPENSIVE AND UNSTABLE — avoid if possible)
A_inv = np.linalg.inv(A)

# Solve Ax = b — preferred!
b = np.array([5, 6])
x = np.linalg.solve(A, b)

# Pseudoinverse for singular or rectangular matrices
A_pinv = np.linalg.pinv(A)

# Conditioning — alert if it's large
print(np.linalg.cond(A))   # if > 1e10, careful
```

### Symbolic SymPy

```python
import sympy as sp
A = sp.Matrix([[1, 2], [3, 4]])
print(A.T)
print(A.inv())             # closed form
print(A.det())
```

### Check

```python
def is_inverse(A, B, tol=1e-9):
    n = A.shape[0]
    return np.allclose(A @ B, np.eye(n), atol=tol)
```

### Common pitfalls

- **Inverse does not exist** if $\det A = 0$. Always check (or handle the `LinAlgError` exception).
- **`np.linalg.inv` on an ill-conditioned matrix** gives numerical garbage even without an error — check `cond`.
- **For LSQ or regression**: never compute $(X^TX)^{-1}$. Use `np.linalg.lstsq` or QR.
- **For SPD systems**: use Cholesky (`scipy.linalg.cho_solve`) — twice as fast as LU.

### Recommended workflow

1. Want $A^{-1}$? What for?
2. To solve $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$? Use `solve`.
3. To solve several systems with the same $A$? Do LU once, reuse.
4. For theory/derivation? Use SymPy or do it by hand.
5. The case where you **really** need the explicit inverse is rare.

### Essential task

Solve every exercise. **Fluency** with the 2×2 inverse is fundamental — you'll use it a thousand times.
