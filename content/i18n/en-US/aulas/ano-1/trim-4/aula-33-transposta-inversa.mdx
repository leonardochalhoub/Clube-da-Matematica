<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Transpose and inverse">

### Transpose

$(A^T)_{ij} = a_{ji}$. Rows are swapped for columns. Properties:

- $(A^T)^T = A$
- $(A + B)^T = A^T + B^T$
- $(\alpha A)^T = \alpha A^T$
- $(AB)^T = B^T A^T$ (**reverses order!**)

**Symmetric** matrix: $A^T = A$.

### Identity

$I_n$: square $n \times n$ matrix with 1's on the diagonal and 0's elsewhere. For all $A_{n \times n}$:
$$AI = IA = A$$

### Inverse

$A_{n \times n}$ is **invertible** if there exists $A^{-1}$ such that $AA^{-1} = A^{-1}A = I$. Equivalents:

1. $A$ is invertible.
2. $\det A \neq 0$.
3. $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ has only $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$.
4. The columns of $A$ are linearly independent.

### 2x2 Inverse

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}, \quad A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad - bc} \begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$$

(Valid if $ad - bc \neq 0$.)

### Properties of the inverse

- $(A^{-1})^{-1} = A$
- $(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1} A^{-1}$ (**reverses order!**)
- $(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T$
- $(\alpha A)^{-1} = (1/\alpha) A^{-1}$

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

**Transpose** is simple: rows become columns.

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix}, \quad A^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 2 & 5 \\ 3 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Identity** is the matrix with 1's on the diagonal and zeros elsewhere. It behaves like the "1" in multiplication: $AI = A$.

**Inverse** $A^{-1}$ is the matrix that "undoes" $A$. $AA^{-1} = I$. But beware: not every matrix has an inverse! Only **square ones with non-zero determinant**.

For 2x2: $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad - bc}\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$.

**Where does it appear?** Solving the system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is equivalent to $\mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$. In practice, row reduction is used (more efficient than computing the inverse).

</Porta>
