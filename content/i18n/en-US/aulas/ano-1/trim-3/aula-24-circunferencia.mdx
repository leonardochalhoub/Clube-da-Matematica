<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Equations and properties">

<Definicao titulo="Circle">
The set of points $(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ that are at distance $r > 0$ from a center $C = (a, b)$.
</Definicao>

### Standard form

$$(x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 = r^2$$

### General form

Expanding: $x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$, with $D = -2a$, $E = -2b$, $F = a^2 + b^2 - r^2$.

Recovered center: $(a, b) = (-D/2, -E/2)$. Radius: $r = \sqrt{D^2/4 + E^2/4 - F}$ (valid if positive; otherwise, not a real circle).

### Line and circle

A line $r$ can:
- **Not intersect** the circle: $d(\text{center}, r) > r$.
- **Be tangent** ($d = r$): exactly 1 point.
- **Be secant** ($d < r$): 2 points of intersection.

### Tangent-radius property

The tangent at $P$ is perpendicular to the radius $\overline{CP}$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

A circle is the set of points **equally distant** from a center. Distance $= r$ (radius).

Applying the distance formula from Lesson 21:
$$\sqrt{(x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2} = r$$

Squaring: $(x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 = r^2$. **That's it**.

When the center is at the origin ($a = b = 0$): $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$. **Unit circle** ($r = 1$): $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ — what you used in Lesson 12 of the trigonometric circle!

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

The circle is the simplest example of a **geometric locus**. Immediate generalization: **sphere** in $\mathbb{R}^3$ — $(x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 + (z-c)^2 = r^2$.

Applications:
- **Telecommunications**: antenna range = circle. Multiple coverage = union of circles.
- **GPS**: each satellite defines a sphere; your location is the intersection of 4 spheres.
- **Robotics**: the reach of an arm with a spherical joint is a sphere.

</Porta>
