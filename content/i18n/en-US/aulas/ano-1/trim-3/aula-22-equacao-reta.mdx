<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Forms of the equation of a line">

### Slope-intercept form

$y = mx + n$, where $m$ is the **slope** and $n$ is the **y-intercept**.

### General form

$Ax + By + C = 0$, with $(A, B) \neq (0, 0)$. For non-vertical lines ($B \neq 0$): $m = -A/B$ and $n = -C/B$.

### Point-slope form

Line through $(x_0, y_0)$ with slope $m$:
$$y - y_0 = m(x - x_0)$$

### Parametric form

Line with direction vector $\vec u = (a, b)$ through $(x_0, y_0)$:
$$\begin{cases} x = x_0 + at \\ y = y_0 + bt \end{cases}, \quad t \in \mathbb{R}$$

### Two-point form

Line through $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$:
$$\frac{y - y_1}{x - x_1} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teenager">

The equation of a line is what connects algebra to geometry. Every line in the plane has an equation $y = mx + n$ that describes **all of its points**.

- $m$ is the **slope** (how much the line "rises" or "falls").
- $n$ is where the line crosses the $y$-axis.

When you have **two points** $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$, you compute the slope by:

$$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

Then you find $n$ by substituting one of the points.

**General form** $Ax + By + C = 0$ is more "symmetric" — it treats $x$ and $y$ equally. Useful when the line is vertical ($x = $ constant), which the slope-intercept form can't express.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

The equation of a line underlies:

- **Linear programming**: maximize $cx$ subject to $Ax \leq b$ — linear constraints (half-spaces bounded by lines).
- **Simple linear regression**: $\hat y = \hat m x + \hat n$ fitted via least squares.
- **Kinematics**: position in rectilinear motion is $s(t) = s_0 + vt$.
- **Computer graphics**: line rasterization (Bresenham's algorithm).

### Point-line distance

For line $Ax + By + C = 0$ and point $P_0 = (x_0, y_0)$:
$$d(P_0, \text{reta}) = \frac{|A x_0 + B y_0 + C|}{\sqrt{A^2 + B^2}}$$

</Porta>
