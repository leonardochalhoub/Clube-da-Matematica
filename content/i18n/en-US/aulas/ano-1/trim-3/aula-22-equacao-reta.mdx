<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Forms of the line equation">

### Slope-intercept form

$y = mx + n$, where $m$ is the **slope** and $n$ is the **y-intercept**.

### General form

$Ax + By + C = 0$, with $(A, B) \neq (0, 0)$. For non-vertical lines ($B \neq 0$): $m = -A/B$ and $n = -C/B$.

### Point-slope form

Line through $(x_0, y_0)$ with slope $m$:
$$y - y_0 = m(x - x_0)$$

### Parametric form

Line with direction vector $\vec u = (a, b)$ through $(x_0, y_0)$:
$$\begin{cases} x = x_0 + at \\ y = y_0 + bt \end{cases}, \quad t \in \mathbb{R}$$

### Equation through two points

Line through $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$:
$$\frac{y - y_1}{x - x_1} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

### Slope from two points

$$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \tan\alpha$$

where $\alpha$ is the angle of the line with the $x$-axis.

### Table of the 5 forms

| Form | Equation | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| Slope-intercept | $y = mx + n$ | graph, $y$ explicit |
| General | $Ax + By + C = 0$ | vertical lines, symmetry |
| Point-slope | $y - y_0 = m(x - x_0)$ | point + slope given |
| Parametric | $(x_0 + at, y_0 + bt)$ | trajectory, animation |
| Intercept | $x/p + y/q = 1$ | intercepts $(p, 0), (0, q)$ |

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

Imagine a **little ramp**. It goes up or down, but always **at the same slant** — no curve. That's a **line**.

To describe the ramp, you only need to know two things: **where it starts** (intercept) and **how steep it is** (slope). Done, you can guess where the ramp is anywhere.

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

A line in the Cartesian plane is described by $y = mx + n$. The **$m$** is the slope (how much it goes up for each step in $x$). The **$n$** is where the line crosses the vertical axis.

**Concrete example**: $y = 2x + 3$. When $x = 0$, $y = 3$ — it starts at 3 on the $y$-axis. When $x$ increases by 1, $y$ increases by 2 — it rises fast.

**Real-world application**: taxi fare. Flag-drop \$5 + \$2 per km. Equation: $y = 2x + 5$. Where $y$ is the price and $x$ is the distance. Perfect line.

**Trick to find the line through 2 points**:
- Compute $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$.
- Substitute one point into $y = mx + n$ to find $n$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

The equation of a line is what connects algebra to geometry. Every line in the plane has an equation $y = mx + n$ that describes **all of its points**.

- $m$ is the **slope** (how "rising" or "falling" the line is). $m = \tan\alpha$ where $\alpha$ is the angle with the $x$-axis.
- $n$ is where the line crosses the $y$-axis.

When you have **two points** $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$, compute the slope by:

$$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

And then find $n$ by substituting one of the points.

**General form** $Ax + By + C = 0$ is more "symmetric" — it treats $x$ and $y$ equally. Useful when the line is vertical ($x = $ constant), which the slope-intercept form cannot express.

**Connection to Lesson 03**: you've seen the affine function $f(x) = ax + b$. A line is the graph of that function, with $a = m$ and $b = n$. Same thing, different language.

**Complete numerical example**: line through $(2, 3)$ and $(5, 9)$. $m = (9-3)/(5-2) = 2$. Substitute $(2, 3)$: $3 = 2 \cdot 2 + n \Rightarrow n = -1$. Equation: $y = 2x - 1$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

The equation of a line is the basis of all linear analysis. In $\mathbb{R}^n$, the equivalent is the **hyperplane** $\sum a_i x_i = b$.

### Immediate applications

- **Linear programming**: maximize $c^Tx$ subject to $Ax \leq b$ — linear constraints (half-spaces bounded by hyperplanes). Simplex traverses vertices.
- **Simple linear regression**: $\hat y = \hat m x + \hat n$ fitted via least squares. OLS estimate:
$$\hat m = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar x)(y_i - \bar y)}{\sum (x_i - \bar x)^2}$$
- **Kinematics**: position in uniform rectilinear motion is $s(t) = s_0 + vt$ — line in the $(t, s)$ plane.
- **Computer graphics**: line rasterization (Bresenham's algorithm, 1965 — IBM).
- **SVMs**: classifier finds the hyperplane of maximum margin between classes.

### Point-line distance

For line $Ax + By + C = 0$ and point $P_0 = (x_0, y_0)$:
$$d(P_0, \text{line}) = \frac{|A x_0 + B y_0 + C|}{\sqrt{A^2 + B^2}}$$

Proof via vector projection — the vector $(A, B)$ is normal to the line (perpendicular).

### Hesse normal form

$$x \cos\theta + y \sin\theta = p$$

where $p \geq 0$ is the distance from the origin to the line and $\theta$ is the angle of the normal. Useful in computational geometry.

### Connection to calculus

The tangent line to a curve $y = f(x)$ at point $(a, f(a))$ is:
$$y = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a)$$

— the local **linearization**. Newton-Raphson, Taylor series, gradient-descent optimization, all based on this idea.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

Line = 1-dimensional affine subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$. All of modern projective geometry starts here.

### Projective view

In homogeneous coordinates $(X, Y, W)$ with $x = X/W$, $y = Y/W$, the line becomes:
$$AX + BY + CW = 0$$

— a **pure linear form**. Points at infinity ($W = 0$) become accessible. Two parallel lines in the affine plane meet at a point at infinity in the projective plane. This is the basis of **OpenGL**, **PBR**, and every modern 3D transformation (rotations, translations, perspective projections).

### Duality

In the projective plane, **points and lines are dual**: every theorem about points has a version about lines, swapping "passes through" ↔ "contains". Pascal ↔ Brianchon. Crucial in algebraic geometry.

### Applications in ML / quant

- **Linear regression** generalizes to $\hat y = X\beta$. Normal system $X^TX\beta = X^Ty$ solved via QR or SVD.
- **PCA**: projections onto hyperplanes that minimize residual variance.
- **Quant finance**: Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM): $E[R_i] = R_f + \beta_i (E[R_m] - R_f)$ — line in the $(\beta, E[R])$ plane. The Markowitz frontier is the envelope; CAPM is the tangent. Fama-French extends to a hyperplane in 3+ dimensions.
- **Gradient descent**: at each step, the function is approximated by its tangent line and we move in the descent direction. Every first-order optimization is "a line with slope $-\nabla f$".

### SymPy

```python
import sympy as sp
from sympy import Line, Point
L = Line(Point(2, 3), Point(5, 9))
print(L.equation())     # 2x - y - 1
print(L.slope)          # 2
print(L.distance(Point(0, 0)))  # 1/sqrt(5)
```

### Numerical

In IEEE 754, near-vertical lines (huge $|m|$) suffer catastrophic cancellation. Use the general form $Ax + By + C = 0$ with unit $(A, B)$ for stability. Robust computational geometry (CGAL, Shewchuk's predicates) uses exact arithmetic to avoid errors.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practical application">

**Recipe: how to find a line equation in any scenario.**

| Given | Strategy |
|---|---|
| 2 points | $m = \Delta y / \Delta x$, then substitute |
| 1 point + slope | point-slope directly |
| 1 point + parallel line | same $m$, new $n$ |
| 1 point + perpendicular line | $m \to -1/m$ |
| General $\to$ slope-intercept | $y = -A/B \cdot x - C/B$ |
| Slope-intercept $\to$ general | $mx - y + n = 0$ |

### Wolfram / SymPy workflow

```python
import sympy as sp
x, y, t = sp.symbols('x y t')

# Line through two points
P, Q = sp.Point(1, 2), sp.Point(4, 8)
L = sp.Line(P, Q)
print(L.equation())   # general form

# Parametric form
print(L.arbitrary_point(t))
```

### When to be careful?

- **Vertical line** ($x = c$) has no defined $m$. Use the general form.
- **Coincident points** ($x_1 = x_2$, $y_1 = y_2$): they don't define a unique line. Input error.
- **Numerically infinite slope**: cancellation — prefer $\Delta x \cdot (y - y_0) = \Delta y \cdot (x - x_0)$.
