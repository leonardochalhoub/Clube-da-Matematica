<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Methods and theory">

### Solution methods

1. **Substitution**: isolate one variable and substitute into the other.
2. **Addition** (elimination): combine equations to eliminate a variable.
3. **Cramer**: ratio of determinants.
4. **Row reduction (Gauss)**: triangularize the matrix.

### Cramer 2x2

For $\begin{cases} a_1 x + b_1 y = c_1 \\ a_2 x + b_2 y = c_2 \end{cases}$ with $D = a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1 \neq 0$:

$$x = \frac{c_1 b_2 - c_2 b_1}{D}, \quad y = \frac{a_1 c_2 - a_2 c_1}{D}$$

### Cramer 3x3

3x3 determinant (Sarrus): $\det A = \sum$ products of the main diagonals $-$ products of the secondary diagonals.

### Classification by determinant

- $D \neq 0$: **unique solution**.
- $D = 0$ + consistent system: **infinitely many solutions** (affine subspace).
- $D = 0$ + inconsistent system: **no solution**.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

A 2x2 system is "two equations, two variables". Like:

$$\begin{cases} 2x + 3y = 12 \\ x - y = 1 \end{cases}$$

**Substitution** is the most natural way: from the second, $x = y + 1$. Substitute in the first: $2(y+1) + 3y = 12 \Rightarrow 5y = 10 \Rightarrow y = 2$. So $x = 3$.

**Check**: $2 \cdot 3 + 3 \cdot 2 = 12$ ✓, $3 - 2 = 1$ ✓.

**Geometrically**: each equation is a line. The solution is the point where the two lines meet.

When the system has **no solution**, the lines are parallel. When it has **infinitely many solutions**, the lines are identical (coincident).

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

A linear system is **the fundamental operation** of linear algebra. In $\mathbb{R}^n$:
$$A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$$

Where $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^n$. A solution exists iff $\mathbf{b} \in \text{Im}(A)$. The solution is unique iff $A$ is invertible ($\det A \neq 0$).

### Numerical methods

For large $n$, **Gaussian elimination** costs $O(n^3)$. For sparse systems, iterative methods (Jacobi, Gauss-Seidel, GMRES) can be $O(n^2)$ or better.

**LU decomposition**: $A = LU$ with $L$ lower triangular and $U$ upper triangular. Useful when several $A\mathbf{x}_i = \mathbf{b}_i$ are solved with the same $A$.

</Porta>
