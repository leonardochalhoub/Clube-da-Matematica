<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Analytic geometry in ℝ²">

<Definicao titulo="Cartesian plane">
$\mathbb{R}^2 = \{(x, y) : x, y \in \mathbb{R}\}$. Each point is an ordered pair. The $x$ (horizontal) and $y$ (vertical) axes meet at $O = (0, 0)$, dividing the plane into 4 quadrants.
</Definicao>

### Distance

For $P = (x_1, y_1)$, $Q = (x_2, y_2)$:
$$d(P, Q) = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$$

### Midpoint

$$M = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)$$

### Segment division in ratio $k$

To divide $\overline{PQ}$ in the ratio $PR/RQ = k$:
$$R = \left(\frac{x_1 + k x_2}{1 + k}, \frac{y_1 + k y_2}{1 + k}\right)$$

### Area calculation

Area of the triangle with vertices $A = (x_1, y_1)$, $B = (x_2, y_2)$, $C = (x_3, y_3)$:
$$\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} |x_1(y_2 - y_3) + x_2(y_3 - y_1) + x_3(y_1 - y_2)|$$

Equivalent to $\frac{1}{2} |\det M|$, where $M$ is the matrix of vectors $\vec{AB}, \vec{AC}$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

Cartesian plane: each point is described by 2 numbers — an $x$ coordinate (horizontal) and a $y$ coordinate (vertical).

**Distance between 2 points**: apply Pythagoras. If you have $P = (1, 2)$ and $Q = (4, 6)$:

$$d = \sqrt{(4-1)^2 + (6-2)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$$

**Midpoint**: take the average of the coordinates. Midpoint between $(1, 2)$ and $(4, 6)$ is $(2.5,\ 4)$.

**Why does it matter?** Because the Cartesian plane is the language that **connects algebra and geometry**. A line becomes an equation. A circle becomes an equation. A parabola becomes an equation. Everything that was "geometric" becomes "algebraic" — and the computer can compute.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

Analytic geometry is the foundation of everything:

- **Computer Graphics**: every pixel is a point, every 2D/3D object is a set of coordinates.
- **GPS**: positioning via coordinates + triangulation.
- **Robotics**: kinematics describes the trajectory of robotic arms.
- **Civil engineering**: topographic surveying produces coordinates.

### Distance in $\mathbb{R}^n$

Generalizes to any dimension:
$$d(P, Q) = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - y_i)^2} = \|P - Q\|_2$$

This is the **Euclidean norm**. In ML, it's the "default distance" used in k-NN, k-means, etc.

### Other metrics

- **Manhattan** ($\ell_1$): $\sum |x_i - y_i|$ — distance in city blocks.
- **Chebyshev** ($\ell_\infty$): $\max |x_i - y_i|$ — king's move in chess.
- **Geodesic metrics**: on a sphere (Earth), distance follows great circles.

</Porta>
