<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Canonical equations">

### Ellipse

The sum of distances to 2 foci is constant:
$$\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$$

Where $a > b > 0$. Foci at $(\pm c, 0)$ with $c = \sqrt{a^2 - b^2}$. Major axis $= 2a$, minor $= 2b$. Eccentricity $e = c/a \in [0, 1)$. When $a = b$, it's a circle.

### Parabola

Distance to focus $=$ distance to directrix:
$$y^2 = 4px$$

Focus at $(p, 0)$, directrix $x = -p$.

### Hyperbola

The difference of distances to 2 foci is constant:
$$\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$$

Foci at $(\pm c, 0)$ with $c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$. Eccentricity $e = c/a > 1$. Asymptotes $y = \pm (b/a) x$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

The **conics** are the 3 curves that appear when cutting a cone with a plane:

1. **Ellipse** — oblique cut (including the circle, when $a = b$).
2. **Parabola** — cut parallel to a generator.
3. **Hyperbola** — cut that hits both halves of the double cone.

**Where do they appear in nature?**
- Planetary orbits are **ellipses** (Kepler, 1609 — derived by Newton).
- Ballistic trajectories are **parabolas** (Galileo).
- Satellite dishes and headlights: parabola shape to concentrate/distribute light/signal.
- Hyperbola appears in sonic shock waves, in some GPS routes.

**Eccentricity $e$** measures how "flattened" the conic is:
- $e = 0$: circle
- $0 < e < 1$: ellipse
- $e = 1$: parabola
- $e > 1$: hyperbola

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

### Applications in orbits (Kepler-Newton)

For a gravitational orbit around a central body:
- Energy $E < 0$: **ellipse** (planet).
- $E = 0$: **parabola** (escape comet).
- $E > 0$: **hyperbola** (passing asteroid, space probes).

Earth's eccentricity: $e \approx 0{,}0167$ (nearly circular).

### Parabolic antenna

Reflects a ray parallel to the axis directly to the focus. Equation: $y^2 = 4px$. Focus at distance $p$ from the vertex. **Satellite TV, reflecting telescope, GPS** — all use the parabola.

### Hyperbola in hyperbolic GPS (LORAN)

The time difference between two signals defines a hyperbola; intersection of 3+ hyperbolas locates the point. It was the dominant system before GPS.

</Porta>
