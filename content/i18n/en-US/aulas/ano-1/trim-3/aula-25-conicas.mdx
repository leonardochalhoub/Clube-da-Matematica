<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Canonical equations">

### Ellipse

Sum of distances to 2 foci is constant:
$$\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$$

Where $a > b > 0$. Foci at $(\pm c, 0)$ with $c = \sqrt{a^2 - b^2}$. Major axis $= 2a$, minor $= 2b$. Eccentricity $e = c/a \in [0, 1)$. When $a = b$, it is a circle.

### Parabola

Distance to focus $=$ distance to directrix:
$$y^2 = 4px$$

Focus at $(p, 0)$, directrix $x = -p$.

### Hyperbola

Difference of distances to 2 foci is constant:
$$\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$$

Foci at $(\pm c, 0)$ with $c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$. Eccentricity $e = c/a > 1$. Asymptotes $y = \pm (b/a) x$.

### Table of the three conics

| Conic | Canonical equation | Eccentricity | Focus(es) | Definition |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Circle | $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$ | $e = 0$ | center | dist. to center $= r$ |
| Ellipse | $x^2/a^2 + y^2/b^2 = 1$ | $0 < e < 1$ | $(\pm c, 0)$ | $r_1 + r_2 = 2a$ |
| Parabola | $y^2 = 4px$ | $e = 1$ | $(p, 0)$ | $r = $ dist. to directrix |
| Hyperbola | $x^2/a^2 - y^2/b^2 = 1$ | $e > 1$ | $(\pm c, 0)$ | $\|r_1 - r_2\| = 2a$ |

### General form of a conic

$$Ax^2 + Bxy + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$$

Discriminant $\Delta = B^2 - 4AC$:
- $\Delta < 0$: ellipse (or circle if $A=C, B=0$).
- $\Delta = 0$: parabola.
- $\Delta > 0$: hyperbola.

Take a **lantern** and point it at the floor. The light makes a **circle** of light!

Now **tilt the lantern** a little. The circle becomes **oval** — an **ellipse**.

Tilt it **a bit more**: the light starts to "stretch" forever — a **parabola**.

Tilt it **a lot**: the light breaks into **two curves** that fly apart — a **hyperbola**.

Those are the four **conics**!

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

The **conics** are curves that appear when you cut a cone with a plane. There are 4 types:

1. **Circle**: horizontal cut.
2. **Ellipse**: oblique cut (egg shape).
3. **Parabola**: cut parallel to a generator of the cone.
4. **Hyperbola**: vertical cut, hitting both sides of the double cone.

**Where they show up in life**:
- **Earth's orbit around the Sun**: ellipse (almost a circle, but not perfect).
- **Free throw in basketball**: parabola.
- **Satellite TV dish**: parabola — concentrates the entire signal at a point (focus).
- **Sound of a supersonic plane**: the shock wave has a hyperbolic cross-section.

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

The **conics** are the 3 curves that appear when cutting a cone with a plane:

1. **Ellipse** — oblique cut (including circle, when $a = b$).
2. **Parabola** — cut parallel to a generator.
3. **Hyperbola** — cut hitting both halves of the double cone.

**Where do they appear in nature?**
- Planetary orbits are **ellipses** (Kepler, 1609 — derived by Newton).
- Ballistic trajectory is a **parabola** (Galileo).
- Parabolic antennas and headlights: parabola shape to concentrate/distribute light/signal.
- The hyperbola appears in sonic shock waves, in the routes of comets with positive energy.

**Eccentricity $e$** measures how "flattened" the conic is:
- $e = 0$: circle.
- $0 < e < 1$: ellipse.
- $e = 1$: parabola.
- $e > 1$: hyperbola.

**Unified definition (focus-directrix)**: a conic is the locus of points $P$ such that $|PF|/d(P, \ell) = e$ is constant.

**Numerical example**: ellipse $x^2/25 + y^2/9 = 1$. $a = 5, b = 3, c = \sqrt{25 - 9} = 4$. Foci at $(\pm 4, 0)$. Eccentricity $e = 4/5 = 0.8$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

Conics are **solutions of Kepler's equation** ($1/r^2$ gravitation) and appear in nearly every applied area.

### Applications in orbits (Kepler-Newton)

For a gravitational orbit around a central body, the mechanical energy $E$ determines the type:

| Energy | Eccentricity | Conic | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| $E < 0$ | $0 \leq e < 1$ | ellipse | planet, artificial moon |
| $E = 0$ | $e = 1$ | parabola | escape comet |
| $E > 0$ | $e > 1$ | hyperbola | interstellar probe (Voyager) |

Eccentricities:
- Earth: $e \approx 0.0167$ (almost circular).
- Mars: $e \approx 0.0934$.
- Halley: $e \approx 0.967$ (very elongated ellipse).
- 1I/'Oumuamua (interstellar 2017): $e \approx 1.2$ (hyperbola — escaped the Sun).

### Parabolic antenna

Reflects a ray parallel to the axis directly to the focus. Equation: $y^2 = 4px$. Focus at distance $p$ from the vertex. **Satellite TV, reflecting telescopes, GPS** — all use parabolas. Hertz patent 1888.

### Hyperbola in LORAN/hyperbolic GPS

The time difference between two signals defines a hyperbola; the intersection of 3+ hyperbolas locates the point. It was the dominant system before GPS. Still used as a military backup.

### Derivation of the canonical ellipse equation

Let $F_1 = (-c, 0)$ and $F_2 = (c, 0)$. Sum of distances $= 2a$:
$$\sqrt{(x+c)^2 + y^2} + \sqrt{(x-c)^2 + y^2} = 2a$$

Isolating one root and squaring twice:
$$\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{a^2 - c^2} = 1$$

Setting $b^2 = a^2 - c^2$, we get $x^2/a^2 + y^2/b^2 = 1$. QED.

### Whisper gallery

In an elliptical room, sound emitted at focus $F_1$ converges to $F_2$. The U.S. Capitol has this property.

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

Conics are **algebraic curves of degree 2** — the simplest non-trivial case in algebraic geometry.

### Projective view

In homogeneous coordinates $(X, Y, W)$, every conic becomes:
$$\sum a_{ij} X_i X_j = 0$$

— a quadratic form. Classification by **matrix signature** (Sylvester):
- $(2, 0)$ or $(0, 2)$: ellipse / circle.
- $(1, 1)$: hyperbola.
- Degenerate: parabola.

In the projective plane, **all non-degenerate conics are equivalent** (under projective coordinate change). The difference between ellipse, parabola, hyperbola is in **how they intersect the line at infinity** — 0, 1, or 2 points respectively.

### Applications in ML / quant finance

- **Quadratic optimization**: contours of a quadratic function are ellipses (positive definite), hyperbolas (saddle), or degenerate (singular). Newton's method in practice deals with ellipses in parameter space.
- **Quant**: volatility surfaces (volatility smile/skew) are often modeled by conics in strike-vol. Heston ↦ ellipses in the $(\rho, \sigma)$ plane.
- **Kalman filter**: the covariance is an uncertainty ellipse; the Bayesian update produces a new ellipse.
- **Markowitz frontier**: hyperbola in the risk-return plane (mean-variance). The tangent is the CAPM.

### Hamilton-Jacobi equation and confocal conics

Liouville-integrable system: conics with the same focus form orthogonal (elliptic) coordinates. Crucial in classical mechanics for problems with axial symmetry. Jacobi 1838.

### Curiosity

Apollonius of Perga (c. 200 BCE) wrote *Conics* in 8 books — 7 survived. Practically all modern terminology (ellipse, parabola, hyperbola, focus) comes from him. The [Nobel Prize](https://nobelprize.org) winners in physics since Kepler implicitly use his ideas.

import sympy as sp

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practical application">

**Recipe: identify and analyze a conic.**

1. **Look at the signs** of the $x^2$ and $y^2$ terms:
   - Same sign, equal coefficients → circle.
   - Same sign, different coefficients → ellipse.
   - Opposite signs → hyperbola.
   - Only one quadratic → parabola.
2. **Center** (if ellipse/hyperbola): complete the squares.
3. **Compute $a, b, c$**: $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$ (ellipse), $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$ (hyperbola).
4. **Eccentricity** $e = c/a$.

### Workflow for a typical problem

```python
import sympy as sp
from sympy.geometry import Ellipse, Point, Line

# Given vertices and focus, find the ellipse
E = Ellipse(center=Point(0,0), hradius=5, vradius=3)
print(E.foci)             # foci
print(E.eccentricity)     # 4/5
print(E.equation())       # canonical form

# Tangent at a point
P = Point(3, 0)  # vertex
print(E.tangent_lines(P))
```

### When to be careful?

- **Rotated conic** ($Bxy \neq 0$): you need an axis change. Angle $\tan(2\theta) = B/(A - C)$.
- **Degenerate conic**: 2 lines (concurrent, parallel, or coincident), or just a point, or empty. Happens when the determinant $= 0$.
- **CG application**: parabolas and ellipses are interpolated via NURBS. Beware of Bezier approximation.
