<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Vectors in mechanics">

### Fundamental principles

1. **Static equilibrium**: $\sum \vec F_i = \vec 0$ (object at rest or in uniform rectilinear motion).
2. **Newton's 2nd law**: $\sum \vec F_i = m \vec a$.
3. **Decomposition**: for a ramp of inclination $\theta$, gravity $\vec g = -g\hat\jmath$ projects to $-g\sin\theta$ parallel (downhill) and $-g\cos\theta$ normal.

### Work and energy

- Work: $W = \vec F \cdot \vec d$.
- Power: $P = \vec F \cdot \vec v$.
- Kinetic energy: $K = \frac{1}{2} m |\vec v|^2$.
- Work-energy theorem: $W_{\text{total}} = \Delta K$.

### Vector kinematics

- Position: $\vec r(t)$.
- Velocity: $\vec v(t) = d\vec r/dt$.
- Acceleration: $\vec a(t) = d\vec v/dt$.

For uniformly accelerated motion:
$$\vec r(t) = \vec r_0 + \vec v_0 t + \frac{1}{2} \vec a t^2$$

### Classical forces

| Force | Expression | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Weight | $\vec P = m\vec g$ | always vertical, downward |
| Normal | $\vec N \perp$ surface | reaction to compression |
| Friction | $f = \mu N$ | opposes motion |
| Tension | $T$ along the cable | equal at both ends (ideal cable) |
| Spring | $\vec F = -k\vec x$ | Hooke |
| Centripetal | $F_c = mv^2/r$ | toward the center |

### Equilibrium in 2D

3 independent equations: $\sum F_x = 0$, $\sum F_y = 0$, $\sum M_O = 0$ (moment about any point $O$).

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

Imagine two boys pulling a **box**. One pulls **to the right**, the other **to the left**, with the same force. **The box doesn't move** — they cancel out.

Now one pulls **upward** and the other **sideways**. The box goes on a **diagonal**, because the two forces **add** like little arrows.

When all the little arrows **add to zero**, the box stays **still**. That's **equilibrium**!

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

In physics, **every force has a direction**. That's why it's a vector.

**Newton** discovered: $\vec F = m\vec a$. The greater the force, the greater the acceleration. The greater the mass, the harder to accelerate.

**Equilibrium**: when all forces cancel (sum to zero), the object stays still or moves in a straight line at constant velocity.

**Ramp trick**: gravity pulls downward, but only **part** of it makes the object slide along the ramp. The other part just presses against the ramp. For a 30° ramp, $\sin 30° = 0.5$ — only half of gravity pulls the object down the ramp.

**In real life**: bridge cables, elevator tension, car braking, basketball shots — all are vectors adding up.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

In physics, **any quantity with direction is a vector**: force, velocity, acceleration, displacement. And vector addition **respects direction**: 50N to the right + 50N to the left = 0N (zero, they cancel).

**Equilibrium**: object at rest $\Rightarrow$ all forces cancel.

**Newton**: net force = mass × acceleration. More force → more acceleration. More mass → harder to accelerate.

**Decomposition** is the most useful trick: on a 30° ramp, gravity $g$ splits into $g \cos 30°$ pressing on the ramp (normal) + $g \sin 30°$ pulling down the ramp (parallel). It is this parallel part that makes the object slide.

**Projectile motion**: $\vec v_0 = (v_0\cos\theta, v_0\sin\theta)$. Acceleration $\vec a = (0, -g)$. Parabolic trajectory:
- Time of flight: $t = 2v_0\sin\theta / g$.
- Range: $R = v_0^2 \sin(2\theta)/g$ — maximum at $\theta = 45°$.
- Maximum height: $h = v_0^2\sin^2\theta/(2g)$.

**Connection to Lesson 27**: work $W = \vec F \cdot \vec d$ uses the dot product.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

Vector mechanics is the basis of statics (Applied Mechanics I) and dynamics (Applied Mechanics II) in engineering. Every analysis of:

- **Trusses**: equilibrium at each node (coplanar force systems).
- **Beams**: distribution of moment and shear force (vectors along the length).
- **Machines**: mechanism analysis, kinematic pairs.
- **Vehicles**: longitudinal and lateral dynamics (braking, cornering).
- **Structures**: buildings, bridges, planes, ships — all solved via $\sum \vec F = 0$ + $\sum M = 0$.

### Vector equilibrium analysis

In 2D problems: 2 equations ($\sum F_x = 0, \sum F_y = 0$) + 1 moment ($\sum M = 0$). In 3D: 6 equations.

### Full inclined plane

Block of mass $m$ on a $\theta$ ramp with friction $\mu$:

| Force | Parallel component | Normal component |
|---|---|---|
| Weight | $-mg\sin\theta$ | $-mg\cos\theta$ |
| Normal | 0 | $+N$ |
| Friction | $\pm \mu N$ | 0 |

Normal equilibrium: $N = mg\cos\theta$. So maximum friction is $\mu mg\cos\theta$.

Block slides $\iff mg\sin\theta > \mu_s mg\cos\theta \iff \tan\theta > \mu_s$.

### Projectile motion

$\vec r(t) = (v_0\cos\theta \cdot t, v_0\sin\theta \cdot t - \frac{1}{2}g t^2)$. Cartesian equation:
$$y = x\tan\theta - \frac{g x^2}{2 v_0^2 \cos^2\theta}$$

— **parabola** (Lesson 25).

### Circular motion

Centripetal acceleration: $\vec a_c = -\omega^2 \vec r$. Magnitude $a_c = v^2/r$. Centripetal force must be supplied (rope, friction, gravity).

### Cables and tensions

Ideal cable (zero mass, inextensible): tension is the same along its length. Ideal pulley: changes direction without changing magnitude.

### Connection to the [Nobel Prize](https://nobelprize.org)

Vector mechanics is the foundation for orbital mechanics (Newton 1687) and relativity (Einstein 1916). Detection of gravitational waves (LIGO, [Nobel 2017](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/2017/)) uses vector decomposition of stress-energy tensors.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

Classical mechanics in vector form is a special case of **Lagrangian / Hamiltonian mechanics** — formalism invariant under coordinate change.

### Lagrangian view

Lagrangian $L(q, \dot q, t) = T - V$ (kinetic minus potential energy). Euler-Lagrange equations:
$$\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q_i} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_i} = 0$$

Newton in Cartesian coordinates is a special case. In curvilinear coordinates (polar, spherical, cylindrical), Newton becomes ugly; Lagrange is trivial.

### Applications in engineering / quant

- **Robotics**: forward/inverse kinematics of arms is pure vector algebra. Quaternions avoid gimbal lock.
- **Aerospace**: five forces on a rocket (gravity, thrust, drag, lift, lateral). 3D vector analysis.
- **Quant**: derivatives in multivariate (multidimensional Black-Scholes-Merton) use vector algebra. Greeks (delta, gamma) are vectors / Jacobian matrices.
- **CFD**: Navier-Stokes equations are vector. Discretization via finite volumes is vector algebra on a mesh.

### Many-particle systems

For $N$ particles, phase space has dimension $6N$. In statistical mechanics, integrals of vector functions over this space define temperature, pressure, etc.

### Numerical

ODE integrators for mechanics:
- **Explicit Euler**: $\vec r_{n+1} = \vec r_n + \vec v_n \Delta t$. Simple but unstable.
- **Verlet/Leapfrog**: symplectic, conserve energy in Hamiltonian systems.
- **Runge-Kutta 4**: high order, but not symplectic.

For planet simulation, games, protein folding: Verlet > Euler.

### NumPy / SciPy

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.integrate import solve_ivp

def projectile(t, y):
    g = 9.81
    return [y[2], y[3], 0, -g]   # dx/dt, dy/dt, dvx/dt, dvy/dt

v0, theta = 50, np.radians(60)
y0 = [0, 0, v0*np.cos(theta), v0*np.sin(theta)]
sol = solve_ivp(projectile, [0, 10], y0, dense_output=True, rtol=1e-9)
```

### Curiosity

Newton published *Principia* in 1687 without using modern vector notation — every formulation is geometric. Modern vector algebra came with Gibbs/Heaviside at the end of the 19th century. **Vector reformulation of the Principia** is a classic graduate didactic exercise.

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practical application">

**Recipe: solve a mechanics problem in 5 steps.**

1. **Free-body diagram (FBD)**: draw the object and all the forces on it.
2. **Choose axes**: align with the surface (ramp) or with the motion; simplifies decompositions.
3. **Decompose every force** along the axes.
4. **Apply** $\sum F_x = ma_x$, $\sum F_y = ma_y$ (and moment if applicable).
5. **Solve the system**.

### Wolfram / NumPy workflow

```python
import numpy as np

# Block on ramp with friction
m, theta, mu, g = 5, np.radians(30), 0.2, 9.81
F_grav_par = -m*g*np.sin(theta)
F_normal   =  m*g*np.cos(theta)
F_friction = -mu*F_normal*np.sign(F_grav_par)  # opposes motion
F_parallel_total = F_grav_par + F_friction
a = F_parallel_total / m
print(f"a = {a:.2f} m/s²")
```

### When to be careful?

- **Static vs kinetic friction**: distinct coefficients; static until motion begins, kinetic after.
- **Non-inertial frame**: in the frame of an accelerating vehicle, a "fictitious" force $-m\vec a_0$ appears.
- **Stretching cable**: tension varies along it. Non-ideal.
- **Sign**: axis orientation can flip signs. Always be consistent with the FBD.
