<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Vectors in mechanics">

### Fundamental principles

1. **Static equilibrium**: $\sum \vec F_i = \vec 0$ (object at rest or in uniform rectilinear motion).
2. **Newton's 2nd law**: $\sum \vec F_i = m \vec a$.
3. **Decomposition**: for a ramp with inclination $\theta$, gravity $\vec g = -g\hat\jmath$ projects into $-g\sin\theta$ parallel (down the ramp) and $-g\cos\theta$ normal.

### Work and energy

- Work: $W = \vec F \cdot \vec d$.
- Power: $P = \vec F \cdot \vec v$.

### Vector kinematics

- Position: $\vec r(t)$.
- Velocity: $\vec v(t) = d\vec r/dt$.
- Acceleration: $\vec a(t) = d\vec v/dt$.

For uniformly accelerated motion:
$$\vec r(t) = \vec r_0 + \vec v_0 t + \frac{1}{2} \vec a t^2$$

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

In physics, **every quantity with direction is a vector**: force, velocity, acceleration, displacement. And vector addition **respects direction**: 50N to the right + 50N to the left = 0N (zero, they cancel).

**Equilibrium**: object at rest ⟹ all forces cancel.

**Newton**: resultant force = mass × acceleration. More force → more acceleration. More mass → harder to accelerate.

**Decomposition** is the most useful trick: on a 30° ramp, gravity $g$ splits into $g \cos 30°$ pressing on the ramp (normal) + $g \sin 30°$ pulling down along the ramp (parallel). It's that parallel part that makes the object slide.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

Vector mechanics is the foundation of statics (Applied Mechanics I) and dynamics (Applied Mechanics II) in engineering. Every analysis of:

- **Trusses**: equilibrium at each node (coplanar force systems).
- **Beams**: distribution of bending moment and shear force (vectors along the length).
- **Machines**: mechanism analysis, kinematic pair.
- **Vehicles**: longitudinal and lateral dynamics (braking, cornering).

### Vector analysis of equilibrium

In 2D problems: 2 equations ($\sum F_x = 0, \sum F_y = 0$) + 1 moment ($\sum M = 0$). In 3D: 6 equations.

</Porta>
