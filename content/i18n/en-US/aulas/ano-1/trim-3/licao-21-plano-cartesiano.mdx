<Definicao titulo="Cartesian plane">
$\mathbb{R}^2 = \{(x, y) : x, y \in \mathbb{R}\}$. Each point is an ordered pair. The $x$ (horizontal) and $y$ (vertical) axes meet at $O = (0, 0)$, dividing the plane into 4 quadrants.
</Definicao>

### Distance

For $P = (x_1, y_1)$, $Q = (x_2, y_2)$:
$$d(P, Q) = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$$

### Midpoint

$$M = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)$$

### Segment division in ratio $k$

To divide $\overline{PQ}$ in the ratio $PR/RQ = k$:
$$R = \left(\frac{x_1 + k x_2}{1 + k}, \frac{y_1 + k y_2}{1 + k}\right)$$

### Area calculation

Area of the triangle with vertices $A = (x_1, y_1)$, $B = (x_2, y_2)$, $C = (x_3, y_3)$:
$$\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} |x_1(y_2 - y_3) + x_2(y_3 - y_1) + x_3(y_1 - y_2)|$$

Equivalent to $\frac{1}{2} |\det M|$, where $M$ is the matrix of vectors $\vec{AB}, \vec{AC}$.

### Properties table

| Concept | Formula | Origin |
|---|---|---|
| Distance | $\sqrt{\Delta x^2 + \Delta y^2}$ | Pythagoras |
| Midpoint | $((x_1+x_2)/2, (y_1+y_2)/2)$ | arithmetic mean |
| Centroid | $((x_1+x_2+x_3)/3, ...)$ | mean of 3 vertices |
| Triangle area | $\frac{1}{2}\|x_1(y_2-y_3)+\ldots\|$ | determinant |
| Collinearity | area $= 0$ | det $= 0$ |

Imagine a **treasure hunt** on the living-room floor. You say: "walk 3 steps to the right and 4 steps forward". Done, you found the treasure! Those two numbers are the **coordinates** of the treasure.

To find out how far the chicken needs to **walk in a straight line** from the corner of the room to the treasure, just think of a **triangle**: 3 to the right, 4 forward, and diagonally it's... 5 steps! That's the **distance**.

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

The **Cartesian plane** is like infinite graph paper. Each little point has two numbers — how far to the right ($x$) and how far up ($y$).

**Distance between two points**: use the **Pythagorean theorem**. Imagine drawing a right triangle with the two points as opposite vertices. The legs are the difference in $x$ and the difference in $y$. The hypotenuse is the distance!

**Example**: from point $(1, 1)$ to point $(4, 5)$. Difference in $x$ is 3. Difference in $y$ is 4. Distance $= \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$.

**Midpoint**: the point in the middle between two points. Just **average** the coordinates. Midpoint between $(2, 4)$ and $(6, 10)$ is $(4, 7)$.

**Where it's used**: GPS, game maps, robots that walk on the floor, Wi-Fi coverage.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

Cartesian plane: each point is described by 2 numbers — an $x$ coordinate (horizontal) and a $y$ coordinate (vertical). **Descartes** unified algebra and geometry in 1637 — hence the adjective "Cartesian".

**Distance between 2 points**: apply Pythagoras. If you have $P = (1, 2)$ and $Q = (4, 6)$:

$$d = \sqrt{(4-1)^2 + (6-2)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$$

**Midpoint**: average the coordinates. Midpoint between $(1, 2)$ and $(4, 6)$ is $(2.5,\ 4)$.

**Centroid of a triangle**: average of the 3 vertices. For $A, B, C$: $G = \frac{A + B + C}{3}$ — coordinate by coordinate.

**Why does it matter?** Because the Cartesian plane is the language that **connects algebra and geometry**. A line becomes an equation. A circle becomes an equation. A parabola becomes an equation. Everything that was "geometric" becomes "algebraic" — and the computer can compute it.

**Physics connection**: in 2D kinematics, the position of a body is a pair $(x(t), y(t))$. The trajectory is the set of these points.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

Analytic geometry is the foundation of practically all modern applied mathematics.

### Generalization

Distance in $\mathbb{R}^n$:
$$d(P, Q) = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - y_i)^2} = \|P - Q\|_2$$

This is the **Euclidean norm** ($\ell_2$). In ML, it's the "default distance" used in k-NN, k-means, t-SNE, neural networks (MSE loss).

### Other metrics

| Metric | Formula | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Manhattan ($\ell_1$) | $\sum \|x_i - y_i\|$ | city blocks, robustness to outliers |
| Chebyshev ($\ell_\infty$) | $\max \|x_i - y_i\|$ | chess, planning |
| Mahalanobis | $\sqrt{(x-\mu)^T \Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)}$ | multivariate correlation |
| Geodesic | great circles | distance on a sphere |
| Cosine | $1 - \frac{\vec u \cdot \vec v}{\|u\|\|v\|}$ | NLP, recommendation |

### Signed (oriented) areas

The formula $\frac{1}{2}(x_1(y_2 - y_3) + \ldots)$ **without the absolute value** gives **signed** area — positive if $A, B, C$ are counterclockwise, negative otherwise. Crucial in CG (back-face culling) and algorithms like ConvexHull (Graham scan).

### Cross-domain

- **Computer Graphics**: each pixel is a point, each 2D/3D object is a set of coordinates. Ray tracing operates in $\mathbb{R}^3$.
- **GPS**: positioning via coordinates + trilateration with 4 satellites.
- **Robotics**: forward/inverse kinematics of robotic arms via composition of transformations.
- **Civil engineering**: topographic surveying produces UTM coordinates (a Cartesian variant).
- **Statistics**: a scatter plot is literally a Cartesian plane, and linear regression seeks the best-fit line.

### Demonstration: Pythagoras → distance

Construct a right triangle with horizontal leg $|x_2 - x_1|$ and vertical leg $|y_2 - y_1|$. The hypotenuse is the segment $\overline{PQ}$. By Pythagoras: $d^2 = (x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2$. QED.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

The "distance formula" is just the **norm induced by the canonical inner product** on $\mathbb{R}^n$.

### Functional-analytic view

We define $\langle u, v \rangle = \sum u_i v_i$. The norm $\|x\| = \sqrt{\langle x, x \rangle}$ satisfies:

1. **Positivity**: $\|x\| \geq 0$, $= 0 \iff x = 0$.
2. **Homogeneity**: $\|\alpha x\| = |\alpha| \|x\|$.
3. **Triangle inequality**: $\|x + y\| \leq \|x\| + \|y\|$ (Cauchy-Schwarz).

The **metric** $d(x, y) = \|x - y\|$ inherits these properties. $(\mathbb{R}^n, d)$ is a **complete metric space** — every Cauchy sequence converges.

### Generalization: inner product spaces

In any Hilbert space $H$ (e.g., $L^2(\Omega)$ — square-integrable functions), the "distance" between $f$ and $g$ is $\|f - g\|_2 = \sqrt{\int |f - g|^2}$. **Same formalism** as $\mathbb{R}^n$ — signal engineering, quantum mechanics, etc.

### Applications in ML / quant finance

- **Embeddings**: GPT/Claude represent tokens as vectors in $\mathbb{R}^{4096}$ or $\mathbb{R}^{12288}$. "Semantic similarity" $=$ Euclidean or cosine distance.
- **Clustering**: k-means converges to local minima of the sum of squared distances. Still the workhorse algorithm.
- **Quant finance**: distance between return series defines correlation; hierarchical clustering (Mantegna 1999) uses $\sqrt{2(1-\rho)}$ as a metric between assets.
- **Gauss-Bonnet**: on manifolds, the local metric is a tensor; geodesics generalize "straight lines".

### Historical note

Descartes published *La Géométrie* as an appendix to *Discours de la méthode* in 1637. Fermat developed parallel ideas independently. The modern $(x, y)$ notation came later — Descartes used the final letters of the alphabet for variables (hence $x, y, z$).

### Wolfram / SymPy

```python
import sympy as sp
P = sp.Point(1, 2)
Q = sp.Point(4, 6)
print(P.distance(Q))   # 5
print(P.midpoint(Q))   # Point2D(5/2, 4)
```

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practical application">

**Recipe: how to solve ANY basic analytic geometry problem.**

1. **Draw the coordinate system** (even roughly). Mark the points.
2. **Identify what's being asked**: distance? midpoint? area? locus?
3. **Apply the corresponding formula** without skipping steps.
4. **Verify with geometry** (does the order of magnitude make sense?).

### Wolfram Alpha / SymPy workflow

```python
import sympy as sp
from sympy import Point, Segment, Triangle

A, B, C = Point(0,0), Point(4,0), Point(0,3)
T = Triangle(A, B, C)
print(T.area)          # 6
print(T.centroid)      # Point2D(4/3, 1)
print(T.perimeter)     # 12
```

Use Wolfram Alpha for visuals: `distance from (1,2) to (4,6)` produces the step-by-step formula.

### When to be careful?

- **Confusing coordinates**: watch out for the sign of $\Delta y$ — the formula squares it, but the geometric interpretation does not.
- **Coincident points**: $d(P, P) = 0$. Trivial but a frequent input error.
- **Different scales on axes**: if $x$ is in km and $y$ in m, you must normalize before computing "geometric" distance.
- **Non-Euclidean metric**: in GPS over the spherical Earth, straight-line distance on the map is NOT the actual distance — use Haversine.
