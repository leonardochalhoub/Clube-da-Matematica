<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Vectors in ℝ²">

<Definicao titulo="Vector">
$\vec v \in \mathbb{R}^2$ is an ordered pair $(v_1, v_2)$. Geometrically, it represents a **displacement** or **oriented direction**.
</Definicao>

### Operations

- **Sum**: $\vec u + \vec v = (u_1 + v_1, u_2 + v_2)$.
- **Scalar**: $\alpha \vec v = (\alpha v_1, \alpha v_2)$.
- **Subtraction**: $\vec u - \vec v = (u_1 - v_1, u_2 - v_2)$.

### Magnitude (norm)

$$|\vec v| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2}$$

### Unit vector

$\hat v = \vec v / |\vec v|$ has magnitude 1. **Versor**.

### Canonical vectors

$\hat\imath = (1, 0)$, $\hat\jmath = (0, 1)$. Every vector: $\vec v = v_1 \hat\imath + v_2 \hat\jmath$.

### Polar form

$\vec v = (|\vec v|\cos\theta, |\vec v|\sin\theta)$ where $\theta$ is the angle with the positive $x$-axis.

### Properties (8 vector-space axioms)

| Property | Expression |
|---|---|
| Commutative | $\vec u + \vec v = \vec v + \vec u$ |
| Associative | $(\vec u + \vec v) + \vec w = \vec u + (\vec v + \vec w)$ |
| Identity | $\vec v + \vec 0 = \vec v$ |
| Inverse | $\vec v + (-\vec v) = \vec 0$ |
| Distributive (scalar/vector) | $\alpha(\vec u + \vec v) = \alpha\vec u + \alpha\vec v$ |
| Distributive (vector/scalar) | $(\alpha + \beta)\vec v = \alpha\vec v + \beta\vec v$ |
| Compatibility | $\alpha(\beta\vec v) = (\alpha\beta)\vec v$ |
| Scalar identity | $1 \cdot \vec v = \vec v$ |

These 8 properties characterize a **vector space** — seen formally in linear algebra (Term 12).

### Parallelogram rule

$\vec u + \vec v$ is the diagonal of the parallelogram formed by $\vec u$ and $\vec v$.

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

A vector is an "**arrow**" in the plane. The arrow $(3, 4)$ goes 3 to the right and 4 up. **Its length**: apply Pythagoras → $\sqrt{9 + 16} = 5$.

**Vector sum**: stack the arrows, "**tip to head**". In coordinates, add component by component: $(2, 1) + (3, 4) = (5, 5)$.

**Multiply by scalar**: stretches or contracts the arrow. $3 \cdot (2, 1) = (6, 3)$.

**Where it shows up**:
- **Wind speed**: 50 km/h east is a vector.
- **Boxing punch**: force has direction.
- **GPS**: position is a vector (latitude, longitude).
- **Joystick**: up and right at the same time = diagonal vector.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

A vector is "an arrow" in the plane. It has:
- **Magnitude** (length of the arrow)
- **Direction** (the line on which it lies)
- **Orientation** (which end of the line)

In coordinates, it's just a pair $(a, b)$. The pair $(3, 4)$ is an arrow that goes 3 to the right and 4 up. Magnitude: $\sqrt{9 + 16} = 5$.

**Sum** is placing one arrow at the tip of the other. $(2,1) + (3,4) = (5, 5)$ — added component by component.

**Scalar multiplication**: stretches or contracts. $3 \cdot (2, 1) = (6, 3)$ — three times bigger.

**Versor (unit vector)**: divide by the magnitude. For $(3, 4)$, magnitude 5, the versor is $(3/5, 4/5)$. **Direction** preserved and **magnitude 1**.

**Where it appears**: everything in physics that has direction is a vector. Velocity. Force. Acceleration. Electric field. Angular momentum. In games, character positions; in ML, word embeddings are vectors of 100+ dimensions.

**Connection to previous lessons**:
- Distance $|\vec u - \vec v|$ generalizes the distance between points.
- Direction vector of a line: $(x_2 - x_1, y_2 - y_1)$ parametric.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

A vector is the most important object of all applied mathematics. In $\mathbb{R}^n$:

### Cross-domain applications

- **Mechanics**: force is a vector, sum of forces is the resultant (parallelogram rule). Equilibrium: $\sum \vec F = \vec 0$.
- **Kinematics**: velocity $\vec v$ and acceleration $\vec a$ are vectors; $\vec v(t) = \vec v_0 + \vec a t$ (vector composition).
- **Statics**: equilibrium = $\sum \vec F = \vec 0$ — vector system of equations.
- **Electromag**: electric/magnetic field; Lorentz force $\vec F = q\vec E + q\vec v \times \vec B$.
- **Signals**: an audio/image sample is a vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$, with huge $n$.
- **Computer Graphics**: object pose = position (3D) + orientation (3D) = 6D vector.

### Decomposition

Every vector in $\mathbb{R}^2$ decomposes **uniquely** into components parallel and perpendicular to a given direction — the basis for resolving forces on inclined planes. Generalizes to projection onto any subspace (Lesson 27).

### Linear combination and basis

$\hat\imath, \hat\jmath$ form a **canonical basis** of $\mathbb{R}^2$. Every $\vec v = v_1 \hat\imath + v_2 \hat\jmath$. More generally: two **linearly independent** vectors $\vec a, \vec b$ form a basis; every vector is $\alpha\vec a + \beta\vec b$.

Vectors are **linearly dependent** if $\alpha\vec a + \beta\vec b = \vec 0$ has a non-trivial solution. In $\mathbb{R}^2$: dependent $\iff$ parallel $\iff \det\begin{pmatrix} a_1 & b_1 \\ a_2 & b_2 \end{pmatrix} = 0$.

### Norm and triangle inequality

$$|\vec u + \vec v| \leq |\vec u| + |\vec v|$$

Equality $\iff$ vectors are parallel with the same orientation. Proof via Cauchy-Schwarz (Lesson 27).

### Polar coordinates

$\vec v = (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$ with $r = |\vec v|$ and $\theta = \arctan(v_2/v_1)$ (watch the quadrant — use `atan2`).

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

$\mathbb{R}^2$ with vector addition and scalar multiplication is the simplest example of a **real vector space** — a fundamental category of linear algebra.

### Abstract view

Vector space $V$ over field $\mathbb{F}$: set with addition and scalar multiplication satisfying 8 axioms. Important examples:

- $\mathbb{R}^n$ — column of numbers.
- $\mathbb{C}^n$ — complex vectors (quantum mechanics).
- $L^2(\Omega)$ — square-integrable functions.
- Polynomials of degree $\leq n$.
- $m \times n$ matrices.

### Applications in ML / quant finance

- **Embeddings**: word2vec, GloVe, BERT represent tokens as vectors in $\mathbb{R}^{300}$ to $\mathbb{R}^{12288}$. Operations like `king - man + woman ≈ queen` are literally vector algebra.
- **Quant finance**: a return vector is an element of $\mathbb{R}^N$ ($N$ = number of assets). A portfolio is a vector of weights. Risks via the covariance norm.
- **Reinforcement Learning**: state and action are vectors. The policy network maps $\mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^k$.
- **Quantum**: states are vectors in a complex Hilbert space. Operators are Hermitian matrices.
- **Computer vision**: a $1024\times 1024$ RGB image is a vector in $\mathbb{R}^{3 \cdot 10^6}$.

### Generalization: modules over rings

When the "field of scalars" is just a ring (e.g., $\mathbb{Z}$), you get **modules**. They appear in algebraic geometry, cryptography (lattices), and error-correcting codes.

### Numerical

In IEEE 754, summing very imbalanced vectors (Kahan summation) has cancellation errors. BLAS Level 1 (saxpy etc.) is implemented in optimized assembly. For $n = 10^9$, the difference between a naive `for` and BLAS is 100×.

### NumPy / SymPy

```python
import numpy as np
u = np.array([3, 4])
v = np.array([1, 2])
print(u + v)              # [4 6]
print(np.linalg.norm(u))  # 5
print(u / np.linalg.norm(u))  # versor

# Symbolic SymPy
import sympy as sp
v = sp.Matrix([3, 4])
print(v.norm())           # 5
```

### Curiosity

Hamilton (1843) invented quaternions trying to extend complex numbers to 3D. He failed in 3D (you can't define division), but succeeded in 4D. Today, quaternions are standard in CG and robotics for representing 3D rotations without gimbal lock.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practical application">

**Recipe: working with vectors in practice.**

1. **Write each vector as a pair** $(v_1, v_2)$ — clarity first.
2. **For sum/difference/scalar**: operate component by component.
3. **For magnitude**: $\sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2}$.
4. **For versor**: divide by the magnitude.
5. **For the angle with $x$**: $\theta = \text{atan2}(v_2, v_1)$ (not $\arctan$ — loses quadrant).

### Wolfram / NumPy workflow

```python
import numpy as np

# Initial position and velocity
r0 = np.array([0., 0.])
v  = np.array([3., 4.])

# After t seconds
def position(t):
    return r0 + v * t

print(position(2))   # [6 8]
print(np.linalg.norm(v))  # 5

# Angle with horizontal (radians and degrees)
theta = np.arctan2(v[1], v[0])
print(np.degrees(theta))  # 53.13...
```

### When to be careful?

- **Zero vector $(0,0)$**: magnitude 0, undefined direction. Be careful when normalizing.
- **`atan` vs `atan2`**: `atan(y/x)` loses the quadrant. Always use `atan2(y, x)`.
- **Sum with different scales**: a height vector (m) + a time vector (s) makes no sense — analyze units.
- **Vectors in high dimensions**: most pairs are nearly orthogonal (concentration of measure). The norm grows with $\sqrt n$.
