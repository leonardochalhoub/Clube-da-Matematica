### Parallelism and perpendicularity

Given lines $r: y = m_r x + n_r$ and $s: y = m_s x + n_s$:

| Position | Criterion (slope-intercept) | Criterion (general form) |
|---|---|---|
| Distinct parallels | $m_r = m_s$, $n_r \neq n_s$ | $A_r/A_s = B_r/B_s \neq C_r/C_s$ |
| Coincident | $m_r = m_s$, $n_r = n_s$ | $A_r/A_s = B_r/B_s = C_r/C_s$ |
| Concurrent | $m_r \neq m_s$ | $A_r B_s - A_s B_r \neq 0$ |
| Perpendicular | $m_r \cdot m_s = -1$ | $A_r A_s + B_r B_s = 0$ |

Cases with vertical lines: $x = a$ is vertical, $y = b$ is horizontal. Verticals to each other are parallel; vertical with horizontal are perpendicular.

### Angle between lines

$$\tan\theta = \left|\frac{m_r - m_s}{1 + m_r m_s}\right|$$

(Assuming neither line is vertical.)

### Point-to-line distance

Point $P_0 = (x_0, y_0)$ and line $Ax + By + C = 0$:

$$d(P_0, r) = \frac{|A x_0 + B y_0 + C|}{\sqrt{A^2 + B^2}}$$

### Distance between parallel lines

For $r: Ax + By + C_1 = 0$ and $s: Ax + By + C_2 = 0$:
$$d(r, s) = \frac{|C_1 - C_2|}{\sqrt{A^2 + B^2}}$$

### Perpendicular bisector

Set of points equidistant from $A$ and $B$. It is the line perpendicular to $\overline{AB}$ through the midpoint.

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

Imagine **two train tracks**. They run side by side and **never meet**. That's **parallel**.

Now imagine two **rulers forming a plus sign**, $+$. They cross making a **square corner**. That's **perpendicular**.

When two lines are not parallel and not perpendicular, they just **cross at a point** at an angle.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

Two lines in the plane can do 4 things:

1. **Parallel**: never meet (same slope).
2. **Coincident**: they are the same line.
3. **Concurrent**: cross at a point.
4. **Perpendicular**: cross at 90°.

**How can you tell?** Look at the slope $m$:
- $m_1 = m_2$: parallel (or coincident if $n$ is also equal).
- $m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1$: perpendicular.
- Otherwise: cross at some point.

**Concrete example**: $y = 2x + 1$ and $y = 2x - 5$ are parallel (slope 2 in both). $y = 2x$ and $y = -x/2$ are perpendicular ($2 \cdot (-1/2) = -1$).

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

Two lines in the plane can:

1. **Be parallel** (same slope, never cross).
2. **Be perpendicular** (form a 90° angle).
3. **Cross at one point** (concurrent).
4. **Be the same line** (coincident).

**Parallelism criterion**: $m_r = m_s$ — same slope.

**Perpendicularity criterion**: $m_r \cdot m_s = -1$. Why? Think of $m_1 = \tan\alpha_1$. If $\alpha_2 = \alpha_1 + 90°$, then $\tan\alpha_2 = -\cot\alpha_1 = -1/m_1$. So $m_1 m_2 = -1$.

**Complete numerical example**:
- $r: y = 2x + 1$ and $s: y = -x/2 + 5$. Product $2 \cdot (-1/2) = -1$. **Perpendicular**.
- Find the crossing point: $2x + 1 = -x/2 + 5 \Rightarrow 5x/2 = 4 \Rightarrow x = 8/5$. Substitute: $y = 16/5 + 1 = 21/5$. They cross at $(8/5, 21/5)$.

**Point-to-line distance** is fundamental — used in nearly every subsequent geometric problem. The formula $|Ax_0 + By_0 + C|/\sqrt{A^2 + B^2}$ comes from projecting the point onto the direction normal to the line.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

In practice, **solving 2x2 linear systems** is the intersection of lines. Cramer and row reduction start here.

### 2x2 system and geometric interpretation

$$\begin{cases} a_1 x + b_1 y = c_1 \\ a_2 x + b_2 y = c_2 \end{cases}$$

| Case | Geometry | Determinant |
|---|---|---|
| Unique solution | concurrent lines | $\det \neq 0$ |
| Infinite solutions | coincident lines | $\det = 0$ + consistent |
| No solution | distinct parallel lines | $\det = 0$ + inconsistent |

### Immediate applications

- **Linear regression**: the best-fit line is perpendicular to the direction of maximum error variance.
- **CG / SVG**: polygon clipping (Sutherland-Hodgman) tests sides of lines via the sign of $Ax + By + C$.
- **Robotics**: trajectory planning avoids obstacles represented as half-planes.
- **GIS**: proximity analysis — distance from each point to a road network.

### Proof of the formula $m_1 m_2 = -1$

Let $\vec u_1 = (1, m_1)$ and $\vec u_2 = (1, m_2)$ be direction vectors. Perpendicularity $\iff \vec u_1 \cdot \vec u_2 = 0 \iff 1 + m_1 m_2 = 0 \iff m_1 m_2 = -1$.

### Perpendicular bisector as a locus

Points $(x, y)$ equidistant from $A = (x_A, y_A)$ and $B = (x_B, y_B)$:
$$(x - x_A)^2 + (y - y_A)^2 = (x - x_B)^2 + (y - y_B)^2$$

Expanding, the quadratics cancel, leaving a line $\Rightarrow$ it is perpendicular to $\overline{AB}$ through the midpoint.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

The notion of "orthogonality" between lines generalizes to **orthogonality of subspaces** in any inner product space.

### Linear algebra view

Line = 1D affine subspace. Direction is a vector $\vec v$. Two lines $r_1, r_2$ are parallel $\iff \vec v_1 \parallel \vec v_2$. Perpendicular $\iff \langle \vec v_1, \vec v_2 \rangle = 0$.

In $\mathbb{R}^n$, the **orthogonal complement** of subspace $V$ is $V^\perp = \{w : \langle v, w \rangle = 0 \forall v \in V\}$. $V \oplus V^\perp = \mathbb{R}^n$. Basis for Gram-Schmidt, QR, and SVD.

### Applications in ML / quant

- **Linear regression**: the residual vector is orthogonal to the column space of $X$ (orthogonality principle). Formally, $\hat\beta = (X^TX)^{-1} X^Ty$ projects $y$ orthogonally.
- **PCA**: principal components are orthogonal — eigenvectors of the covariance matrix.
- **Quant finance**: portfolio rebalancing uses orthogonal projection of expected returns onto the constraint space.
- **Quantum**: orthogonal states $\langle \psi | \phi \rangle = 0$ correspond to "mutual exclusivity" of measurements.

### Distance via projection

The formula $d = |Ax_0 + By_0 + C|/\sqrt{A^2+B^2}$ is the magnitude of the projection of the "point-to-line" vector onto the unit normal $(A, B)/\sqrt{A^2+B^2}$. Generalizes to point-hyperplane distance in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

### Numerical

Be careful with near-parallel lines in linear systems — the condition number $\kappa(A) = \cot(\theta/2)$ blows up as $\theta \to 0$. Partial pivoting in Gauss + rescaling mitigates.

### SymPy

```python
import sympy as sp
from sympy import Line, Point
r = Line(Point(0, 0), Point(1, 2))
s = Line(Point(0, 5), Point(1, 7))
print(r.is_parallel(s))      # True
print(r.is_perpendicular(s)) # False
print(r.angle_between(s))    # 0
print(s.distance(Point(0, 0)))
```

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practical application">

**Recipe: classify two lines in 3 steps.**

1. **Compare slopes** $m_1, m_2$:
   - Equal? Go to parallel/coincident.
   - Product $-1$? Perpendicular.
   - Otherwise? Concurrent — find the intersection.
2. **If parallel**: compare $n_1, n_2$. Equal $\Rightarrow$ coincident; otherwise distinct.
3. **To find the angle**: use $\tan\theta = |(m_1 - m_2)/(1 + m_1 m_2)|$.

### Complete workflow

```python
import sympy as sp
from sympy import Line, Point, pi
r = Line(Point(0, 1), Point(1, 3))   # y = 2x + 1
s = Line(Point(0, 5), Point(2, 4))   # y = -x/2 + 5
print(r.is_perpendicular(s))                   # True
print(r.intersection(s))                       # crossing point
print(sp.deg(r.angle_between(s)))              # 90
print(s.distance(Point(0, 0)))                 # origin-to-line distance
```

### When to be careful?

- **Vertical lines**: $m$ undefined — use general form.
- **Near-parallel**: a small perturbation changes the intersection a lot. In practice, compute $\det$ and discard if $|\det| < \epsilon$.
- **Distance vs sign**: the formula $|Ax_0 + By_0 + C|$ uses absolute value. Without it, you get the **side** of the line — useful in clipping algorithms.
