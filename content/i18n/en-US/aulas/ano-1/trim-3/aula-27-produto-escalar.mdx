<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Definition and properties">

<Definicao titulo="Dot product">
For $\vec u, \vec v \in \mathbb{R}^2$:
$$\vec u \cdot \vec v = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2$$

Equivalently:
$$\vec u \cdot \vec v = |\vec u| |\vec v| \cos\theta$$

where $\theta \in [0, \pi]$ is the angle between the vectors.
</Definicao>

### Properties

- **Commutative**: $\vec u \cdot \vec v = \vec v \cdot \vec u$.
- **Distributive**: $\vec u \cdot (\vec v + \vec w) = \vec u \cdot \vec v + \vec u \cdot \vec w$.
- **Linear in scalar**: $(\alpha \vec u) \cdot \vec v = \alpha (\vec u \cdot \vec v)$.
- **Positive**: $\vec u \cdot \vec u = |\vec u|^2 \geq 0$, with equality $\iff \vec u = \vec 0$.

### Sign and geometry

| $\vec u \cdot \vec v$ | $\cos\theta$ | Angle |
|---|---|---|
| $> 0$ | $> 0$ | acute $\theta < 90°$ |
| $= 0$ | $0$ | right, $\theta = 90°$ (orthogonal) |
| $< 0$ | $< 0$ | obtuse $\theta > 90°$ |

### Orthogonality

$\vec u \perp \vec v \iff \vec u \cdot \vec v = 0$.

### Angle

$$\cos\theta = \frac{\vec u \cdot \vec v}{|\vec u| |\vec v|}$$

### Projection

Projection of $\vec u$ onto the direction of $\vec v$:
$$\text{proj}_{\vec v} \vec u = \frac{\vec u \cdot \vec v}{|\vec v|^2} \vec v$$

Magnitude (scalar) of the projection: $\frac{\vec u \cdot \vec v}{|\vec v|}$.

### Central application — mechanical work

$W = \vec F \cdot \vec d$ — work done by a force is its dot product with the displacement.

Imagine two **little straws** pointing in different directions. When they're **in the same direction**, they **add force together** — the dot product is **big**.

When they're **perpendicular** (in a **+** shape), neither helps the other — the dot product is **zero**.

When they're **opposite**, one **cancels** the other — the dot product is **negative**.

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

The **dot product** is an operation between two vectors that gives **a number** (not a vector). That's why it's "scalar".

The rule is simple: $(u_1, u_2) \cdot (v_1, v_2) = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2$.

**Example**: $(3, 4) \cdot (1, 2) = 3 \cdot 1 + 4 \cdot 2 = 3 + 8 = 11$.

**What's it for?** To know if two vectors **point more or less in the same direction**:
- **Positive** result: they point in the same direction.
- **Zero** result: they are perpendicular (90°).
- **Negative** result: they point in opposite directions.

**Real application**: pushing a cart. If you push **in the direction of motion**, you do **a lot of work**. If you push **upward** or **sideways** (perpendicular to the motion), you do **zero useful work**.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

The dot product combines two vectors **but the result is a number** (not a vector). Hence "scalar".

The formula is simple: $(u_1, u_2) \cdot (v_1, v_2) = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2$.

Example: $(3, 4) \cdot (1, 2) = 3 + 8 = 11$.

**Why does it matter?** Because the dot product measures how much two vectors **point in the same direction**:
- Same direction: dot product is positive and maximal.
- Perpendicular: dot product is **zero** $\Rightarrow$ perpendicularity criterion.
- Opposite directions: dot product is negative.

The geometric formula is $\vec u \cdot \vec v = |\vec u||\vec v|\cos\theta$. If $\theta = 90°$, $\cos = 0$, dot product zero.

**Where it shows up?** Mechanical work in physics: $W = \vec F \cdot \vec d$ — work is force times displacement, but only the part of the effort that is **in the direction** of motion counts.

**Numerical angle example**: angle between $(1, 0)$ and $(1, 1)$? $\cos\theta = (1 \cdot 1 + 0 \cdot 1)/(1 \cdot \sqrt 2) = 1/\sqrt 2$. $\theta = 45°$.

**Connection to Lesson 23**: the criterion $m_1 m_2 = -1$ for perpendicularity comes from the dot product of the lines' direction vectors.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

The dot product is the **fundamental operation** of Euclidean linear algebra. Generalizes to $\mathbb{R}^n$:
$$\vec u \cdot \vec v = \sum_{i=1}^n u_i v_i$$

### Cross-domain applications

- **Physics**: work $W = \vec F \cdot \vec d$, power $P = \vec F \cdot \vec v$, flux $\Phi = \vec E \cdot \vec A$ (Gauss).
- **ML**: cosine similarity = $\vec u \cdot \vec v / (|\vec u||\vec v|)$ — standard metric for embeddings, classifiers, semantic search.
- **DSP**: cross-correlation $r_{xy}(\tau) = \sum x_n y_{n+\tau}$ is a dot product — basis for matched filters, GPS, radar.
- **Statistics**: covariance $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]$ is a centered dot product. Correlation = cosine between centered vectors.
- **Quantum**: amplitude $\langle \phi | \psi \rangle$ is a dot product (complex version). Born rule: probability $= |\langle \phi | \psi \rangle|^2$.

### Vector law of cosines

$|\vec u - \vec v|^2 = |\vec u|^2 + |\vec v|^2 - 2 \vec u \cdot \vec v$

Recovers the law of cosines from Lesson 15: $c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C$.

### Cauchy-Schwarz inequality

$$|\vec u \cdot \vec v| \leq |\vec u| |\vec v|$$

Equality when vectors are parallel. Crucial in probability (correlation $\leq 1$), in norms, in optimization. Proof via $|\vec u + t\vec v|^2 \geq 0$ for all $t$ — discriminant $\leq 0$.

### Orthogonal decomposition

Every $\vec u$ is uniquely written as the sum of a part parallel and a part perpendicular to $\vec v$:
$$\vec u = \text{proj}_{\vec v} \vec u + (\vec u - \text{proj}_{\vec v} \vec u)$$

Basis of **Gram-Schmidt** in linear algebra → QR factorization, least squares.

### Equivalence of the two definitions

Proof of $\vec u \cdot \vec v = |\vec u||\vec v|\cos\theta$ via the law of cosines:

Triangle with sides $|\vec u|, |\vec v|, |\vec u - \vec v|$. Law of cosines:
$$|\vec u - \vec v|^2 = |\vec u|^2 + |\vec v|^2 - 2|\vec u||\vec v|\cos\theta$$

But $|\vec u - \vec v|^2 = (u_1 - v_1)^2 + (u_2 - v_2)^2 = |\vec u|^2 + |\vec v|^2 - 2(u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2)$.

Equating: $u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 = |\vec u||\vec v|\cos\theta$. QED.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

The dot product is the canonical example of a **symmetric positive-definite bilinear form** on $\mathbb{R}^n$.

### Functional view

In any vector space $V$ with inner product $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$:
- **Induced norm**: $\|x\| = \sqrt{\langle x, x \rangle}$.
- **Cauchy-Schwarz**: $|\langle x, y \rangle| \leq \|x\|\|y\|$.
- **Projection**: $P_y x = \frac{\langle x, y \rangle}{\langle y, y \rangle} y$.

Generalizes to $L^2(\Omega)$: $\langle f, g \rangle = \int f\bar g$. Fourier $\hat f(\omega) = \langle f, e^{i\omega t}\rangle$ is literally "decomposition into an orthogonal basis of complex exponentials".

### Applications in ML / quant

- **Attention mechanism (Transformers)**: $\text{softmax}(QK^T/\sqrt d)V$. The product $QK^T$ is a matrix of dot products — how "similar" each query is to each key.
- **Kernel methods (SVM)**: kernel trick $K(x, y) = \langle \phi(x), \phi(y) \rangle$ implicit in feature space. RBF, polynomial, etc.
- **Quant**: portfolio covariance $\sigma_p^2 = w^T \Sigma w$ is a quadratic form (dot product with matrix). Markowitz minimizes this.
- **Sharpe ratio**: $S = (E[r] - r_f)/\sigma$ — normalized dot product.
- **Word2vec**: log-likelihood gradient is a function of dot products between embeddings.

### Generalization: metric tensor

On a manifold $(M, g)$, at each point $p$ there is an inner product $g_p: T_pM \times T_pM \to \mathbb{R}$. Differential geometry becomes "local dot product". General Relativity uses a Lorentzian metric tensor (signature $(-,+,+,+)$).

### Numerical

`numpy.dot` for float64: relative error $O(n \epsilon_{\text{mach}})$. For huge $n$, use Kahan summation or pairwise summation. cuBLAS/MKL optimize memory and SIMD — take advantage.

### NumPy / SymPy

```python
import numpy as np
u = np.array([3, 4])
v = np.array([1, 2])
print(u @ v)                              # 11 (matmul operator = dot in 1D)
print(np.dot(u, v))                       # 11
cos_t = u @ v / (np.linalg.norm(u) * np.linalg.norm(v))
print(np.degrees(np.arccos(cos_t)))       # ~10.30°
```

### Curiosity

J. Willard Gibbs (1884) and Oliver Heaviside formalized the modern "vector calculus" we know. Hamilton had quaternions; Grassmann had extension calculus; Gibbs/Heaviside extracted the dot and cross products as primary operations. The notation $\vec u \cdot \vec v$ is theirs.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practical application">

**Recipe: using the dot product in real problems.**

| Question | Computation |
|---|---|
| Are they perpendicular? | $\vec u \cdot \vec v \stackrel{?}{=} 0$ |
| What is the angle? | $\theta = \arccos\frac{\vec u \cdot \vec v}{\|\vec u\|\|\vec v\|}$ |
| Projection of $\vec u$ onto $\vec v$? | $\frac{\vec u \cdot \vec v}{\|\vec v\|^2} \vec v$ |
| Work done by a force? | $W = \vec F \cdot \vec d$ |
| Cosine similarity? | $\frac{\vec u \cdot \vec v}{\|\vec u\|\|\vec v\|}$ |

### NumPy workflow

```python
import numpy as np

u = np.array([1, 2, 3])
v = np.array([4, 5, 6])

dot = np.dot(u, v)
cos_sim = dot / (np.linalg.norm(u) * np.linalg.norm(v))
print(f"dot={dot}, cos sim={cos_sim:.4f}")

# Projection
proj = (dot / np.dot(v, v)) * v
print(f"projection: {proj}")
```

### When to be careful?

- **Zero vectors**: $\vec u \cdot \vec 0 = 0$. Trivial, but angle undefined.
- **Near-parallel vectors**: $\arccos$ near 0 or $\pi$ has poor precision. Use $\arctan2$ tricks when possible.
- **In high dimensions**: cosine similarity is preferred over Euclidean distance — concentration of measure makes distances near-equal.
- **Watch units**: $\vec F$ in N and $\vec d$ in m → $W$ in J. But if $\vec d$ is in km, multiply by 1000 first.
