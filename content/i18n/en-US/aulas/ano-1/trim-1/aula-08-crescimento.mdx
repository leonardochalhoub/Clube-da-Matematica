<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="General model">

The equation $N(t) = N_0 e^{kt}$ is a solution of:

<Equation explicacao="Separable variable ODE: the rate of change of N is proportional to N itself. This equation is central in Trim 10 — the rigorous justification of the model appears when you learn derivatives and ODEs.">
{`\\frac{dN}{dt} = kN`}
</Equation>

### Canonical applications

| Phenomenon | Equation | $k$ |
|---|---|---|
| Population growth | $P(t) = P_0 e^{rt}$ | $r > 0$ intrinsic rate |
| Continuous compound interest | $S(t) = S_0 e^{it}$ | $i$ nominal rate |
| Radioactive decay | $N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$ | $\lambda$ decay constant |
| Cooling (Newton) | $T(t) - T_a = (T_0 - T_a) e^{-kt}$ | $k$ depends on material |
| RC discharge | $V(t) = V_0 e^{-t/RC}$ | $\tau = RC$ |

### Time-vs-half-life connection

<Equation explicacao="Half-life τ is the time to reduce by half. Doubling time is analogous for growth.">
{`\\tau_{1/2} = \\frac{\\ln 2}{|k|} \\approx \\frac{0{,}693}{|k|}`}
</Equation>

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

You take a sheet of paper and fold it in half. Now there are 2 parts. Fold again: 4 parts. Another fold: 8 parts.

Each fold **doubles** the quantity. In 10 folds it would be **a thousand parts**. In 20 folds, more than **a million**. In 42 folds (if you could — nobody can), the sheet would be **bigger than the Moon**.

That's how things grow really fast. Mathematicians call this **exponential**.

There are things that grow this way in the world: bacteria, viruses, money with interest, people telling a piece of gossip to two friends, each of them telling two more...

There are things that **shrink** this way too: hot water cooling down, medicine leaving the body, sand pouring from a leaky bucket.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

**Exponential growth model**: each thing creates more copies of itself. Like bacteria, which divide in half:

| Hour | Quantity |
|---:|---:|
| 0 | 100 |
| 1 | 200 |
| 2 | 400 |
| 3 | 800 |
| 4 | 1,600 |
| ... | ... |
| 24 | 1,677,721,600 |

In **one day**, 100 bacteria become **1.7 billion**. That's why spoiled food spoils fast — just one bacterium surviving and in hours you have millions.

The formula is $N(t) = N_0 \cdot 2^t$ (when it doubles every hour). If the base is not 2 (say it triples every 2 hours), the formula changes: $N(t) = N_0 \cdot 3^{t/2}$.

**Decay** is the opposite. Imagine that every hour **half** of the bacteria **die** (in a drug that kills them):

| Hour | Quantity |
|---:|---:|
| 0 | 1,000 |
| 1 | 500 |
| 2 | 250 |
| 3 | 125 |
| 4 | 62 |
| ... | ... |

The formula is $N(t) = N_0 \cdot (1/2)^t$. The "**half-life**" is the time it takes to drop in half. In the example above, it's 1 hour.

Carbon-14 (fossil dating) has a half-life of **5,730 years**. That's why fossils can be dated up to ~50,000 years.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

**Exponential growth is scary.** It goes slow at the beginning, then explodes.

Classic story: king loses chess, prize is "1 grain of rice on the 1st square, 2 on the 2nd, 4 on the 3rd, 8 on the 4th, and so on up to the 64th". Total: $2^{64} - 1 \approx 1{,}8 \times 10^{19}$ grains. **More rice than all global production for thousands of years.**

**Real exponential growth:**
- COVID-19 in the first 30 days of 2020.
- Bacteria in nutrient medium.
- Unpaid interest (debt).

**Real exponential decay:**
- Carbon-14 in a fossil.
- Coffee cooling down.
- Capacitor discharging.

The rule: **if the rate is proportional to the quantity**, the function is exponential.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

The model $\dot N = kN$ is ubiquitous. In **chemical engineering** (1st-order reaction kinetics), **electrical engineering** (RC, RL circuits), **biomedical** (compartmental pharmacokinetics), **structural** (creep), **financial** (continuous pricing).

### Linearization via log

Plotting $N(t)$ vs $t$ in **log-y scale** transforms the exponential into a straight line:
$$\ln N = \ln N_0 + kt$$

The slope of the line is $k$. This is the basis of **simple linear regression** on data that looks exponential.

### Limitations of the pure model

Pure exponential growth is **unsustainable**: it implies $N \to \infty$. Realistic models:
- **Logistic**: $\dot N = rN(1 - N/K)$, $K$ = capacity
- **Gompertz**: $\dot N = rN \ln(K/N)$, common in tumor modeling
- **Allee effect**: incorporates decreasing rate in small populations

Knowing the limitations is more important than memorizing the formula.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

In practice, the model $\dot N = rN$ is the **null model** — first approximation. Every serious analysis starts there and adds correction terms.

### Model identification from data

If you have a time series $N_1, N_2, \ldots, N_n$ and suspect it is exponential:

1. Plot in **log-y scale**.
2. If it becomes a straight line, it's exponential. Slope = $r$.
3. If it curves downward, it's logistic. Use nonlinear fit.
4. If it becomes a line with a **break** (slope change), it's a regime-shift — two exponentials.

### Living chart: implied vol = volatility surface

In implied volatility (Black-Scholes), the temporal evolution of options follows a **GBM** (geometric Brownian motion): $dS = \mu S \, dt + \sigma S \, dW$. Solution: $S_t = S_0 \exp((\mu - \sigma^2/2)t + \sigma W_t)$. **Exponential motion with Gaussian noise in the exponent.**

### Econometric identification

GDP growth, M1, debt — all modeled as exponential in first approximation. But:
- $\hat r$ obtained by OLS on $\log N$ vs $t$ is biased when errors have autocorrelation.
- Use **Hamilton filter** or **Kalman filter** if there are regime-shifts.
- For population growth, **logistic** with demographic surveys.

### Caution: log of successive small changes

In finance, **log-return** $r_t = \log(P_t/P_{t-1}) \approx \Delta P/P$ for small variations. Why log? Because returns add (instead of multiplying): $P_T/P_0 = \prod(P_t/P_{t-1}) \Rightarrow \log(P_T/P_0) = \sum r_t$. **Sum of random variables is tractable; product is not.**

### Spreading models (epidemic, innovation, viral)

Bass diffusion model ($\dot N = (p + qN/K)(K - N)$) — Frank Bass 1969 — generalizes logistic with 2 parameters (innovation $p$ + imitation $q$). Used in forecasting adoption of new products (smartphone, EV, vaccine). Successfully fitted to 100+ products.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practice — 3 real scenarios with Brazilian data">

### Scenario 1: Inflation and purchasing power (Brazil 2019-2026)

IPCA accumulated 2019-2025: ~36% (average ~5.3% per year). For a salary of R\$ 5,000 in 2019:

$$\text{real purchasing power}(t) = 5{,}000 \cdot \e^{-0{,}053 t}$$

In 2026 ($t = 7$): purchasing power ≈ R\$ 3,450. **Real loss of 31%** over 7 years without adjustment.

**Operational implication**: a fixed salary loses purchasing power quickly. To maintain $5,000$ real reais, in 7 years the nominal salary needs to be $5,000 \cdot \e^{0{,}053 \cdot 7} = 7,246$. Those who didn't negotiate annual adjustments of at least 5.3% **got poorer without noticing**.

### Scenario 2: COVID-19 in Brazil (SIR-Like model)

Brazilian cases from March/2020 to May/2020 grew at $r \approx 0{,}28$/day. Doubling time: $\ln 2 / 0{,}28 \approx 2{,}5$ days.

Without intervention, projection in 60 days: $1 \cdot \e^{0{,}28 \cdot 60} \approx 18 \text{ million}$ — clearly impossible, signaling that the pure exponential model saturated.

**Lesson**: the exponential model **only holds while $N \ll K$** (system capacity). After May/2020, growth became logistic, then oscillatory (second wave).

### Scenario 3: Netflix decay (lifetime customer value)

Classic churn modeling: $S(t) = S_0 \cdot \e^{-c t}$ with $c$ = churn rate. For Netflix BR (estimated), $c \approx 0{,}02$/month (2% monthly cancellation).

**Estimated lifetime**: $\E[T] = 1/c = 50$ months ≈ 4.2 years. With monthly fee of R\$ 50, LTV = R\$ 2,500 per customer.

**Acceptable acquisition cost (CAC)**: up to ~30% of LTV ≈ R\$ 750 per new customer. Above that, the business model breaks. **Every SaaS startup has this calculation on slide 1 of the pitch**.
