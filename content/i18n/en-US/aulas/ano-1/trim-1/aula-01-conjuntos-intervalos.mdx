### Fundamental number sets

<Definicao titulo="Hierarchy of real numbers">

- **Naturals** $\mathbb{N} = \{0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots\}$. (Brazilian convention includes zero; French convention excludes it.)
- **Integers** $\mathbb{Z} = \{\ldots, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$ — closed under addition, subtraction, and multiplication.
- **Rationals** $\mathbb{Q} = \{p/q \mid p \in \mathbb{Z},\ q \in \mathbb{Z}_{\neq 0}\}$ — every number expressible as a ratio of integers.
- **Reals** $\mathbb{R}$ — completes the axis (fills in the "holes" like $\sqrt{2}$, $\pi$, $\e$).

</Definicao>

### Set operations

<Definicao titulo="Union, intersection, difference, complement">

- **Union**: $A \cup B = \{x : x \in A \text{ or } x \in B\}$
- **Intersection**: $A \cap B = \{x : x \in A \text{ and } x \in B\}$
- **Difference**: $A \setminus B = \{x \in A : x \notin B\}$
- **Complement** (in a universe $U$): $A^c = U \setminus A$

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

Imagine you have a box of **toys**. Another box of **books**.

- **Union** (∪) is putting both boxes together into one. It has toys AND books.
- **Intersection** (∩) is what is in BOTH boxes at the same time. Usually empty — a toy is not a book.
- But if one box has "**red marbles**" and the other has "**small marbles**", the intersection is the marbles that are **red AND small**.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

Mathematicians love to organize numbers into families.

- The family of **naturals** ℕ is just what you use to count things: 0, 1, 2, 3, ...
- The family of **integers** ℤ adds the negatives: ..., −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, ...
- The family of **rationals** ℚ adds fractions: 1/2, 3/4, −5/7, ...
- The family of **reals** ℝ adds weird numbers like **π** (3.14159...) and √2 (1.41421...).

And **intervals** are short ways of saying "all numbers between such and such":
- `[0, 5]` means "every number from 0 to 5, **including** 0 and 5".
- `(0, 5)` means "every number from 0 to 5, **without** 0 and without 5".

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teenager">

This is the lesson that establishes the **language** we will use over the next 3 years. All advanced mathematics — calculus, linear algebra, statistics — uses this notation. Without it, it is like trying to read a physics book without knowing the letters.

**The 4 central number sets:**

| Symbol | Name | Example |
|---|---|---|
| $\mathbb{N}$ | naturals | 0, 1, 2, 3, ... |
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | integers | ..., −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, ... |
| $\mathbb{Q}$ | rationals | 1/2, 3/4, −7/5, 0.333... = 1/3 |
| $\mathbb{R}$ | reals | all of these + $\pi$, $\sqrt{2}$, $\e$ |

And an important observation: **not every real number is rational**. Irrationals (like $\sqrt{2}$ and $\pi$) are real numbers that CANNOT be written as a fraction of integers. This is a theorem known since Pythagoras (5th c. BCE) — an elegant proof you will see in exercise 28 of this lesson.

**Intervals** are abbreviations for "all real numbers between such limits":

- `[3, 7]` = $\{x \in \mathbb{R} : 3 \leq x \leq 7\}$ (closed, with included edges)
- `(3, 7)` = $\{x : 3 < x < 7\}$ (open, without the edges)

When the teacher writes $x \in [3, 7]$ on the board, they are saying: "x is any real number between 3 and 7, including 3 and 7". Super compact. Compare with writing this out in words.

<Insight>

**Why is there a difference between $[3, 7]$ and $(3, 7)$?** Because in advanced mathematics, "including the point" and "approaching it without reaching it" are different phenomena. When you study **limits** in a few months, you will see that $\lim_{x \to 3^+}$ asks what happens *as x approaches 3 without being 3*. The difference between open and closed starts here, now, when you are 15.

</Insight>

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

### Critical points

**1. Does ℕ include 0?** Brazilian convention (BNCC) and French: YES. American convention and several classical ones: NO. I adopt **0 ∈ ℕ** throughout this program, aligned with the BNCC. When reading foreign books, check the author's convention.

**2. Brazilian vs. Anglo-Saxon interval notation:**
- BR: $]a, b[$ for open, $[a, b]$ for closed (Bourbaki notation inherited from France)
- Anglo: $(a, b)$ for open, $[a, b]$ for closed

I adopt **Anglo notation** (parentheses for open) for compatibility with 90% of the open books referenced in `livros/CATALOG.md`.

**3. Density of ℚ in ℝ.** Between any two distinct reals $a < b$, there exists a rational $q$ with $a < q < b$. **Proof** (Exercise 26): use the Archimedean property — for every $\eps > 0$, there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $1/n < \eps$.

**4. Finite vs. countably infinite vs. uncountable sets.** Cantor (1874) proved that ℝ is **uncountable** but ℚ is **countable** — both are infinite, but with different cardinalities. You will use this fact in university-level Analysis (Calculus IV / Real Analysis).

### Cross-domain connection

- **Calculus**: the domain of a function is always a subset of ℝ.
- **Probability**: the sample space is a set.
- **Linear Algebra**: vectors are tuples in ℝⁿ or ℂⁿ.
- **Computing**: primitive types `int`, `float`, `bool` correspond to ℤ (with bound), ℝ (with finite precision), $\{T, F\}$.

### Numerical example

Solve $|2x - 3| \leq 5$ and express the result in interval notation:

$$|2x - 3| \leq 5 \iff -5 \leq 2x - 3 \leq 5 \iff -2 \leq 2x \leq 8 \iff -1 \leq x \leq 4$$

Answer: $x \in [-1, 4]$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

Set theory is the ZFC foundation (Zermelo-Fraenkel + Axiom of Choice) that supports all of modern mathematics. The intuitive "definitions" we present in this lesson have known pitfalls:

- **Russell's paradox**: the set of all sets that do not contain themselves. If it contains itself, then it does not. If it does not contain itself, then it does. This broke Cantor's naive theory (1901) and led Zermelo to axiomatize the theory with restricted comprehension.

- **Continuum Hypothesis**: Cantor conjectured that no cardinality exists between $|\mathbb{N}|$ and $|\mathbb{R}|$. Gödel (1940) and Cohen (1963) proved that the hypothesis is **independent** of the ZFC axioms — both it and its negation are consistent with ZFC.

- **Axiom of Choice**: apparently innocent, but implies strange results like Banach-Tarski (decompose a sphere and reassemble two equal to the original). The decision to adopt AC is practical but philosophically delicate.

In professional practice (engineering, applied science, finance), none of this appears. You use $\mathbb{R}^n$ as Euclidean space and move on. But if one day you do graduate work in Analysis, Logic, or Set Theory, you will return to these paradoxes.

### What is "real" anyway?

Computationally, ℝ does not exist — every digital implementation uses **floating point** (IEEE 754), with ~7 digits of precision (float32) or ~15 (float64). Floating-point errors propagate in numerical computation (covered in Trimester 10 with REAMAT). When you write `0.1 + 0.2` in Python, you will get `0.30000000000000004`. ℚ ⊊ ℝ is a mathematical abstraction; the computer works in a finite subset much smaller than ℚ.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practice — examples with real data">

### Example 1: ENEM age range

ENEM 2025 registration accepts candidates with 16 full years up to the date of the first exam. In notation:

$$\text{candidate's age} \in [16, +\infty)$$

Note the right half-open interval (no upper limit).

### Example 2: Minimum wage and income tax exemption

In 2025 in Brazil, total IR exemption for income:

$$\text{monthly income} \in [0,\ 2\,824]$$

(Range in R\$.) Above that, progressive rate.

### Example 3: Fasting glucose ranges (medicine)

| Range | Interval (mg/dL) | Diagnosis |
|---|---|---|
| Normal | $[0, 99]$ | healthy |
| Pre-diabetes | $[100, 125]$ | caution |
| Diabetes | $[126, +\infty)$ | treatment |

Every medical guideline is expressed as a **partition** of the set of possible values into intervals. If you become a biomedical engineer or healthcare software developer, you will work with these ranges every day.

### Example 4: Domain of a cost function

Cost function $C(q) = 50 + 12q + 0.5q^2$ for manufacturing $q$ units.

Mathematical domain: $C: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$.
Physical (real) domain: $q \in \{0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots\} = \mathbb{N}$ (you do not manufacture half a car).

When the calculation of the "optimal point" yields $q^* = 17.3$, it is your job as an engineer to decide whether to round to 17 or 18 — continuous math produces a solution in ℝ; the application requires a solution in ℕ. This **discrete vs. continuous** tension is one of the five sources of epistemological difficulty in Calculus according to Rezende (UFF, 2003).

</Porta>
