<Definicao titulo="Quadratic function">
$f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$, $f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c$ with $a, b, c \in \mathbb{R}$ and $a \neq 0$.
</Definicao>

### Roots — Quadratic formula

<Equation numero="1" explicacao="The famous solution formula. Δ = b² − 4ac is the discriminant. Δ > 0: two distinct real roots. Δ = 0: a double root. Δ < 0: no real roots (two complex conjugates).">
{`x = \\frac{-b \\pm \\sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}`}
</Equation>

### Vertex

<Equation numero="2" explicacao="x-coordinate of the vertex — critical point (maximum if a < 0, minimum if a > 0). It is proven by completing the square, or by averaging the roots.">
{`x_V = -\\frac{b}{2a}, \\quad y_V = f(x_V) = -\\frac{\\Delta}{4a}`}
</Equation>

### Canonical form (completing the square)

<Equation numero="3">
{`f(x) = a(x - x_V)^2 + y_V`}
</Equation>

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

Take a ball and throw it really high. It goes up, up, up, then down, down, down.

If you draw the path of the ball, it makes a curve. This drawing has a name: **parabola**.

Every time you throw something into the air — a ball, a stone, a rocket — its path makes the drawing of a parabola.

Mathematicians discovered a formula that describes this path with letters and numbers: $y = ax^2 + bx + c$. You don't need to memorize it now — just know that **every parabola has a formula like this**.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

The parabola is the curve a ball draws in the air. But it is also the shape of:

- The **satellite dish** (TV satellite parabolic antenna)
- The **car headlight** (parabolic reflector)
- **Arch bridge structure**
- **Trajectory of fireworks**

Why does this curve appear so much? Because it has a special property: if a ray comes in parallel to the axis of the parabola, it hits the surface and **reflects to a single point** called the **focus**. This is why the parabolic antenna concentrates the signal at a small receiver — all the signals that arrive become one.

The formula is $y = ax^2 + bx + c$. The $a$ controls how "open" the parabola is: large $a$ makes it narrow, small $a$ makes it wide. The sign of $a$ controls which way it points: $a > 0$ points up (smile), $a < 0$ points down (sad face).

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

The quadratic function $f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c$ is the first "curved" function we study formally. The graph is a **parabola** — opens upward if $a > 0$, opens downward if $a < 0$.

**Three things to know:**

1. **Roots**: where the parabola crosses the x-axis. The quadratic formula gives:
   $$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

2. **Vertex**: the highest point (if $a < 0$) or lowest (if $a > 0$). x-coordinate: $x_V = -b/(2a)$.

3. **Concavity**: $a > 0$ is "smiling" (cup), $a < 0$ is "sad" (cap).

The trajectory of a thrown ball is a parabola. The parabolic antenna has this shape. The car headlight too. **Why do parabolas appear in so many places?** Because they have a geometric property: every ray parallel to the axis is reflected to the focus. (Or inversely: rays leaving the focus go out parallel.) You'll see this in Calculus 3.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

The quadratic is **much** more than precalculus:

- Second-order differential equations with constant coefficients (Term 10) reduce to a **characteristic** quadratic equation $\lambda^2 + b\lambda + c = 0$.
- Unconstrained optimization of quadratic functions is trivial **convex programming** — basis of regression.
- The quadratic formula generalizes to Cardano (3rd degree) and Ferrari (4th degree). Galois (1832) proved that there is no general formula for degree ≥ 5 with radicals.
- Discriminant $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$ controls whether the solutions are real or complex. It appears in vibratory mechanics: $\Delta > 0$ is supercritical damping (no oscillation), $\Delta = 0$ is critical, $\Delta < 0$ is underdamped (oscillates).

### Proof of the quadratic formula (completing the square)

$ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \Rightarrow x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x = -\frac{c}{a}$

Add $\left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2$ to both sides:

$\left(x + \frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 = \frac{b^2 - 4ac}{4a^2}$

Taking the root: $x + \frac{b}{2a} = \pm\frac{\sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$

Therefore $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$. ∎

### Sum and product of roots (Vieta)

If $x_1, x_2$ are roots of $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$, then:

$$x_1 + x_2 = -\frac{b}{a}, \quad x_1 \cdot x_2 = \frac{c}{a}$$

(Useful in problems where you don't need to find the roots individually, only their sum or product.)

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

The quadratic reappears at industrial scale. I list what matters in practice:

### Quadratic Programming (QP)

Optimization of the form $\min \frac{1}{2} x^T Q x + c^T x$ subject to $Ax \leq b$. When $Q$ is positive semi-definite, it is convex and has a unique solution (KKT). Applications:

- **Markowitz portfolio**: $\min \mathbf{w}^T \Sigma \mathbf{w}$ — minimum variance portfolio given expected return.
- **Constrained least squares**: regression under $\sum w_i = 1$, $w_i \geq 0$.
- **Linear SVM**: maximum margin classifier is a QP in $\mathbb{R}^n$.
- **MPC** (Model Predictive Control): at each control step, a QP is solved.

Standard solvers: OSQP, Gurobi, CPLEX. Computational cost $O(n^3)$ in the worst case (interior-point resolution); acceptable up to $n \sim 10^4$ on modern hardware.

### Parabolic approximation in non-linear optimization

Newton-Raphson in optimization: at each step, approximate the objective function by a quadratic (2nd-order Taylor truncation) and minimize this quadratic. **Every Newton iteration is solving a local quadratic.** That's why knowing the quadratic formula is the basis of advanced optimization.

### Parabolic curve fitting

In the lab, fitting $y = ax^2 + bx + c$ to $n$ points is OLS in variables $\{1, x, x^2\}$. Coefficients obtained by the normal equation $X^T X \beta = X^T y$, with $X$ the design matrix. Standard residual: $\hat\sigma^2 = \text{SSR}/(n-3)$. **Caution**: extrapolation outside the sampled interval fails catastrophically — extrapolated quadratic goes to $\pm\infty$ quickly.

### Quadratic in differential equations

ODE $y'' + b y' + c y = 0$ has characteristic equation $\lambda^2 + b\lambda + c = 0$. The roots determine the form of the solution:

| $\Delta$ | Behavior | Application |
|---|---|---|
| $\Delta > 0$ | Overdamped (no oscillation) | Car suspension |
| $\Delta = 0$ | Critically damped | Automatic door (returns without slamming) |
| $\Delta < 0$ | Underdamped (oscillates) | Pendulum, RLC circuit |

All mechanical vibration analysis and electrical oscillation passes through here — Term 10 details it.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practice — 3 real scenarios">

### Scenario 1: Ballistic trajectory (projectile launch)

A projectile is launched with initial velocity $v_0 = 25$ m/s at an angle $\theta = 45°$ from the ground. The components:

$v_{0x} = v_0 \cos\theta \approx 17.68$ m/s, $v_{0y} = v_0 \sin\theta \approx 17.68$ m/s.

Height as a function of time: $h(t) = v_{0y} t - \tfrac{1}{2} g t^2 = 17.68 t - 4.9 t^2$.

**Analysis.** Vertex of the parabola: $t_V = -b/(2a) = 17.68/9.8 \approx 1.80$ s, $h_V = h(t_V) \approx 15.96$ m. Hits the ground: $h(t) = 0 \Rightarrow t = 0$ or $t \approx 3.61$ s. Horizontal range: $x = v_{0x} \cdot 3.61 \approx 63.8$ m.

### Scenario 2: Open-top rectangular box (classic optimization)

Square cardboard of side 30 cm. Squares of side $x$ are cut from the corners and folded into a box. Volume:

$$V(x) = x \cdot (30 - 2x)^2 = 4x^3 - 120 x^2 + 900 x$$

Not quadratic — it's cubic! But its **derivative** is quadratic: $V'(x) = 12 x^2 - 240 x + 900 = 0$. By the quadratic formula: $x = (240 \pm \sqrt{57600 - 43200})/24 = (240 \pm 120)/24$. Therefore $x = 5$ (valid maximum) or $x = 15$ (degenerate, doesn't fit). Maximum volume: $V(5) = 5 \cdot 400 = 2{,}000$ cm³.

### Scenario 3: Monopolist's profit (microeconomics)

A monopolist faces demand $Q(p) = 1000 - 50 p$ and unit cost $c = 5$. Profit:

$$L(p) = (p - 5) \cdot Q(p) = (p - 5)(1000 - 50p) = -50 p^2 + 1250 p - 5000$$

Quadratic in $p$ with $a = -50$ (concavity downward). Vertex: $p^* = -1250/(2 \cdot (-50)) = 12.5$. Maximum profit: $L(12.5) = 50 \cdot 12.5^2 \cdot ... = 2{,}812.50$ currency units. **Operational answer**: charging BRL 12.50 maximizes profit even by losing half the customers (375 units sold vs. potential of 750).

</Porta>
