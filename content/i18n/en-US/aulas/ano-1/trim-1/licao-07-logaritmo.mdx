<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Definition and properties">

<Definicao titulo="Logarithm">
For $a > 0, a \neq 1$ and $x > 0$:
$$\log_a x = y \iff a^y = x$$

- $\ln x = \log_e x$ (natural logarithm)
- $\log x = \log_{10} x$ (common logarithm — Brazilian/Portuguese convention)
</Definicao>

### Operational properties

<Equation numero="P1" explicacao="Log of a product = sum of logs.">
{`\\log_a (xy) = \\log_a x + \\log_a y`}
</Equation>

<Equation numero="P2" explicacao="Log of a quotient = difference of logs.">
{`\\log_a (x/y) = \\log_a x - \\log_a y`}
</Equation>

<Equation numero="P3" explicacao="Log of a power = exponent times log.">
{`\\log_a (x^p) = p \\cdot \\log_a x`}
</Equation>

<Equation numero="P4" explicacao="Change of base.">
{`\\log_a x = \\frac{\\log_b x}{\\log_b a}`}
</Equation>

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

You have a big number, like **a thousand**. You want to know: "**how many zeros does this number have?**". The answer is 3, because **a thousand = 1,000** (a 1 followed by 3 zeros).

And if it's **a million**? It has 6 zeros.

And **a billion**? It has 9 zeros.

A logarithm (in base 10) is a machine that does exactly that: **it counts the zeros**. Put in 1,000, it answers 3. Put in a million, it answers 6. Put in a billion, it answers 9.

Why is that useful? Because there are **gigantic** numbers in the world (stars, distances, the money of an entire country), and the logarithm puts them into human-speech-sized numbers.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

You know that $10^3 = 1{,}000$. So the logarithm of 1,000 in base 10 is 3. Done.

The question the logarithm answers is: **"to what power do I raise the base to get this number?"**

- $\log_{10}(100) = 2$, because $10^2 = 100$.
- $\log_{10}(1{,}000) = 3$.
- $\log_2(8) = 3$, because $2^3 = 8$.
- $\log_2(1{,}024) = 10$, because $2^{10} = 1{,}024$.

**Why does it exist?** Because it turns multiplication into addition. Before the calculator, multiplying big numbers was a nightmare — people used a "table of logarithms" to swap multiplication for an easy paper-and-pencil addition. For 300 years, that's how astronomers and engineers computed.

**Where does it appear today?**

- **pH** (chemistry): measures acidity. Soda has pH 3, pure water has pH 7. Each pH unit is 10× more acidic — logarithmic scale.
- **Decibel** (sound): measures intensity. Conversation = 60 dB, rock concert = 110 dB. Each 10 dB is 10× more sonic energy.
- **Richter magnitude** (earthquake): magnitude 5 vs. magnitude 7 = **100×** more energy released.
- **Stars** (astronomy): stellar magnitude is logarithmic.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

The logarithm answers the question: **"to what power do I raise the base to get the number?"**

- $\log_2 8 = 3$ because $2^3 = 8$.
- $\log_{10} 1000 = 3$ because $10^3 = 1000$.
- $\log_{10} 1 = 0$ because $10^0 = 1$.

The two most used:
- **$\ln$** (natural logarithm, base $e$): appears in calculus, ODEs, decay.
- **$\log$** (base 10): appears in physical scales (Richter, decibel, pH).

**Why does it exist?** Because it turns multiplication into addition. Before the calculator, multiplying 234 × 567 was work; applying log and adding was easier. (Log tables — 17th to 20th c. — were fundamental.)

**Modern applications:**
- **pH** = $-\log_{10}[H^+]$ — pH 7 = neutral.
- **Decibel** (dB) — logarithmic scale of sound intensity.
- **Earthquake magnitude** (Richter) — each unit = 10× more energy.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

Log and exponential are duals: $\log \exp = \exp \log = $ identity. In calculus:

- $\frac{d}{dx} \ln x = \frac{1}{x}$
- $\int \frac{1}{x} dx = \ln |x| + C$
- $\frac{d}{dx} a^x = a^x \ln a$

### Exponential decay and half-life

If $N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$, the **half-life** $\tau_{1/2}$ satisfies $N(\tau_{1/2}) = N_0/2$:
$$e^{-\lambda \tau_{1/2}} = 1/2 \Rightarrow \tau_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda}$$

### Log scales in engineering

- **Bode**: gains in dB, frequencies in log
- **Richter**: $M = \log_{10}(A/A_0)$
- **pH**: $-\log_{10}[H^+]$
- **Acoustic decibel**: $L = 10 \log_{10}(I/I_0)$ — threshold 0 dB ≈ $10^{-12}$ W/m²

The log scale "compresses" several orders of magnitude onto a navigable axis. A magnitude-7 quake releases **1,000,000,000 ($10^9$)** times more energy than a magnitude-1 tremor.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

In practice, **the logarithm is the operator that makes multiplication additive**. Every time you face repeated multiplication — compound rates, multiplicative chain processes, exponential distributions — you switch to log.

### Log in data science

- **Log-likelihood**: $\ell(\theta) = \log L(\theta) = \sum_i \log f(x_i; \theta)$. You always optimize the log-likelihood, not the likelihood — because the sum is numerically stable, the product is not.
- **Cross-entropy loss** in classification: $-\sum y_i \log \hat p_i$. Without log, gradients vanish.
- **Log-transform** in regression: a long-tailed variable (income, real-estate price) becomes approximately normal.

### Log in information and entropy

Shannon entropy: $H = -\sum p_i \log_2 p_i$. Measures information in **bits**. The surprise of an event with probability $p$ is $\log_2(1/p)$ — the less likely, the more surprise. This intuition (Shannon 1948) founded information theory.

### Log in human perception

**Weber-Fechner law**: the perceived intensity of a stimulus is logarithmic in the physical intensity. That's why decibel, pH, Richter, stellar magnitude are all logarithmic — they **mirror the human perceptual scale**.

### Caution: log of zero and negatives

$\log 0 = -\infty$ and $\log x$ is not defined for $x < 0$ (in $\mathbb{R}$). In code, always add $\epsilon$: `log(x + 1e-10)`. In statistics, soft-log: $\log(1 + x)$ is defined for all $x > -1$ — used in finance (log-return).

### Log-likelihood ratio in hypothesis testing

LRT: $\Lambda = -2 \log(L_0/L_1)$. Under the null hypothesis, $\Lambda \sim \chi^2$. **Wilks 1938** — basis of the likelihood-ratio test. Every frequentist parametric inference uses it.

### Discrete logarithm and cryptography

In a finite field $\mathbb{F}_p$, given $g, h$ finding $x$ such that $g^x = h \pmod p$ is the **discrete logarithm problem**. Computationally hard — basis of **Diffie-Hellman**, **DSA** and **ElGamal**. Elliptic curves ($\text{ECDLP}$) are the modern version used in SSL/TLS, blockchain, etc.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practice — 3 real scenarios">

### Scenario 1: pH and stomach acid

Gastric juice has $[H^+] \approx 10^{-2}$ mol/L. So $\text{pH} = -\log_{10}(10^{-2}) = 2$.

Compare with:
- Distilled water: $[H^+] = 10^{-7}$ → pH 7
- Soda (Coca-Cola): $[H^+] \approx 10^{-3}$ → pH 3
- Human blood: $[H^+] \approx 10^{-7.4}$ → pH 7.4

Acidity ratio of blood vs. stomach acid: $10^{7.4 - 2} \approx 250{,}000$. **Stomach acid is 250 thousand times more acidic than blood.** That's why the stomach has a mucus lining — without it, the acid itself would digest the wall.

### Scenario 2: Capacitor discharging to 10% (engineering)

A capacitor discharges as $V(t) = V_0 \e^{-t/\tau}$. When does it reach 10% of the initial voltage?

$$0.10 V_0 = V_0 \e^{-t/\tau} \Rightarrow t = \tau \ln(10) \approx 2.303 \tau$$

**Engineering rule of thumb**: a capacitor discharges 90% in **2.3 time constants**, and 99% in **4.6 time constants** ($\ln 100$). That 2.3 — coming from $\ln 10$ — is so common in circuits it becomes a memorized number.

### Scenario 3: Time for an investment to double (finance)

Investing $S_0$ at rate $r$ per year, with continuous compounding: $S(t) = S_0 \e^{rt}$. When does it double?

$$2 S_0 = S_0 \e^{rt} \Rightarrow t_\text{dupl} = \frac{\ln 2}{r} \approx \frac{0.693}{r}$$

**Rule of 70** (finance shortcut): $t_\text{dupl} \approx 70/r\%$. At 7% p.a., it doubles in ~10 years. At 14% p.a., it doubles in ~5 years. At 1% p.a. (real savings after inflation), it doubles in ~70 years.

**Operational implication**: 1% rate difference seems small, but over 30 retirement years, $10{,}000 \cdot 1.07^{30} \approx 76{,}000$ vs. $10{,}000 \cdot 1.08^{30} \approx 100{,}000$ — **24 thousand reais difference** for 1% per year. That's why a pension fund with 1% higher management fee than the competitor is infinitely expensive.

</Porta>
