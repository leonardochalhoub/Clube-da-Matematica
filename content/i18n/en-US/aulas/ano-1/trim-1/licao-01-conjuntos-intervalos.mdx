### Fundamental number sets

<Definicao titulo="Hierarchy of real numbers">

- **Naturals** $\mathbb{N} = \{0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots\}$. (Brazilian convention includes zero; French convention excludes it.)
- **Integers** $\mathbb{Z} = \{\ldots, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$ — closed under addition, subtraction, and multiplication.
- **Rationals** $\mathbb{Q} = \{p/q \mid p \in \mathbb{Z},\ q \in \mathbb{Z}_{\neq 0}\}$ — any number expressible as a ratio of integers.
- **Reals** $\mathbb{R}$ — completes the number line (fills the "gaps" such as $\sqrt{2}$, $\pi$, $e$).

</Definicao>

> "Qualquer número real corresponde a uma posição única na reta numérica. O inverso também é verdadeiro: cada localização na reta numérica corresponde a exatamente um número real." — [OpenStax College Algebra 2e, §1.1](https://openstax.org/books/college-algebra-2e/pages/1-1-real-numbers-algebra-essentials#Figure_01_01_001)

<Definicao titulo="Rational and irrational numbers">

> "O conjunto dos números racionais é escrito como $\lbrace m/n \mid m \text{ e } n \text{ são inteiros e } n \neq 0 \rbrace$. Percebe-se pela definição que números racionais são frações (ou quocientes) contendo inteiros tanto no numerador quanto no denominador, e o denominador nunca é 0." — [OpenStax College Algebra 2e, §1.1](https://openstax.org/books/college-algebra-2e/pages/1-1-real-numbers-algebra-essentials#fs-id11713028028)

> "Tais números são ditos *irracionais* porque não podem ser escritos como frações. Esses números compõem o conjunto dos números irracionais." — [OpenStax College Algebra 2e, §1.1](https://openstax.org/books/college-algebra-2e/pages/1-1-real-numbers-algebra-essentials#fs-id11713028028)

</Definicao>

### Operations between sets

<Definicao titulo="Union, intersection, difference, complement">

- **Union**: $A \cup B = \{x : x \in A \text{ ou } x \in B\}$
- **Intersection**: $A \cap B = \{x : x \in A \text{ e } x \in B\}$
- **Difference**: $A \setminus B = \{x \in A : x \notin B\}$
- **Complement** (in a universe $U$): $A^c = U \setminus A$

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

Imagine you have a box with **toys**. Another box with **books**.

- **Union** (∪) is combining both boxes into one. Contains toys AND books.
- **Intersection** (∩) is what's in BOTH boxes at once. Usually empty — a toy is not a book.
- But if one box has "**red balls**" and the other has "**small balls**", the intersection is the balls that are **red AND small**.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

Mathematicians love organizing numbers into families.

- The family of **naturals** $\mathbb{N}$ is just what you use to count things: 0, 1, 2, 3, ...
- The family of **integers** $\mathbb{Z}$ adds the negatives: ..., −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, ...
- The family of **rationals** $\mathbb{Q}$ adds fractions: 1/2, 3/4, −5/7, ...
- The family of **reals** $\mathbb{R}$ adds weird numbers like **π** (3.14159...) and $\sqrt{2}$ (1.41421...).

And **intervals** are short ways to say "all numbers between this and that":
- `[0, 5]` means "every number from 0 to 5, **including** 0 and 5".
- `(0, 5)` means "every number from 0 to 5, **without** 0 and without 5".

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

This is the lesson that establishes the **language** we will use over the next 3 years. All advanced mathematics — calculus, linear algebra, statistics — uses this notation. Without it, it's like trying to read a physics book without knowing the letters.

**The 4 central number sets:**

| Symbol | Name | Example |
|---|---|---|
| $\mathbb{N}$ | naturals | 0, 1, 2, 3, ... |
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | integers | ..., −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, ... |
| $\mathbb{Q}$ | rationals | 1/2, 3/4, −7/5, 0.333... = 1/3 |
| $\mathbb{R}$ | reals | all of the above + $\pi$, $\sqrt{2}$, $e$ |

An important observation: **not every real number is rational**. Irrational numbers (such as $\sqrt{2}$ and $\pi$) are real numbers that CANNOT be written as a fraction of integers. This is a theorem known since Pythagoras (5th century BC) — an elegant proof you'll see in exercise 1.27 of this lesson.

**Intervals** are abbreviations for "all real numbers between these limits":

- `[3, 7]` = $\{x \in \mathbb{R} : 3 \leq x \leq 7\}$ (closed, with endpoints included)
- `(3, 7)` = $\{x : 3 < x < 7\}$ (open, without the endpoints)

When the teacher writes on the board $x \in [3, 7]$, they are saying: "x is any real number between 3 and 7, including 3 and 7". Very compact. Compare with writing that out in full.

<Insight>

**Why is there a difference between $[3, 7]$ and $(3, 7)$?** Because in advanced mathematics, "including the point" and "approaching it without reaching it" are different phenomena. When you study **limits** in a few months, you'll see that $\lim_{x \to 3^+}$ asks what happens *when x approaches 3 without being 3*. The difference between open and closed starts here, now, when you're 15 years old.

</Insight>

</Porta>

### Pontos críticos

**1. Does ℕ include 0?** Brazilian (BNCC) and French convention: YES. American and many classical conventions: NO. I adopt **0 ∈ ℕ** throughout this program, aligned with BNCC. When reading foreign books, verify the author's convention.

**2. Brazilian vs. Anglo-Saxon interval notation:**
- BR: $]a, b[$ for open, $[a, b]$ for closed (Bourbaki notation inherited from France)
- Anglo: $(a, b)$ for open, $[a, b]$ for closed

I adopt **Anglo notation** (parentheses for open) for compatibility with 90% of the open books referenced in `livros/CATALOG.md`.

> "Neste livro, usaremos a notation de intervalo com parênteses para intervals abertos e colchetes para intervals fechados, como é comum na literatura de língua inglesa." — [Stitz-Zeager, Precalculus, Cap. 1](https://www.stitz-zeager.com/szprecalculus07042013.pdf)

**3. Density of ℚ in ℝ.** Between any two distinct reals $a < b$, there exists a rational $q$ with $a < q < b$. **Proof** (Exercise 1.29): use the Archimedean property — for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $1/n < \varepsilon$.

**4. Finite vs. countably infinite vs. uncountable sets.** Cantor (1874) proved that ℝ is **uncountable** but ℚ is **countable** — both are infinite, but with distinct cardinalities. You will use this fact in university analysis (Calculus IV / Real Analysis).

### Properties of Real Numbers

> "A propriedade comutativa da adição afirma que os números podem ser adicionados em qualquer ordem sem afetar a soma." — [OpenStax College Algebra 2e, §1.1](https://openstax.org/books/college-algebra-2e/pages/1-1-real-numbers-algebra-essentials#fs-id11713028028)

> "A propriedade distributiva afirma que o produto de um fator por uma soma é a soma do fator por cada termo da soma." — [OpenStax College Algebra 2e, §1.1](https://openstax.org/books/college-algebra-2e/pages/1-1-real-numbers-algebra-essentials#fs-id11713028028)

### Cross-domain connections

- **Calculus**: domain of a function is always a subset of ℝ.
- **Probability**: sample space is a set.
- **Linear Algebra**: vectors are tuples in ℝⁿ or ℂⁿ.
- **Computing**: primitive types `int`, `float`, `bool` correspond to ℤ (with bounds), ℝ (with finite precision), $\{T, F\}$.

### Numerical example

Solve $|2x - 3| \leq 5$ and express the result in interval notation:

$$|2x - 3| \leq 5 \iff -5 \leq 2x - 3 \leq 5 \iff -2 \leq 2x \leq 8 \iff -1 \leq x \leq 4$$

Answer: $x \in [-1, 4]$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

Set theory is the foundation ZFC (Zermelo-Fraenkel + Axiom of Choice) that underpins all modern mathematics. The "intuitive" definitions we present in this lesson have well-known pitfalls:

- **Russell's Paradox**: the set of all sets that do not contain themselves. If it contains itself, then it doesn't. If it doesn't, then it does. This broke Cantor's naive set theory (1901) and led Zermelo to axiomatize the theory with restricted comprehension.

- **Continuum Hypothesis**: Cantor conjectured that no cardinality exists between $|\mathbb{N}|$ and $|\mathbb{R}|$. Gödel (1940) and Cohen (1963) proved that the hypothesis is **independent** of ZFC axioms — both it and its negation are consistent with ZFC.

- **Axiom of Choice**: seemingly innocent, but implies strange results like Banach-Tarski (decompose a sphere and reassemble two copies of the original). The decision to adopt AC is practical but philosophically delicate.

In professional practice (engineering, applied science, finance), none of this appears. You use $\mathbb{R}^n$ as Euclidean space and move on. But if one day you pursue graduate study in Analysis, Logic, or Set Theory, you will return to these paradoxes.

### What is "real" anyway?

Computationally, ℝ does not exist — every digital implementation uses **floating point** (IEEE 754), with ~7 digits of precision (float32) or ~15 (float64). Floating-point errors propagate in numerical computation (covered in Term 10 with REAMAT). When you write `0.1 + 0.2` in Python, you get `0.30000000000000004`. $\mathbb{Q} \subsetneq \mathbb{R}$ is a mathematical abstraction; the computer works on a finite subset much smaller than $\mathbb{Q}$.

</Porta>

## Worked examples

<Exemplo titulo="Listing elements of a set (application)" numero="1">

**Problem:** List the set $A = \{x \in \mathbb{Z} : -2 < x \leq 3\}$ in roster notation.

**Strategy:** The problem asks us to list the elements of a set. The set notation gives us two pieces of information:
1.  What number set the elements belong to: $\mathbb{Z}$ (integers).
2.  What conditions those elements must satisfy: $-2 < x \leq 3$.
Let's combine this information to identify all integers that fit.

**Solution:**

1.  **Identify the number type:** The elements $x$ must be integers ($\mathbb{Z}$).
2.  **Analyze the first condition:** $x > -2$. This means $x$ must be greater than $-2$. The integers satisfying this are $-1, 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$.
3.  **Analyze the second condition:** $x \leq 3$. This means $x$ must be less than or equal to $3$. The integers satisfying this are $\ldots, 1, 2, 3$.
4.  **Combine the conditions:** We need integers $x$ that are *simultaneously* greater than $-2$ AND less than or equal to $3$.
    *   Integers greater than $-2$: $\{-1, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, \ldots\}$
    *   Integers less than or equal to $3$: $\{\ldots, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3\}$
    *   The intersection of these two sets is $\{-1, 0, 1, 2, 3\}$.

**Verification:** All listed numbers are integers.
- $-1$ is greater than $-2$ and less than or equal to $3$. (OK)
- $0$ is greater than $-2$ and less than or equal to $3$. (OK)
- $1$ is greater than $-2$ and less than or equal to $3$. (OK)
- $2$ is greater than $-2$ and less than or equal to $3$. (OK)
- $3$ is greater than $-2$ and less than or equal to $3$. (OK)
No other integer between $-2$ and $3$ (excluding $-2$, including $3$) has been left out.

</Exemplo>

<Exemplo titulo="Intersection of intervals (intermediate)" numero="2">

**Problem:** Given intervals $A = [-3, 7)$ and $B = (1, 10]$, find $A \cap B$.

**Strategy:** The intersection of two sets includes only elements that belong to *both* sets. Again, visualization on the number line is the most effective tool.

**Solution:**

1.  **Visualize interval $A$:** $A = [-3, 7)$ goes from -3 (included) to 7 (excluded).
    ```
    ←—————[----------)————→
         -3          7
    ```
2.  **Visualize interval $B$:** $B = (1, 10]$ goes from 1 (excluded) to 10 (included).
    ```
    ←——————————(----------]————→
               1          10
    ```
3.  **Combine for the intersection:** We need to find the region where the two intervals overlap.
    *   The leftmost point where both are present: $1$. But $1$ is excluded in $B$, so it is excluded in the intersection.
    *   The rightmost point where both are present: $7$. But $7$ is excluded in $A$, so it is excluded in the intersection.
    *   The overlapping region is between $1$ (excluded) and $7$ (excluded).

$$ A \cap B = (1, 7) $$

**Verification:**
- A point in the intersection: $x=3$. It is in $A$ (since $-3 \leq 3 < 7$) and in $B$ (since $1 < 3 \leq 10$). (OK)
- A point that is only in $A$: $x=-2$. Not in $B$. (OK)
- A point that is only in $B$: $x=8$. Not in $A$. (OK)
- The endpoints: $1$ is not in $B$, $7$ is not in $A$. Both excluded in the intersection.
The answer is consistent.


**Fonte.** [Stitz–Zeager — Precalculus §1.2](https://www.stitz-zeager.com/szprecalculus07042013.pdf) — licença CC-BY-NC-SA.
</Exemplo>

<Exemplo titulo="Absolute value inequality, type |u| ≤ c (intermediate)" numero="3">

**Problem:** Solve the inequality $|x - 4| \leq 3$ and express the solution in interval notation.

**Strategy:** Absolute value inequalities of the type $|u| \leq c$ (where $c > 0$) can be rewritten as a compound inequality: $-c \leq u \leq c$. We will apply this property and then solve for $x$.

**Solution:**

1.  **Rewrite the inequality:** The absolute value property allows us to transform $|x - 4| \leq 3$ into:
    $$ -3 \leq x - 4 \leq 3 $$
2.  **Isolate $x$:** To isolate $x$, we add $4$ to all parts of the inequality:
    $$ -3 + 4 \leq x - 4 + 4 \leq 3 + 4 $$
    $$ 1 \leq x \leq 7 $$
3.  **Express in interval notation:** The inequality $1 \leq x \leq 7$ means that $x$ can be any real number between 1 and 7, including 1 and 7.

$$ \text{Solution: } [1, 7] $$

**Verification:**
- Test a point within the interval: $x=5$. $|5 - 4| = |1| = 1$. $1 \leq 3$. (OK)
- Test an endpoint: $x=1$. $|1 - 4| = |-3| = 3$. $3 \leq 3$. (OK)
- Test the other endpoint: $x=7$. $|7 - 4| = |3| = 3$. $3 \leq 3$. (OK)
- Test a point outside the interval: $x=0$. $|0 - 4| = |-4| = 4$. $4 \not\leq 3$. (OK)
- Test a point outside the interval: $x=8$. $|8 - 4| = |4| = 4$. $4 \not\leq 3$. (OK)
The solution is correct.


**Fonte.** [OpenStax — College Algebra 2e §1.7](https://openstax.org/books/college-algebra-2e/pages/1-7-linear-inequalities-and-absolute-value-inequalities) — licença CC-BY 4.0.
</Exemplo>

<Exemplo titulo="Quadratic inequality (advanced)" numero="4">

**Problem:** Determine the solution set of the inequality $x^2 - x - 6 > 0$ in interval notation.

**Strategy:** To solve a quadratic inequality, we first find the roots of the associated quadratic equation ($x^2 - x - 6 = 0$). These roots divide the real line into intervals. Then we test a point in each interval to see where the inequality is satisfied.

**Solution:**

1.  **Find the roots of the associated equation:** $x^2 - x - 6 = 0$.
    *   We can factor the equation: $(x - 3)(x + 2) = 0$.
    *   The roots are $x = 3$ and $x = -2$.
2.  **Divide the real line into intervals:** The roots $-2$ and $3$ divide the real line into three intervals:
    *   $(-\infty, -2)$
    *   $(-2, 3)$
    *   $(3, +\infty)$
3.  **Test a point in each interval:**
    *   **Interval $(-\infty, -2)$:** Choose $x = -3$.
        $(-3)^2 - (-3) - 6 = 9 + 3 - 6 = 6$. Since $6 > 0$, this interval *satisfies* the inequality.
    *   **Interval $(-2, 3)$:** Choose $x = 0$.
        $(0)^2 - (0) - 6 = -6$. Since $-6 \not> 0$, this interval *does not satisfy* the inequality.
    *   **Interval $(3, +\infty)$:** Choose $x = 4$.
        $(4)^2 - (4) - 6 = 16 - 4 - 6 = 6$. Since $6 > 0$, this interval *satisfies* the inequality.
4.  **Combine the intervals that satisfy:** The solution is the union of intervals where the inequality is true. Note that the inequality is strictly greater than 0, so the roots are not included.

$$ \text{Solution: } (-\infty, -2) \cup (3, +\infty) $$

**Verification:**
- The parabola $y = x^2 - x - 6$ opens upward (the coefficient of $x^2$ is positive). This means the function values are positive "outside" the roots and negative "between" the roots. The solution found is consistent with a parabola graph.


**Fonte.** [OpenStax — College Algebra 2e §3.6](https://openstax.org/books/college-algebra-2e/pages/3-6-other-types-of-equations) — licença CC-BY 4.0.
</Exemplo>

<Exemplo titulo="Modeling with point exclusion (real modeling)" numero="5">

**Problem:** A temperature sensor operates correctly in an environment where the temperature $T$ (in Celsius) must be between $10\,°C$ (inclusive) and $40\,°C$ (exclusive). Additionally, due to a specific requirement, the temperature cannot be exactly $25\,°C$. Express the valid operating range for $T$ as a union of intervals.

**Strategy:** First, define the base operating interval. Then remove the specific point that is not allowed. Removing a single point from an interval can result in two disjoint intervals.

**Solution:**

1.  **Define the base interval:** The temperature must be between $10\,°C$ (inclusive) and $40\,°C$ (exclusive). This is represented by the interval $[10, 40)$.
2.  **Identify the point to be excluded:** The temperature cannot be $25\,°C$.
3.  **Remove the point from the base interval:** The number $25$ is within the interval $[10, 40)$. When removing $25$, the interval is divided into two parts:
    *   All numbers from $10$ (inclusive) to $25$ (exclusive): $[10, 25)$.
    *   All numbers from $25$ (exclusive) to $40$ (exclusive): $(25, 40)$.
4.  **Express as a union of intervals:** The valid operating range is the union of these two disjoint intervals.

$$ \text{Operating range: } [10, 25) \cup (25, 40) $$

**Verification:**
- $T=10$: Valid. It is in the first interval.
- $T=20$: Valid. It is in the first interval.
- $T=25$: Not valid. It is not in either interval.
- $T=30$: Valid. It is in the second interval.
- $T=40$: Not valid. Excluded from the original interval.
The solution is correct and meets all conditions.


**Fonte.** [Yoshiwara — Modeling, Functions, and Graphs cap. 1](https://textbooks.aimath.org/textbooks/approved-textbooks/yoshiwara/) — licença livre.
</Exemplo>

<Exercicio numero="1.57" dificuldade="desafio"
  solucao={<>Na etapa <Eq>{`n`}</Eq>, cada um dos <Eq>{`2^{n-1}`}</Eq> intervals restantes da etapa anterior é dividido em três, e o terço médio é removido.
