<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Definition and properties">

<Definicao titulo="Affine function">
$f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is **affine** if there exist $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $f(x) = ax + b$.
</Definicao>

- $a$: **slope** (angular coefficient, inclination)
- $b$: **y-intercept** (linear coefficient)
- Graph: a straight line. $a > 0$: increasing. $a < 0$: decreasing. $a = 0$: constant.

<Equation
  numero="1"
  audioTexto="Equation one. F of x two minus f of x one, divided by x two minus x one, equals a. The rate of change between any two points of an affine function is constant and equal to the slope."
  explicacao="Rate of change between two points of the line. For an affine function, this value is CONSTANT — it does not depend on which pair of points you choose. It is this constancy that characterizes the affine function among all others.">
{`\\frac{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}{x_2 - x_1} = a`}
</Equation>

### Zero of the function and intercept

$f(x) = 0 \iff x = -b/a$ (when $a \neq 0$). The pair $(0, b)$ is the **vertical intercept**. The pair $(-b/a, 0)$ is the **zero** (or horizontal intercept).

### Uniqueness theorem from two points

<Teorema titulo="Two points determine an affine function">
Given two distinct points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ with $x_1 \neq x_2$, there exists a unique affine function $f(x) = ax + b$ that passes through both. The coefficients are:
$$a = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}, \quad b = y_1 - a x_1$$
</Teorema>

**Proof (sketch).** Existence: define $a$ by the formula above and $b = y_1 - a x_1$. We verify $f(x_1) = y_1$ by construction, and $f(x_2) = a(x_2 - x_1) + y_1 = (y_2 - y_1) + y_1 = y_2$. Uniqueness: if $g(x) = a' x + b'$ also satisfies $g(x_i) = y_i$, then $a' = (y_2 - y_1)/(x_2 - x_1) = a$ and $b' = y_1 - a' x_1 = b$. ∎

### Composition and operations

Let $f(x) = a_1 x + b_1$ and $g(x) = a_2 x + b_2$. Then:

- **Sum**: $(f + g)(x) = (a_1 + a_2) x + (b_1 + b_2)$ — affine, with summed slopes.
- **Composition**: $(f \circ g)(x) = a_1 (a_2 x + b_2) + b_1 = a_1 a_2 x + (a_1 b_2 + b_1)$ — affine, with multiplied slopes.
- **Inverse** (if $a_1 \neq 0$): $f^{-1}(y) = (y - b_1)/a_1$ — also affine, with slope $1/a_1$.

The set of **invertible** affine functions ($a \neq 0$) under composition forms a group — the structure $(\mathbb{R}^* \times \mathbb{R}, \circ)$. This observation will be used in linear algebra (Lesson 31+) and in affine geometry.

### Worked numerical example

**Problem.** Determine the affine function whose graph passes through $(2, 5)$ and $(7, -10)$.

**Solution.** Applying the theorem:
$$a = \frac{-10 - 5}{7 - 2} = \frac{-15}{5} = -3, \quad b = 5 - (-3)(2) = 5 + 6 = 11.$$

Therefore $f(x) = -3x + 11$. **Check:** $f(2) = -6 + 11 = 5$ ✓ and $f(7) = -21 + 11 = -10$ ✓.

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

Imagine a staircase. Each step has **the same height**.

- If each step is 20 cm, after 1 step you have climbed 20 cm.
- After 2 steps, you've climbed 40 cm.
- After 5 steps, you've climbed 100 cm = 1 meter.

The height you are at depends **on how many steps you climbed**. And it always grows **the same amount** per step.

This is an affine function. The height is $f(n) = 20 \cdot n$ where $n$ is the number of steps.

If the staircase started on a floor 50 cm above the ground (like a small platform), it would be $f(n) = 20n + 50$. The 50 is the **starting point** (where you are before climbing anything). The 20 is **how much you climb per step**.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

You go to the cinema. The ticket costs **BRL 30 fixed**. Each bag of popcorn costs **BRL 8**. If you buy $n$ bags:

$$\text{total spent} = 30 + 8n$$

This is an affine function! Where:
- The **30** is the spending **even if you buy 0 popcorns** (ticket). This is the **b** in the formula $f(x) = ax + b$.
- The **8** is how much the bill **increases for each additional popcorn**. This is the **a** in the formula.

In any function of the type "has a fixed part + a part that grows at the same rate", you have an affine function. Other examples:

- **Uber bill**: flag drop (BRL 6 fixed) + BRL 2 per km. ⟹ $f(d) = 6 + 2d$.
- **Electricity bill**: availability charge (BRL 25) + BRL 0.80 per kWh. ⟹ $f(k) = 25 + 0.80\,k$.
- **Scooter rental**: BRL 3 to unlock + BRL 0.80 per minute.

The line on the graph **represents** this formula visually. The steeper the line, the faster your bill grows.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

Affine function is the simplest "interesting" type of function — and it's the one that opens the door to calculus.

The rule is: $f(x) = ax + b$. Put in a value of $x$, multiply by $a$, add $b$, out comes $f(x)$. Just like that.

**Why is it important?** Because the graph is a **line**, and the slope of that line is exactly $a$. When you walk **1 step to the right** on the $x$ axis, you go up **a steps on the y axis**. Always. At any point on the line. This **constancy** is what makes the affine function "linear" in behavior.

In a few lessons you'll study **instantaneous rate of change** (the derivative). And you'll see that: for an affine function, the derivative is constant and equal to $a$. For other functions (parabolas, exponentials), the "slope" changes with $x$ — and that's exactly where calculus appears. But for a line, it's easy: the slope is $a$, end of story.

**Concrete example.** You drive a car at constant speed of 60 km/h. The distance traveled is $d(t) = 60t$ (with $t$ in hours). The "slope" $a = 60$ is the speed. Makes sense — the steeper the line, the faster you go.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

The affine function is the point at which most physical models are **locally linearized**. Even non-linear phenomena (free fall with air resistance, non-Newtonian dynamics) are, to first order, approximated by affines — that's what the derivative formalizes.

### Reduced form vs. general form

- Reduced: $y = ax + b$
- General: $Ax + By + C = 0$
- Parametric: $\{(x_0 + t\vec{u}_x, y_0 + t\vec{u}_y) : t \in \mathbb{R}\}$

Each is useful in different contexts. In CAD, parametric. In linear optimization, general. In statistics (regression), reduced.

### Line fitting (preview of linear regression)

Given $(x_1, y_1), \ldots, (x_n, y_n)$, what is the line that best passes through them? **Least squares**:

$$a = \frac{n \sum x_i y_i - \sum x_i \sum y_i}{n \sum x_i^2 - (\sum x_i)^2}, \quad b = \bar{y} - a \bar{x}$$

You'll see in detail in Term 11 (inferential statistics).

### Applications (partial list)

- **Hooke**: $F = kx$ — elastic force as a function of elongation (affine with $b = 0$, that is, linear)
- **Ohm**: $V = RI$ — voltage as a function of current (linear)
- **Celsius → Fahrenheit conversion**: $F = 1.8C + 32$ (affine, $a = 1.8$, $b = 32$)
- **Hourly equation of uniform motion**: $s(t) = s_0 + vt$ (affine in $t$, $b = s_0$, $a = v$)

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

In practice, "**linear is local**". Most engineering technical activity involving models is, at the end of the day, line fitting:

- **Parameter estimation** in control engineering: plant identification via sinusoidal excitation and linear fit in the Laplace domain.
- **Taylor linearization**: any smooth function around $x_0$ is locally affine — $f(x) \approx f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x - x_0)$. All stability analysis of dynamical systems depends on this.
- **OLS linear regression**: the line that minimizes squared error of $n$ points is the basis of the econometrician (GDP ~ investment), the pharmacist (dose ~ response), the agronomist (rainfall ~ harvest).
- **Logistic regression**: the "linearization" of log-odds — log of the probability ratio is an affine function of the covariates. Every classifying AI (spam filter, fraud detection) has logistic regression at its core.

### Caution: heteroscedasticity and spurious linearity

In production, fitting $y = ax + b$ to data without checking residuals is a recipe for disaster. The prerequisites of regression (Gauss-Markov):

1. Linearity of the actual relationship — not just the data.
2. Residuals with constant variance (homoscedasticity).
3. Uncorrelated residuals.
4. Residuals with mean zero.

When these fail, $\hat a$ remains an estimator, but loses efficiency or consistency properties. **OLS without checking is cargo cult**.

### Linearization vs. transformation

When the relationship is NOT affine, there are two approaches: (a) fit with a non-linear model, (b) **transform variables** until it becomes affine. Classic examples:

- $y = a x^b$ ⟹ $\ln y = \ln a + b \ln x$ — log-log linear (power law)
- $y = a \e^{bx}$ ⟹ $\ln y = \ln a + bx$ — semi-log linear (exponential)
- $y = a/x$ ⟹ $y = a \cdot (1/x)$ — affine in $1/x$

Plotting on log-log or semi-log scale and seeing if it becomes a line is the first diagnostic tool.

</Porta>

### Scenario 1: CEMIG electricity bill (Minas Gerais, April/2026)

Residential B1 class tariff (estimated): BRL 0.82/kWh + yellow flag BRL 0.02/kWh + 25% ICMS on consumption + fixed availability fee BRL 35.40 (three-phase residence). For consumption $k$ (kWh):

$$C(k) = (0.82 + 0.02) \cdot 1.25 \cdot k + 35.40 = 1.05 k + 35.40$$

**Affine function**, with $a = 1.05$ BRL/kWh and $b = 35.40$ BRL. For $k = 200$ kWh, the bill is $C(200) = 245.40$ BRL.

**Marginal analysis**: each additional kWh costs exactly BRL 1.05. The "fixed portion" of BRL 35.40 does not depend on consumption — that's what one pays **even traveling the entire month with the breaker off**.

### Scenario 2: Crack propagation rate (fracture mechanics)

In fatigue tests (Paris curve), the propagation of a crack in metal can be linearized as:

$$\log(da/dN) = \log C + m \log(\Delta K)$$

where $da/dN$ is crack speed per cycle, $\Delta K$ is the variation of the stress intensity factor, and $C, m$ are material constants. On log-log scale this is a line with slope $m$ — called the **Paris exponent**.

For typical structural steel, $m \approx 3$. This means: doubling $\Delta K$ multiplies the propagation speed by $2^3 = 8$ — drastically non-linear behavior that becomes linear on log-log scale.

### Scenario 3: Aggregate demand curve (microeconomics)

The demand for a good is often modeled as an affine function: $Q(p) = \alpha - \beta p$ where $\alpha, \beta > 0$ and $p$ is the price. The price elasticity of demand is $\epsilon = -\beta p / Q$ — varies along the curve, even though the function is affine.

Numerical example: $Q(p) = 1000 - 25 p$. For $p = 20$, $Q = 500$, elasticity $|\epsilon| = 25 \cdot 20 / 500 = 1$ (unitary). For $p = 30$, $Q = 250$, elasticity $|\epsilon| = 3$ (elastic). For $p = 10$, $Q = 750$, $|\epsilon| = 0.33$ (inelastic). **Operational observation**: a company that raises the price of an inelastic good gains revenue; for an elastic good, it loses.
