<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Definition and interpretation">

<Definicao titulo="Average rate of change of f on [a, b]">

For $f$ defined on $[a, b]$ with $a < b$:

$$\text{ARC}(f, [a, b]) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}$$

</Definicao>

### Geometric interpretation

It is the **slope of the secant line** to the graph of $f$ through the points $(a, f(a))$ and $(b, f(b))$.

### Special cases

- $f$ affine ($f(x) = mx + n$): ARC is constant and equal to $m$, regardless of the interval.
- $f$ quadratic: ARC varies depending on the interval.

### The question that opens calculus

**What if $\Delta x \to 0$?** The secant line "becomes" a tangent line, and the ARC converges to the **instantaneous rate of change** — which is exactly the **derivative** $f'(a)$.

This is the topic of Trim 5 (limits) and Trim 6 (derivatives). This lesson is the antechamber.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

You leave home for school. The school is **600 meters** from your house. It takes you **10 minutes** walking.

On **average**, you walked $600 / 10 = 60$ meters per minute. That is the **average speed**.

But on the way you had to wait 2 minutes at the traffic light. There you were stopped, walking 0 meters in those 2 minutes. At other times you walked fast to make up time, going 80 or 90 m/min.

Speed **on average** was 60 m/min. But speed **at each instant** changed all the time.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

You take an elevator from floor 0 to floor 20. It takes 30 seconds.

**Average speed** = (20 floors) / (30 s) = 0.667 floors/second.

But at the start the elevator accelerates (small speed), in the middle it goes at high speed, and at the end it decelerates. Speed **changes all the time**.

The average rate of change is the speed that holds **on average**. It answers the question "how far did I go divided by how long it took". It ignores all the details in between.

The "**instantaneous speed**" — the one on the car's speedometer at one instant — is what you get when the time interval becomes **infinitely short**. This calculation is called a **derivative**, and it is what all this high-school math is building toward. For now, **average** rate is the way.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

This lesson is different. **This is where calculus begins.**

The average rate of change answers the question: "**by how much did $y$ change relative to $x$, on average, on this interval?**".

Speed is the perfect example. If you drive from São Paulo to Campinas (98 km) in 1h20min ($= 1.33$ h), your **average speed** is:

$$v_{\text{average}} = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} = \frac{98 \text{ km}}{1{,}33 \text{ h}} \approx 73{,}5 \text{ km/h}$$

But during the trip you went through stretches at 110 km/h and stretches at 30 km/h. Speed was not 73.5 the whole time — it was 73.5 **on average**. **Instantaneous speed** at any given moment can be quite different.

Here is the bridge that opens calculus:

> **What if I take intervals $[t, t + \Delta t]$ that are smaller and smaller? Does average speed approach instantaneous speed?**

Answer: YES. And this "taking smaller and smaller intervals" is what is called a **limit**. And the result is the **derivative** — the central concept of calculus.

You don't need to understand the derivative now. But remember: **the derivative is the instantaneous rate of change**. Everything you do in this lesson with $\Delta y / \Delta x$ will become $dy/dx$ a few months from now.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

This lesson is the practical fulfillment of **Bruner's principle — the spiral curriculum** that guides the Clube's program. Rate of change appears here in "average" form (algebraic, no limit); it reappears in Trim 5 as a formal limit; comes back in Trim 6 as the derivative; resurfaces in Trim 9 as the integral (FTC) and in Trim 11 as linear regression (statistical estimate of the derivative). It is **one concept, five incarnations**.

### Cauchy connection

Augustin-Louis Cauchy (1821) defined the derivative as the limit of the difference quotient:

$$f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}$$

This quotient $\frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}$ is exactly the ARC on the interval $[a, a+h]$. Cauchy formalizes "$h \to 0$" via $\eps$-$\delta$ — you will see this in Trim 5.

### ARC and the Mean Value Theorem (MVT — don't confuse)

For $f$ continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$, there exists $c \in (a,b)$ with:

$$f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}$$

That is: the derivative at some interior point is exactly equal to the ARC. A **non-trivial** result that you prove in Trim 6 or Analysis. Note: in Portuguese, both "Teorema do Valor Médio" and "Taxa de Variação Média" share the acronym TVM; the theorem guarantees the existence of a point where the tangent is parallel to the secant.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

The ARC is the universal **discretization** of the derivative. In any application where you compute a derivative **from data**, you are computing an ARC.

### Finite differences in numerical methods

To solve an ODE numerically, one approximates $\dot y(t) \approx (y(t+h) - y(t))/h$ — **forward difference**, first order. Or $\dot y(t) \approx (y(t+h) - y(t-h))/(2h)$ — **central difference**, second-order more accurate.

These schemes form the basis of Euler, Runge-Kutta, Crank-Nicolson — every numerical integration of differential equations (REAMAT ch. 5).

### Estimating derivatives from noisy data

In the lab, you have $\{(t_i, y_i)\}$ with noise. Computing $(y_{i+1} - y_i)/(t_{i+1} - t_i)$ amplifies the noise (the derivative is a "high-pass filter"). Solutions:

- **Smoothing** (Savitzky-Golay filter): fits a local polynomial before differentiating.
- **Local regression** (LOESS): moving regression line.
- **Kalman filter**: estimates derivative and state simultaneously.

### ARC in finance

**Simple return** $r_t = (P_t - P_{t-1})/P_{t-1}$ is exactly the ARC of the price. **Log return** $\log(P_t/P_{t-1})$ is an ARC in log space. In practice, log-returns are preferred: they sum, are tractable under normality, and $\log(1+r) \approx r$ for small $r$.

### ARC in ML/DL

**Numerical gradient**: $\nabla f \approx (f(x + h) - f(x))/h$. Used to verify back-propagation. Always compare analytical gradient (autograd) vs numerical before trusting.

**Adversarial gradient**: to generate adversarial examples, compute the gradient of the loss with respect to the input pixels — the ARC of the classification function in image space.

### Caution: machine precision

For very small $h$, $f(x+h) - f(x)$ suffers **catastrophic cancellation** in float64. Truncation error ($O(h)$) decreases with $h$, but rounding error ($\sim \epsilon_\text{machine}/h$) grows. Optimum: $h \approx \sqrt{\epsilon} \approx 10^{-8}$. Below that, numerical noise dominates.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practice — 3 real scenarios">

### Scenario 1: Average car speed with toll-booth radar

A toll booth in SP records passage at km 50 at 14:30 and km 200 at 15:50. Distance 150 km, time 1h20min = 4/3 h. Average speed:

$$v_\text{average} = \frac{150}{4/3} = 112{,}5 \text{ km/h}$$

Highway limit: 110 km/h. **Fined**, even without radar catching them in motion — it is the **ARC of position** that proves the violation.

### Scenario 2: Annualized growth of Brazil's GDP

Brazil GDP (in real constant 2025 values): R$ 5.5 trillion in 2010, R$ 8.3 trillion in 2020. Compound growth:

$$r = \left(\frac{8{,}3}{5{,}5}\right)^{1/10} - 1 = 1{,}0413 - 1 = 0{,}0413 = 4{,}13\% \text{ p.a.}$$

**Average annualized real growth: 4.13%.** Use this value to compare with inflation, real interest rates, real minimum wage. **Do not use linear ARC** $(8{,}3 - 5{,}5)/10 = 0{,}28$ trillion/year — that number is not a rate, it is absolute change.

### Scenario 3: Approximate marginal cost

A company has $C(q) = 0{,}5 q^2 + 30 q + 200$ (R$). Compute the cost of producing one more unit when 100 are already produced.

**Method 1: ARC on $[100, 101]$.** $C(100) = 5{,}000 + 3{,}000 + 200 = 8{,}200$. $C(101) = 5{,}100{,}5 + 3{,}030 + 200 = 8{,}330{,}5$. ARC = 130.5 R$/unit.

**Method 2: derivative (preview)**. $C'(q) = q + 30$. $C'(100) = 130$ R$/unit.

**Comparison**: the ARC (130.5) is a **good estimate** of the marginal cost (130). The difference comes from the quadratic term. For smaller $\Delta q$, the ARC gets even closer. This is the essence of the derivative concept.

</Porta>
