<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Proofs and use">

### Law of sines

<Equation explicacao="Holds for any triangle (acute, obtuse, right). R is the radius of the circumscribed circle.">
{`\\frac{a}{\\sin A} = \\frac{b}{\\sin B} = \\frac{c}{\\sin C} = 2R`}
</Equation>

**Proof** (for an acute triangle): construct the altitude $h$ from vertex $C$ to side $\overline{AB}$. Then $h = b \sin A = a \sin B$. Therefore $a/\sin A = b/\sin B$. Same argument for $c$. ∎

**Special case** (right angle at $C$): $\sin C = 1$, so $c = 2R$ — the hypotenuse is the diameter of the circumscribed circle. **Thales' theorem** (geometric).

### Law of cosines

<Equation explicacao="Generalizes Pythagoras. When C = 90°, cos C = 0 and we recover c² = a² + b².">
{`c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\\cos C`}
</Equation>

**Proof**: by the dot product of vectors $\vec{CB} - \vec{CA} = \vec{AB}$:
$$|\vec{AB}|^2 = |\vec{CB}|^2 + |\vec{CA}|^2 - 2 \vec{CB} \cdot \vec{CA}$$

Since $\vec{CB} \cdot \vec{CA} = |\vec{CB}||\vec{CA}|\cos C = ab \cos C$, we obtain $c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab \cos C$. ∎

### When to use each law

| You have | You want | Use |
|---|---|---|
| 2 angles + 1 side (AAS, ASA) | the other sides | **Law of sines** |
| 2 sides + angle opposite to one (SSA) | remaining (ambiguous!) | **Law of sines** |
| 2 sides + angle between them (SAS) | third side | **Law of cosines** |
| 3 sides (SSS) | any angle | **Law of cosines** inverted |

### Ambiguous case (SSA)

Given $a$, $b$, and $A$ (angle opposite to $a$): there may be 0, 1, or 2 triangles. Decision:
- If $a \geq b$: 1 triangle.
- If $a < b \sin A$: 0 triangles (geometrically impossible).
- If $b \sin A < a < b$: 2 triangles.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

In Lesson 11 we learned to "solve" a right triangle: given a side and an angle, find the other sides.

But what if the triangle **doesn't have a 90° angle**? Then Pythagoras doesn't work, and the right-triangle sine/cosine doesn't either.

The solution: **law of sines** and **law of cosines**. They work for **any** triangle.

**Law of sines**: the ratio between each side and the sine of the opposite angle is constant.
$$\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}$$

**Law of cosines**: $c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C$. It's Pythagoras "modified" by the cosine of the angle between sides $a$ and $b$.

In practice: you have 3 measurements (3 sides, or 2 sides and 1 angle, etc.) and want to find the rest. Apply the right law and do the math.

**Example**: you walk 5 km north and then turn 60° to the east and walk 3 more km. How far are you from the starting point?

By the law of cosines: $d^2 = 5^2 + 3^2 - 2 \cdot 5 \cdot 3 \cdot \cos(120°) = 25 + 9 + 15 = 49$. So $d = 7$ km.

(Note: the interior angle of the triangle is $180° - 60° = 120°$ due to the path deviation.)

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

The law of sines and cosines are fundamental in:

- **Surveying / Cadastre**: triangulation of points without direct access.
- **Navigation**: GPS does 3D triangulation by combining 4+ satellites — a multidimensional version of the law of cosines.
- **Astronomy**: stellar parallax uses the law of sines.
- **Robotics**: inverse kinematics of robotic arms with 2 segments solves via the law of cosines.

### Triangle area

$$\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} ab \sin C$$

Comes from "base × height / 2" with height $= a \sin C$. When $C = 90°$, recovers "leg × leg / 2".

### Heron's formula

Given 3 sides $a, b, c$, with semi-perimeter $s = (a+b+c)/2$:
$$\text{Area} = \sqrt{s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)}$$

Provable by combining the law of cosines + identity $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$.

</Porta>
