<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Structure of the solutions">

### Equations with sine

For $\sin x = a$ with $a \in [-1, 1]$:
$$x = \arcsin a + 2k\pi \quad \text{ou} \quad x = \pi - \arcsin a + 2k\pi, \quad k \in \mathbb{Z}$$

Where $\arcsin: [-1, 1] \to [-\pi/2, \pi/2]$ is the principal inverse.

### Equations with cosine

For $\cos x = a$ with $a \in [-1, 1]$:
$$x = \pm \arccos a + 2k\pi, \quad k \in \mathbb{Z}$$

Where $\arccos: [-1, 1] \to [0, \pi]$.

### Equations with tangent

For $\tan x = a$ with $a \in \mathbb{R}$:
$$x = \arctan a + k\pi, \quad k \in \mathbb{Z}$$

Where $\arctan: \mathbb{R} \to (-\pi/2, \pi/2)$.

### General recipe

To solve a trigonometric $f(x) = 0$:

1. **Reduce** to basic equations $\sin x = a$, $\cos x = a$, $\tan x = a$ via identities.
2. **List** all solutions in the interval $[0, 2\pi)$ (or $[-\pi, \pi]$).
3. **Generalize** by adding $2k\pi$ (or $k\pi$ for tangent).

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

Solving $\sin x = 1/2$ is not as simple as solving $x + 3 = 5$.

Why? Because sine repeats over and over. If $x = \pi/6$ is a solution, then $x = \pi/6 + 2\pi$ is too. And $x = \pi/6 + 4\pi$. And... infinitely many.

Moreover, $\sin(\pi - \pi/6) = \sin(5\pi/6) = 1/2$ as well! So in $[0, 2\pi)$ there are **two** solutions: $\pi/6$ and $5\pi/6$.

**Recipe:**
1. Find the solutions on the trigonometric circle, in $[0, 2\pi)$.
2. Add $2k\pi$ (all whole turns).

For tangent it is different: the period of $\tan$ is $\pi$ (not $2\pi$), because $\tan(x + \pi) = \tan x$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

In practice, **you do not solve trigonometry by hand** after high school. You use a calculator or solver. But the structure of the solutions is important because it controls the form of the answers in engineering problems.

In control, **phase coupling** between two signals is given by $\arctan$ of the imaginary/real ratio of the quotient of the transfer functions. In mechanics, an equilibrium angle is a root of a trigonometric equation.

### Numerical methods

For trigonometric equations that do not reduce to basic ones (e.g., $x = \cos x$), Newton-Raphson or bisection is used. **There is no closed-form formula.**

</Porta>
