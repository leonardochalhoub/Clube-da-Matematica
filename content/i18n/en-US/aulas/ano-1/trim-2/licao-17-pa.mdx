<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Definition and formulas">

<Definicao titulo="Arithmetic Progression (PA)">
Sequence $(a_n)$ such that $a_{n+1} - a_n = r$ (constant) for all $n$. $r$ is called the **common difference**.
</Definicao>

### General term

$$a_n = a_1 + (n-1) r$$

Proof by induction on $n$. Equivalent to $a_n = a_p + (n-p) r$ for any $p$.

### Sum of the first $n$ terms

$$S_n = \sum_{k=1}^n a_k = \frac{n(a_1 + a_n)}{2} = \frac{n(2a_1 + (n-1)r)}{2}$$

**Proof** (Gauss as a child, ~1789): write $S_n$ twice, once in increasing order and once in decreasing order:

$$\begin{aligned} S_n &= a_1 + a_2 + \ldots + a_n \\ S_n &= a_n + a_{n-1} + \ldots + a_1 \end{aligned}$$

Adding term by term: $2 S_n = n(a_1 + a_n)$, since each pair sums to $a_1 + a_n$. ∎

### Properties

- **Arithmetic mean**: three consecutive terms satisfy $a_n = (a_{n-1} + a_{n+1})/2$.
- **Increasing** if $r > 0$, **decreasing** if $r < 0$, **constant** if $r = 0$.

### Numerical example

PA with $a_1 = 5$, $r = 3$: terms $5, 8, 11, 14, 17, \ldots$

$a_{20} = 5 + 19 \cdot 3 = 62$. $S_{20} = 20 \cdot (5 + 62)/2 = 670$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

PA is a list where each number is the previous one **plus a fixed value**. The fixed value is called the **common difference**.

- $1, 4, 7, 10, 13, \ldots$ — PA with common difference 3.
- $20, 17, 14, 11, 8, \ldots$ — PA with common difference $-3$ (decreasing).
- $0, 5, 10, 15, 20, \ldots$ — PA with common difference 5.

**General term**: $a_n = a_1 + (n-1) r$. Like "what's the 50th term?": take $a_1$, add $49$ times the common difference.

**Sum of the PA**: $S_n = n(a_1 + a_n)/2$. Brilliant trick by **Gauss at age 8**: to sum $1+2+3+\ldots+100$, he paired them up ($1+100, 2+99, 3+98, \ldots$) — each pair gives 101, and there are 50 pairs. Total: 5,050. **Without adding everything one by one.**

**Where does it appear?** Shoe sizes 32, 33, 34, 35, ... (PA). Building floors. Barcodes. Calendar dates.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

PA is the simplest recurrence. It appears as a **base** in:

- **Riemann step sums**: integral approximation via finite sums — sums with index in PA.
- **Time discretization**: regular sampling $t_n = t_0 + n \Delta t$ is PA.
- **Numerical methods**: regular meshes are PAs in coordinates.
- **Classical cryptography**: Caesar ciphers shift letters in PA.

### Sum of squares, cubes, etc.

PA doesn't cover everything. For sums $\sum n^2$ or $\sum n^3$, distinct formulas (Lesson 19 or induction):

$$\sum_{k=1}^n k = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}, \quad \sum_{k=1}^n k^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}, \quad \sum_{k=1}^n k^3 = \left(\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2$$

</Porta>
