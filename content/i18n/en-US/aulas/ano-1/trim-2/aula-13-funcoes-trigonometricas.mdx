<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Definition and parameters">

<Definicao titulo="Trigonometric functions">
$\sin: \mathbb{R} \to [-1, 1]$, $\cos: \mathbb{R} \to [-1, 1]$ — defined by the trigonometric circle (Lesson 12). $\tan: \mathbb{R} \setminus \{(2k+1)\pi/2\} \to \mathbb{R}$.

Fundamental period: $\sin$ and $\cos$ have period $2\pi$; $\tan$ has period $\pi$.
</Definicao>

### Generalized sinusoidal function

For $y(t) = A \sin(\omega t + \varphi) + k$ with $A, \omega > 0$:

- **Amplitude** $A$: distance from the midline to the peaks. Range: $[k - A, k + A]$.
- **Angular frequency** $\omega$: speed of oscillation. Period $T = 2\pi/\omega$. Frequency $f = 1/T = \omega/(2\pi)$.
- **Initial phase** $\varphi$: horizontal shift. $y$ reaches its maximum when $\omega t + \varphi = \pi/2$, that is, $t = (\pi/2 - \varphi)/\omega$.
- **Vertical shift** $k$: middle of the graph.

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

A trigonometric function is a **periodic** function — it repeats every $2\pi$. The graph of $\sin x$ is a wave that rises to 1, falls to $-1$, and returns. Always with the same shape.

The general formula $y = A \sin(\omega t + \varphi) + k$ has 4 controls:
- $A$: **wave height** (amplitude).
- $\omega$: **oscillation speed** (how fast it rises and falls).
- $\varphi$: **horizontal shift** (where the wave starts).
- $k$: **vertical shift** (where the middle of the wave is).

Every real-world wave — sound, light, pendulum oscillation, simplified heartbeat, spring oscillation — is described by this formula.

The difference between $\sin$ and $\cos$ is just a phase shift: $\cos x = \sin(x + \pi/2)$.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

Trigonometric functions are the **soul of signal processing**. Every analysis of a linear time-invariant (LTI) system reduces to:

1. Decompose the input signal into sines/cosines (Fourier).
2. Apply the transfer function $H(j\omega)$ to each component.
3. Reconstruct the output.

### Alternative form: $A \sin + B \cos$

$$A \sin(\omega t) + B \cos(\omega t) = R \sin(\omega t + \varphi)$$

where $R = \sqrt{A^2 + B^2}$ and $\tan\varphi = B/A$. Useful in superposition of oscillations.

### Derivative

$(\sin x)' = \cos x$, $(\cos x)' = -\sin x$ — only valid when $x$ is in radians. Iteration: $\sin'''' = \sin$ — periodic at the fourth derivative.

### Engineering applications

- **AC (alternating current)**: $V(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t)$ with $\omega = 2\pi \cdot 60$ rad/s in Brazil.
- **Acoustics**: orchestral A note is $f = 440$ Hz, or $\omega = 880\pi$ rad/s.
- **Mechanical systems**: mass-spring has $x(t) = A \sin(\omega_n t + \varphi)$ with $\omega_n = \sqrt{k/m}$.
- **Tides**: combination of sinusoids with lunar and solar periods.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

Sinusoidal functions are **eigenvalues** of the time-translation operator: if $y(t) = \e^{j\omega t}$, then $y(t + T) = \e^{j\omega T} y(t)$. They belong to the "eigenspace" of the shift.

This property is **the reason** Fourier works: sinusoids decompose any signal because they are eigenfunctions of LTI systems.

### Identifying amplitude, frequency, phase in data

In practice, you receive a time series $\{y_i\}$ supposedly sinusoidal with noise. $A, \omega, \varphi$ are estimated via:

1. **FFT**: peak in the spectrum ⟹ $\omega$.
2. **Least squares** after linearization $A \cos\varphi$ and $A \sin\varphi$.
3. **Goertzel** if $\omega$ is known (DTMF, modems).

In DSP, **synchronous demodulation** = product by the cosine at the carrier frequency + low-pass filter. Every AM/FM/QAM transmission is this.

### Computational pitfalls

- **Aliasing**: sampling a wave with frequency $f > f_s/2$ produces a fictitious-frequency signal $|f - f_s|$. Nyquist-Shannon theorem: $f_s > 2 f_\text{max}$.
- **Cancellation in $\sin x$ for small $x$**: use truncated Taylor, $\sin x \approx x - x^3/6$.
- **Modular reduction of large $x$**: classic source of GPU bugs. Use Payne-Hanek.

</Porta>
