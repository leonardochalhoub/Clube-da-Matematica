<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Definition via the unit circle">

<Definicao titulo="Trigonometric functions on the unit circle">
Let $C$ be the unit circle centered at $O = (0, 0)$. For $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$ measured in radians from the positive x-axis, counterclockwise, the point $P(\theta) \in C$ is uniquely determined. We define:
$$\cos\theta = x_{P(\theta)}, \quad \sin\theta = y_{P(\theta)}$$
</Definicao>

### Pythagorean identity

Since $P(\theta)$ lies on the unit circle, $x^2 + y^2 = 1$, that is:

<Equation explicacao="The Pythagorean identity generalized to any real angle, not only acute. Holds for every θ ∈ ℝ.">
{`\\cos^2\\theta + \\sin^2\\theta = 1`}
</Equation>

### Periodicity

$\sin(\theta + 2\pi) = \sin\theta$ and $\cos(\theta + 2\pi) = \cos\theta$ for every $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$.

### Signs by quadrant

| Quadrant | $\theta$ | $\sin\theta$ | $\cos\theta$ | $\tan\theta$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | $(0, \pi/2)$ | + | + | + |
| II | $(\pi/2, \pi)$ | + | − | − |
| III | $(\pi, 3\pi/2)$ | − | − | + |
| IV | $(3\pi/2, 2\pi)$ | − | + | − |

### Special angles

| $\theta$ | $0$ | $\pi/6$ | $\pi/4$ | $\pi/3$ | $\pi/2$ | $\pi$ | $3\pi/2$ | $2\pi$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| $\sin$ | $0$ | $1/2$ | $\sqrt 2/2$ | $\sqrt 3/2$ | $1$ | $0$ | $-1$ | $0$ |
| $\cos$ | $1$ | $\sqrt 3/2$ | $\sqrt 2/2$ | $1/2$ | $0$ | $-1$ | $0$ | $1$ |

### Fundamental identities

$$\sin(-\theta) = -\sin\theta \quad (\sin \text{ is odd})$$
$$\cos(-\theta) = \cos\theta \quad (\cos \text{ is even})$$
$$\sin(\theta + \pi/2) = \cos\theta, \quad \cos(\theta + \pi/2) = -\sin\theta$$

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

In Lesson 11 we saw trigonometry on the **right triangle**. But the formula only works for acute angles (between 0 and 90 degrees).

What about larger angles? Like 120°, 270°, 360°, or even negative ones (rotate the other way)?

The solution is the **trigonometric circle**: a circle of radius 1 centered at the origin. For each angle $\theta$ (measured from the x-axis), you take the corresponding point on the circle. The coordinates of that point are exactly $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$. **Extended definition**.

**Radians**: mathematicians prefer measuring angles in radians instead of degrees. Why? Because it is "natural": a full turn of the circle is $2\pi$ radians, which is exactly the perimeter of the unit circle. In radians, calculus stays clean: $(\sin x)' = \cos x$. In degrees, it would be $(\sin x)' = (\pi/180)\cos x$ — ugly.

Conversion: $180° = \pi$ rad. So $90° = \pi/2$, $60° = \pi/3$, $45° = \pi/4$, $30° = \pi/6$. Memorize them.

**Periodicity**: the circle "repeats" every turn. $\sin(\theta + 2\pi) = \sin\theta$. That is why $\sin x$ traces that endless wave.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

The unit circle is the gateway to Fourier analysis — every periodic signal is a linear combination of sines and cosines. In DSP, you live inside this structure.

### Sum and difference identities

$$\sin(\alpha \pm \beta) = \sin\alpha\cos\beta \pm \cos\alpha\sin\beta$$
$$\cos(\alpha \pm \beta) = \cos\alpha\cos\beta \mp \sin\alpha\sin\beta$$

Proof: rotating the circle by $\alpha + \beta$ is the same as rotating first by $\alpha$ and then by $\beta$ (composition of rotations). In linear algebra this becomes a product of rotation matrices.

### Double-angle formulas

$$\sin 2\theta = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta, \quad \cos 2\theta = \cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta$$

### Exponential form (Euler)

$$\e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$$

The famous Euler formula — derived from the Taylor series of $\e^x$, $\sin x$, $\cos x$. For $\theta = \pi$:

$$\e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$$

The "most beautiful equation in mathematics" — connects $e, i, \pi, 1, 0$ in a single identity. Rigorously proved in Trim 9 (series) or in Complex Analysis.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

The unit circle is the fundamental object of:

- **Fourier analysis**: $\hat f(\xi) = \int f(t) \e^{-2\pi i \xi t} dt$ — every Fourier integral lives on $\mathbb{S}^1$.
- **Quantum mechanics**: states are rays in $\mathbb{C}^n$, global phases are $\e^{i\theta}$ on the circle.
- **Signals and systems**: frequency response $H(j\omega)$ evaluated on $z = \e^{j\omega T}$ on the circle of radius 1.
- **Projective geometry**: $S^1 \cong \mathbb{R}P^1$ doubly covers the space of lines through the origin.

### Computationally

`atan2(y, x)` returns $\theta$ in the correct quadrant, $\theta \in (-\pi, \pi]$. Always prefer `atan2` to `atan(y/x)` (which loses quadrant info).

### Trade-off radians vs degrees in code

Math libraries (NumPy, scipy, MATLAB) use **radians** by default. UI/UX (CSS rotations, OpenGL viewport) use degrees by human convention. **Conversion is a classic source of bugs in games and visualizations**.

</Porta>
