<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Intuitive concept">

### The central question

> Given a sequence $(a_n)$, **toward what value (if any) do the terms approach as $n \to \infty$?**

When that value exists, we say the sequence **converges** and write $\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L$.

### Intuitive definition

$\lim a_n = L$ means: **the terms $a_n$ become arbitrarily close to $L$ when $n$ is sufficiently large**.

"Arbitrarily" and "sufficiently" are precisely what gets formalized with $\eps$ and $N$ in Lesson 41:
$$\forall \eps > 0, \exists N : n \geq N \Rightarrow |a_n - L| < \eps$$

### Notable limits

| Sequence | Limit | Intuitive justification |
|---|---|---|
| $1/n$ | $0$ | terms get smaller and smaller |
| $1/n^k$ ($k > 0$) | $0$ | same, faster |
| $q^n$ ($|q| < 1$) | $0$ | decreasing geometric progression |
| $q^n$ ($|q| > 1$) | does not exist (diverges) | geometric progression explodes |
| $(1 + 1/n)^n$ | $\e \approx 2{,}71828$ | Euler's number |
| $\sqrt[n]{n}$ | $1$ | (log trick) |
| $n^k / a^n$ ($a > 1$) | $0$ | exponential grows faster than polynomial |

### Operations with limits

If $\lim a_n = A$ and $\lim b_n = B$ (both finite):

- $\lim(a_n + b_n) = A + B$
- $\lim(a_n \cdot b_n) = A \cdot B$
- $\lim(a_n / b_n) = A/B$ (if $B \neq 0$)
- $\lim c \cdot a_n = c A$ ($c$ constant)

### Sequences that do NOT converge

- **Diverge to $\pm \infty$**: $a_n = n$, $a_n = 2^n$.
- **Oscillate**: $a_n = (-1)^n$ — alternates between 1 and $-1$, tends to nothing.

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old">

The sequence $1/n$ has terms $1, 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/5, \ldots, 1/100, \ldots, 1/1{,}000{,}000, \ldots$

What value does it **approach**? Intuitive answer: **zero**. The terms get smaller and smaller, never reach exactly zero, but get as close as you want.

This idea of "approaching" is what we call a **limit**:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} = 0$$

Read: "the limit, as $n$ tends to infinity, of $1/n$, is zero".

**Other examples:**
- $\lim (1/2)^n = 0$ — decreasing geometric progression.
- $\lim n = \infty$ — diverges.
- $\lim (-1)^n$ — does not exist (oscillates $1, -1, 1, -1, \ldots$).
- $\lim (1 + 1/n)^n = e$ — the famous Euler's number.

**Why does it matter?** Because the limit is the foundation of **calculus**. Every derivative is a limit. Every integral is a limit. You're seeing here the concept that will dominate mathematics for the next 4 years of the program.

This lesson is **deliberately** intuitive. The rigorous formalization ($\eps$, $\delta$, $N$) comes in Trim 5, in Lesson 41. For now: trust "approaches".

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

The limit is the **key concept** of analysis. Sequences either converge or do not converge; when they converge, they have a unique limit value (uniqueness of limit — basic theorem).

### Useful theorems (preview)

- **Squeeze theorem**: if $a_n \leq b_n \leq c_n$ and $\lim a_n = \lim c_n = L$, then $\lim b_n = L$.
- **Monotone bounded converges**: every monotone and bounded sequence has a limit (Bolzano-Weierstrass).
- **Cauchy ⇒ converges**: in $\mathbb{R}$, a Cauchy sequence converges.

### Asymptotic notation

In computer science:
- $a_n = O(b_n)$: $|a_n/b_n|$ bounded.
- $a_n = o(b_n)$: $a_n/b_n \to 0$.
- $a_n \sim b_n$: $a_n/b_n \to 1$.

### The number $e$

$$e = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{1}{n!} \approx 2{,}71828\ldots$$

Appears naturally in continuous interest, decay, and in any 1st-order ODE $\dot y = ky$.

### Caution: not every sequence has a limit

$\lim \sin n$ does not exist (oscillates irregularly because $n$ vs periods of $2\pi$). But $\lim \sin(n)/n = 0$ by the squeeze theorem.
