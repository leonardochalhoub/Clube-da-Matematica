<Porta nivel="formal" titulo="Rigorous definition">

<Definicao titulo="Trigonometric ratios in a right triangle">

Let $\triangle ABC$ be right-angled at $C$, with hypotenuse $\overline{AB}$ of length $c$, legs $\overline{BC}$ (opposite to $A$) of length $a$ and $\overline{CA}$ (adjacent to $A$) of length $b$. For the angle $\hat A = \theta \in (0°, 90°)$:

$$\sin\theta = \frac{a}{c}, \quad \cos\theta = \frac{b}{c}, \quad \tan\theta = \frac{a}{b}$$

</Definicao>

### Why do the ratios depend only on the angle?

**Thales' theorem** (similarity): triangles with the same angles are proportional. So the ratio $a/c$ is the same in any right triangle with angle $\theta$ — independent of size.

### Fundamental identity

<Equation
  numero="1"
  audioTexto="Equation one. Sine squared of theta plus cosine squared of theta equals one. It is the fundamental identity of trigonometry, equivalent to the Pythagorean theorem divided by the square of the hypotenuse."
  explicacao="Pythagorean identity. Comes directly from a² + b² = c² (Pythagoras), dividing both sides by c²: (a/c)² + (b/c)² = 1, that is, sin²θ + cos²θ = 1.">
{`\\sin^2\\theta + \\cos^2\\theta = 1`}
</Equation>

### Notable values

| $\theta$ | $\sin\theta$ | $\cos\theta$ | $\tan\theta$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| $30°$ | $1/2$ | $\sqrt{3}/2$ | $\sqrt{3}/3$ |
| $45°$ | $\sqrt{2}/2$ | $\sqrt{2}/2$ | $1$ |
| $60°$ | $\sqrt{3}/2$ | $1/2$ | $\sqrt{3}$ |

These values **must be memorized**. They come from $30$-$60$-$90$ and $45$-$45$-$90$ (isosceles) triangles.

### Proof — 45-45-90 triangle

An isosceles right triangle has equal legs ($a = b$). By Pythagoras: $c = a\sqrt{2}$. So:

$$\sin 45° = \frac{a}{a\sqrt{2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt 2} = \frac{\sqrt 2}{2}, \quad \cos 45° = \frac{\sqrt 2}{2}, \quad \tan 45° = 1$$

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="5" titulo="For a 5-year-old">

Take a ladder and lean it against a wall.

If the ladder is **very upright** (almost flat against the wall), the top is **very high**. If the ladder is **very tilted**, the top is **very low**.

The ladder's slant — how "upright" it is — is the **angle**. And the height it reaches depends **only on the angle**, not on the length of the ladder.

Trigonometry is the way to calculate that height **without measuring** with a ruler. You just need to know the angle and the length of the ladder — the formulas give the height.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="10" titulo="For a 10-year-old">

Trigonometry is the way to **calculate distances** without having to measure everything.

**Example**: you stand in front of a building. You measure the distance from yourself to the base of the building: 50 meters. You measure the angle between the ground and the top of the building: 60 degrees.

How tall is the building?

The formula is: $\text{height} = 50 \cdot \tan(60°) = 50 \cdot 1{.}73 = 86{.}5$ meters.

There you go, you found the building's height without climbing it.

This trick is used in:

- **Surveying**: measuring the size of mountains, the depth of valleys.
- **Astronomy**: measuring the distance of planets and stars (without going there).
- **Navigation**: GPS triangulates with 4 satellites all the time to know where you are.

Trigonometry was invented in **Babylon 4,000 years ago** to predict eclipses, and in **Greece 2,500 years ago** (Hipparchus, Ptolemy) for astronomy. Today it is inside your cell phone.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="15" titulo="For a 15-year-old teen">

Trigonometry starts simple: ratio between sides of a right triangle.

The magic is: **the ratio depends ONLY on the angle, not on the size**. If you draw any right triangle with one of the angles being 30°, **always** $\sin 30° = 1/2$. Always. Whether the triangle is tiny or huge.

**Why?** Because triangles with the same angles are **similar** — proportional. The ratios between sides are preserved.

**Memorization trick**: SOH-CAH-TOA.
- **S**in = **O**pposite / **H**ypotenuse
- **C**os = **A**djacent / **H**ypotenuse
- **T**an = **O**pposite / **A**djacent

And the notable values (memorize them!):

| $\theta$ | sin | cos | tan |
|---|---|---|---|
| $30°$ | $1/2$ | $\sqrt 3 /2$ | $\sqrt 3 /3$ |
| $45°$ | $\sqrt 2 /2$ | $\sqrt 2 /2$ | $1$ |
| $60°$ | $\sqrt 3 /2$ | $1/2$ | $\sqrt 3$ |

Where does this immediately appear? **Vector decomposition** in physics: force, velocity, acceleration — any tilted vector is broken into a horizontal component ($\cos\theta$) and vertical component ($\sin\theta$).

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="25" titulo="For an engineering student">

Right-triangle trigonometry is the **special case** of general trigonometry (which comes in Lesson 12 with the trigonometric circle). But it is the **most used** case in basic engineering.

### Generalization of the ratios: secant, cosecant, cotangent

$$\sec\theta = \frac{1}{\cos\theta}, \quad \csc\theta = \frac{1}{\sin\theta}, \quad \cot\theta = \frac{1}{\tan\theta}$$

They appear in integration (trig substitutions), in mechanics (friction), and in optics formulas.

### Auxiliary Pythagorean identities

Dividing $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$ by $\cos^2\theta$: $\tan^2\theta + 1 = \sec^2\theta$. By $\sin^2\theta$: $1 + \cot^2\theta = \csc^2\theta$.

### Engineering applications

- **Force resolution**: force $F$ on a ramp of slope $\theta$ — parallel component is $F \sin\theta$, normal is $F \cos\theta$.
- **Simple harmonic analysis**: $A \sin(\omega t + \phi)$ — any sinusoidal oscillation.
- **Surveying and georeferencing**: triangulation to locate points.
- **Cable tension**: cable at $\theta$ with horizontal supporting $W$ — tension is $W/\sin\theta$.

### Numerical engineering example

A factory loading ramp has a slope of $15°$ and length $8$ m. Vertical height of the ramp:

$$h = 8 \sin 15° \approx 8 \cdot 0{.}259 \approx 2{.}07 \text{ m}$$

Horizontal length: $8 \cos 15° \approx 7{.}73$ m.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="40" titulo="For a senior professional">

Trigonometry was the **first applied mathematics** of humanity — Babylonians computed tables 4,000 years ago. Today:

### Computational trigonometry

`sin(x)` in IEEE 754: maximum error $\approx 0{.}5$ ULP. Real implementation uses:
1. Argument reduction: $x \mod 2\pi$ via the Payne-Hanek algorithm (watch out for catastrophic cancellation for large $x$).
2. Minimax polynomial (Remez) on $[-\pi/4, \pi/4]$.
3. Reconstruction by symmetry.

On GPU/SIMD, `sincos.approx` instructions return both with latency around 10 cycles. In DL, they are used in positional embeddings (Transformer, [Vaswani 2017](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762)).

### Fourier — where trigonometry explodes

**Every smooth periodic function** decomposes into a sum of sines and cosines:

$$f(t) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left[a_n \cos(n\omega t) + b_n \sin(n\omega t)\right]$$

This is the basis of signal processing (DSP), audio compression (MP3, AAC), image compression (JPEG, DCT), mechanical vibration analysis, quantum mechanics.

**FFT** (Fast Fourier Transform, Cooley-Tukey 1965) computes the discrete version in $O(N \log N)$. It is inside **all** digital processing.

### Coordinates in control and robotics

Robots and drones depend on transformations between frames (body, world, sensor). Each transformation is a rotation — a composition of sines/cosines via rotation matrices $R(\theta) = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$.

In 3D, three orthogonal rotations (Euler angles) or quaternions — still depend on sine/cosine.

### Caution: degrees vs radians

`Math.sin(90)` in JS returns $\sin(90 \text{ rad})$, not $\sin(90°)$. **Always check the unit**. In scientific programming, it is almost always radians. Conversion: $1 \text{ rad} = 180°/\pi \approx 57{.}296°$. **Differential calculus only holds in radians**: $(\sin x)' = \cos x$ ⟺ $x$ in rad.

</Porta>

<Porta nivel="pratica" titulo="Practice — 3 real scenarios">

### Scenario 1: Height of a building in São Paulo

You are 80 meters from the base of the **Edifício Itália** (currently the tallest building in downtown SP). You measure the angle of elevation to the top: $58°$. Height:

$$h = 80 \cdot \tan 58° \approx 80 \cdot 1{.}600 \approx 128 \text{ m}$$

(The Edifício Itália is ~165 m tall. The difference comes from the fact that you are not at the building's ground level — you are on a block in front of it.)

### Scenario 2: Slope of a street (Lapa, RJ)

A street is 200 meters long with an elevation difference of 18 meters. Slope:

$$\theta = \arctan(18/200) = \arctan(0{.}09) \approx 5{.}14°$$

On roads, it is usually expressed as a **percentage**: $9\%$. Legal urban limit in Brazil: 12% (the Bento Lisboa ramp in Petrópolis is at 17% — ramps steeper than this are rare).

### Scenario 3: Antenna positioning (telecom)

A 5G antenna is installed on a building 60 m tall. To cover a point on the ground 250 m away horizontally, what is the tilt angle (downtilt) of the main beam?

$$\theta = \arctan(60/250) = \arctan(0{.}24) \approx 13{.}5°$$

In practice, urban 5G antennas have downtilt of $5°$ to $15°$. Above that, the beam aims too close and loses range; below that, it over-illuminates the horizon and creates interference.

</Porta>
